# Framework Comparison: Ansoff Matrix vs Related Strategy Frameworks

## When to Use Ansoff vs Alternatives

The Ansoff Matrix answers one specific question: **"How should we grow?"** When the user's question is different, a different framework applies.

| User Question | Correct Framework | Why Not Ansoff |
|---|---|---|
| "How should we grow?" | **Ansoff Matrix** | Direct match |
| "Which products should we invest in or kill?" | **BCG Matrix** | Portfolio resource allocation, not growth direction |
| "How do we beat competitors?" | **Porter's Five Forces** | Competitive dynamics, not growth path |
| "What are our strengths / where are we vulnerable?" | **SWOT** | Internal + external audit, not growth options |
| "How do we position against competitors?" | **Porter's Generic Strategies** | Cost leadership vs differentiation, not market/product axes |
| "Is this market worth entering?" | **TAM/SAM/SOM analysis** | Market sizing, not strategic choice |
| "How do we create a new market category?" | **Blue Ocean Strategy** | Value innovation, not growth within existing markets |

## Ansoff vs BCG Matrix

These two are the most commonly confused because both use a 2×2 grid.

### Different Axes

**Ansoff** axes:
- X: Product (existing / new)
- Y: Market (existing / new)

**BCG** axes:
- X: Relative market share (high / low)
- Y: Market growth rate (high / low)

### Different Questions

| | Ansoff | BCG |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit of analysis** | Strategic initiative or growth move | Business unit or product line |
| **Primary output** | Which direction to grow | How to allocate cash across portfolio |
| **Time orientation** | Forward-looking (what to pursue) | Current snapshot (what to fund, harvest, or kill) |
| **Risk framing** | Diagonal risk gradient (penetration → diversification) | Cash flow quadrants (Stars need investment; Cash Cows generate it) |

### Combined Use (Correct Sequencing)

BCG first, Ansoff second:

1. **BCG**: Identify which business units generate cash (Cash Cows) and which need investment (Stars, Question Marks)
2. **Ansoff**: For each unit where growth is desired, identify *how* to grow — penetrate, develop market, develop product, or diversify

**Example**: A consumer electronics company has three product lines:
- Smartphones: Star (high share, high growth) → BCG says invest
- Ansoff for Smartphones: Market Development (new geographies) preferred over Diversification
- Accessories: Cash Cow → BCG says harvest cash
- Ansoff for Accessories: Market Penetration only; do not divert to new product development

**Mistake to avoid**: Using Ansoff to decide *whether* to invest in a product line. That's BCG's job. Ansoff only decides *how* to grow a unit you've already decided to invest in.

## Ansoff vs Porter's Generic Strategies

Porter's Generic Strategies (Cost Leadership, Differentiation, Focus) describe *how to compete*, not *where to grow*.

### Compatibility

These frameworks are complementary, not interchangeable. They operate at different decision layers:

```
Layer 1 (Ansoff): Where to grow? → Market Development: expand to Southeast Asia
Layer 2 (Porter): How to compete there? → Differentiation: premium brand positioning
```

### Decision Table: Which Framework First?

| Situation | Start With |
|---|---|
| Company has no growth strategy at all | Ansoff |
| Company has chosen a growth direction, now needs competitive approach | Porter's Generic Strategies |
| Company is in an industry where cost structure determines survival | Porter's Five Forces + Generic Strategies |
| Company is evaluating risk of different growth paths | Ansoff |

### Conflict to Watch For

If a company pursues Ansoff **Diversification** into a new market, they enter without an established competitive advantage. Porter's framework would flag this: they have neither cost leadership nor differentiation position in the new market. This is one reason why diversification carries the highest Ansoff risk — the company must build competitive advantage from scratch.

## Ansoff vs SWOT

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is a diagnostic, not a prescriptive framework. Ansoff is prescriptive.

### Correct Workflow

```
SWOT → Ansoff (not the reverse)

1. SWOT: Inventory internal capabilities and external landscape
2. Ansoff: Use SWOT outputs to evaluate which growth quadrant is feasible

SWOT Strengths → informs capability assessment in Ansoff Step 3
SWOT Opportunities → identifies which Ansoff quadrants are viable
SWOT Weaknesses → flags capability gaps for each Ansoff option
SWOT Threats → informs risk assessment in Ansoff Step 3
```

### Mapping SWOT Outputs to Ansoff Quadrants

| SWOT Finding | Ansoff Implication |
|---|---|
| Strength: Strong brand loyalty | Market Penetration and Product Development are lower-risk — existing customers will follow |
| Strength: Manufacturing scale | Market Development preferred — existing product scales to new markets cheaply |
| Weakness: No R&D capability | Product Development is high-risk regardless of Ansoff quadrant classification |
| Opportunity: Underserved segment in adjacent geography | Market Development signal |
| Opportunity: Customers requesting adjacent product | Product Development signal |
| Threat: Commoditization of core product | Urgency on Product Development or Diversification |

### Common Error

Running SWOT *after* choosing an Ansoff strategy, and using SWOT only to justify the choice already made. SWOT is an input to Ansoff, not validation of it.

## Ansoff vs Blue Ocean Strategy

Blue Ocean focuses on creating uncontested market space by redefining value. This is conceptually distinct from Ansoff's growth paths.

### Mapping the Relationship

Blue Ocean strategies often appear to be Ansoff **Diversification** from the outside, but they are structurally different:

| | Ansoff Diversification | Blue Ocean |
|---|---|---|
| **Market** | Enters a defined existing market | Creates a new market category |
| **Competition** | Faces existing competitors | Eliminates competition by making it irrelevant |
| **Risk source** | Capability gap and competitive intensity | Uncertain demand; no market validation exists |
| **Example** | Starbucks enters packaged grocery coffee | Cirque du Soleil creates theatrical circus (no direct competitors) |

### When Blue Ocean Applies Instead of Ansoff

Use Blue Ocean when:
- User is asking how to escape commoditization in existing markets
- User is considering creating an entirely new product category
- Growth question is about value innovation, not market/product expansion

Use Ansoff when:
- User is growing within recognizable market definitions
- Risk and sequencing of expansion is the core concern
- A portfolio of growth options needs to be evaluated systematically

## Quick-Select Decision Guide

```
START: What is the user's primary question?
│
├─ "How should we grow?" → ANSOFF
│
├─ "Which business units / products to fund or cut?" → BCG MATRIX
│
├─ "How do we compete against rivals?" → PORTER'S FIVE FORCES
│
├─ "What are our strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats?"
│   └─ Then use findings as Ansoff inputs → SWOT FIRST, THEN ANSOFF
│
├─ "Should we be cost leader or differentiator?" → PORTER'S GENERIC STRATEGIES
│
├─ "How large is this market opportunity?" → TAM/SAM/SOM
│
└─ "How do we escape commoditization / create a new category?" → BLUE OCEAN
```

## Side-by-Side Summary

| Dimension | Ansoff | BCG | Porter's Five Forces | Porter's Generic | SWOT | Blue Ocean |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary question** | How to grow? | What to fund? | How attractive is the industry? | How to compete? | Where are we strong/weak? | How to create new market space? |
| **Output** | Growth strategy options | Portfolio investment priorities | Industry attractiveness score | Competitive positioning | Capability / risk inventory | Value innovation strategy |
| **Risk framing** | Diagonal gradient | Cash flow quadrants | Structural threats | Competitive trade-offs | Gap identification | Demand uncertainty |
| **Best used** | Growth planning | Multi-product companies | Market entry evaluation | Competitive strategy | Situational audit | Escaping commoditization |
| **Pairs well with** | SWOT (inputs), BCG (sequencing) | Ansoff (growth direction) | Porter's Generic Strategies | Porter's Five Forces | Ansoff (downstream) | Jobs-to-be-Done |
| **Does NOT answer** | Competitive positioning | Growth direction | How to grow | Where to grow | What strategy to pursue | Risk sequencing |
