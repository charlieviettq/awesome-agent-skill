# Example: Taiwanese AI Labeling SaaS Targeting Global Enterprise Clients

## Scenario

**User's question:**
> We founded DataTag.ai in Taipei in early 2024. We build AI-assisted data annotation tools specialized for medical imaging (DICOM files, radiology workflows). We have 3 enterprise pilots in Taiwan and one inbound inquiry from a German hospital network. Our team of 8 has 2 ex-Philips engineers and a founder who spent 6 years at Google Brain in the US. We've raised NT$15M seed. Should we go international now or wait until we dominate Taiwan first? If we go international, where and how?

---

## Analysis

### Step 1: Assess Born Global Eligibility

| Factor | Required | DataTag.ai Assessment |
|--------|----------|-----------------------|
| Knowledge intensity | High — proprietary tech, IP, or expertise | **YES** — DICOM-native annotation engine; 2 ex-Philips engineers hold domain expertise rare outside medtech hubs |
| Founder international experience | Prior work/study abroad, global network | **YES** — Founder has 6 years at Google Brain (Mountain View); English-fluent, existing ML research network |
| Niche market orientation | Global niche too small for MNCs | **YES** — Medical imaging annotation is too specialized and regulated for generic labeling platforms (Scale AI, Labelbox) to serve well |
| Digital/scalable delivery | International delivery at low marginal cost | **YES** — SaaS, DICOM viewer runs in browser; no physical installation needed |
| Home market limitation | Domestic market too small to sustain | **YES** — Taiwan has ~23 hospital systems that could adopt enterprise annotation tools; ARR ceiling ~US$2–3M domestically |

**Score: 5/5 factors present.**

**Verdict: Born Global path is strongly justified.**

Taiwan's domestic ceiling is real. The inbound from Germany confirms that medical imaging workflows are standardized enough internationally (DICOM is a global standard) that product-market fit transfers across borders. Waiting to "dominate Taiwan first" would burn 18–24 months in a market too small to provide meaningful scale, while faster-moving competitors (EU-based, US-based) mature.

---

### Step 2: Map Key Networks and Relationships

| Network Type | Key Relationships | What They Provide |
|---|---|---|
| **Founder personal** | Google Brain alumni in US (Stanford, CMU, UCSF labs) | Warm intros to academic medical centers doing AI research |
| **Industry** | Ex-Philips engineers have contacts at Philips Healthcare, Siemens Healthineers partner ecosystem | Distribution channel access; technical credibility with European procurement teams |
| **Institutional** | Taiwan's ITRI MedTech division; MOEA export vouchers for SaaS | Subsidized trade show presence (RSNA, ECR); reference letters for EU MDR pre-certification process |
| **Customer referral** | Inbound German hospital network (likely found via DICOM open-source forums) | First EU anchor customer; social proof for EU sales cycle |

**Critical gap**: No existing regulatory affairs network in the EU. EU MDR / CE mark pathway for AI-assisted medical devices is required before German hospital can go live. This is a 6–9 month blocker if not started immediately.

---

### Step 3: Select Target Markets (Prioritized)

**Market 1 — Germany (immediate)**
- **Rationale**: Inbound inquiry eliminates cold outreach cost. Germany is the lead market for medical AI in Europe: most demanding hospital procurement standards, largest number of AI-in-medicine pilots (≈ 340 active in 2024). Winning here signals quality to all of DACH.
- **Entry mode**: Strategic partnership with a local DICOM integration consultancy (e.g., existing Philips SI partner) to handle on-site compliance, while DataTag.ai provides cloud platform.
- **Blocker**: CE mark / EU MDR conformity for AI-assisted annotation must begin now. Engage a notified body (TÜV SÜD or BSI) in Q2 2024.
- **Timeline**: Pilot contract signed Q3 2024 → CE mark filed Q4 2024 → full commercial Q2 2025.

**Market 2 — United States (12-month pipeline)**
- **Rationale**: Founder's Google Brain network gives access to UCSF, Stanford, MGH — all running radiology AI programs. US FDA 510(k) for SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) is complex but well-documented; academic medical centers can adopt pre-clearance under research exemptions.
- **Entry mode**: Academic research partnerships first (no regulatory barrier for research use); convert to commercial with FDA clearance.
- **Timeline**: 2 academic pilots by Q1 2025 → 510(k) submission Q3 2025.

**Market 3 — Japan (hold for now)**
- **Rationale**: Large medical AI market, but PMDA regulatory pathway requires Japanese language support and local entity. Philips network has Japan presence, but resource cost is high.
- **Decision**: Revisit after Germany commercial launch. Do not enter simultaneously with Germany + US.

---

### Step 4: Resource-Light Entry Mode Design

DataTag.ai has NT$15M (≈ US$470K) seed. Wholly-owned EU subsidiary is out.

| Mode | Market | Rationale |
|------|--------|-----------|
| SaaS direct + local SI partner | Germany | Partner handles procurement compliance and DICOM infrastructure integration; DataTag retains platform IP and cloud billing |
| Academic research license | US | Zero-cost entry; builds publications and references; de-risks 510(k) with real clinical data |
| Licensing (future) | Japan | Only viable mode at current resource level if Japan opportunity matures |

**Avoid**: Trade show booths without prior qualified leads (burn rate); hiring a full-time EU country manager before €500K ARR in Europe.
