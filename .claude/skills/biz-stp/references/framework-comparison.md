# STP vs. Adjacent Marketing Frameworks: When to Use Which

This document helps you decide between STP and other commonly confused marketing frameworks. Each comparison follows the same structure: what the other framework does, where it overlaps with STP, the decision rule, and a worked example showing the hand-off point.

---

## Quick Decision Table

| User says... | Framework | Why not STP |
|---|---|---|
| "Who are our competitors and how strong are they?" | Porter's Five Forces | Industry-level analysis, not customer segmentation |
| "Should we enter a new market or launch a new product?" | Ansoff Matrix | Growth path decision precedes STP |
| "Where are we strong / weak?" | SWOT | Internal audit, inputs INTO STP, not a substitute |
| "Who is our customer and how do we reach them?" | **STP** | Direct match |
| "How should we price this?" | Pricing Strategy frameworks | STP informs pricing; doesn't replace it |
| "What do customers experience at each touchpoint?" | Customer Journey Map | Post-STP execution layer |
| "How do we grow this segment?" | Ansoff + STP combined | Use Ansoff first, then STP within chosen quadrant |

## STP vs. Porter's Five Forces

### What Porter's Five Forces Does

Analyzes the structural attractiveness of an **industry** using five forces: competitive rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, buyer power, supplier power. Output: "Is this industry worth competing in?"

### Overlap

Both frameworks involve competitive analysis. STP's Targeting step evaluates "Competition" within each segment. Porter's Five Forces evaluates competitive intensity at the industry level.

### The Difference

| Dimension | Porter's Five Forces | STP |
|---|---|---|
| Unit of analysis | Industry | Customer segment |
| Question answered | "Is this industry attractive?" | "Which customers should we serve and how?" |
| Output | Go/no-go on industry entry | Target segment + positioning statement |
| Competitor analysis | Structural (5 forces) | Relative positioning (perceptual map) |

### Decision Rule

**Use Porter's Five Forces BEFORE STP** when:
- You're evaluating whether to enter an industry at all
- You need to understand overall competitive dynamics before committing to segmentation

**Use STP after Porter's** when:
- The industry passes the Five Forces test (worth competing in)
- You now need to find the best beachhead segment

### Worked Hand-Off

> Company X is considering launching a meal-kit delivery service in Taiwan.

1. **Porter's Five Forces first**: Competitive rivalry (high — iChef, Pinkoi Food, homecooking), Buyer power (high — low switching cost), Threat of substitutes (high — convenience stores, UberEats). Verdict: tough industry, but not impossible.

2. **STP next**: Given the industry is tough, don't attack the whole market. Segment by behavioral base (cooking frequency + health consciousness). Target "Health-Conscious Meal Planners" — least price-sensitive, highest retention. Position as "the only meal kit designed around Taiwan's weekly market rhythm."

Porter's told you WHERE the industry pressure is. STP told you HOW to avoid it.

## STP vs. Ansoff Matrix

### What Ansoff Matrix Does

Ansoff maps four growth strategies on a 2×2 grid:

|  | **Existing Product** | **New Product** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Market** | Market Penetration | Product Development |
| **New Market** | Market Development | Diversification |

Output: Which quadrant to pursue for growth.

### Overlap

"New Market" in Ansoff implies new segmentation. "Market Development" and "Diversification" both require STP to execute.

### The Difference

Ansoff answers "what should our growth strategy be?" STP answers "who specifically within that growth strategy, and how do we win?"

Ansoff is a **strategic direction setter**. STP is an **execution tool**.

### Decision Rule

- If the user is asking which direction to grow → Ansoff first
- Once Ansoff quadrant is chosen → STP to find the right segment within that quadrant

### Worked Hand-Off

> A Taiwanese coffee chain (existing product: drip coffee) wants to grow.

**Ansoff analysis:**
- Market Penetration: get current customers to buy more → limited upside, saturated
- Market Development: enter new geographic or demographic markets → promising (younger demographic currently under-served)
- Product Development: launch new beverages for existing customers → moderate
- Diversification: new product + new market → too risky at current scale

**Decision**: Market Development quadrant.

**STP now executes the Ansoff decision:**
- Segmentation: segment the under-35 demographic by psychographic + behavioral (coffee as identity vs. coffee as function)
- Targeting: "Coffee-as-Identity" segment — smaller but high LTV, trend-driven, willing to pay premium
- Positioning: "For Gen Z who treat coffee as self-expression, [Brand] is the specialty coffee chain that lets you build your identity drink — because every cup is customizable to 200+ combinations."

Without STP, "target younger demographics" stays vague. STP turns the Ansoff direction into an executable plan.

## STP vs. SWOT

### What SWOT Does

Audits internal Strengths and Weaknesses, plus external Opportunities and Threats. Output: where the company is strong and what external conditions are favorable.

### Overlap

SWOT's "Opportunities" and "Threats" often map to market conditions that inform STP Targeting. A SWOT Strength (e.g., "strong distribution in northern Taiwan") directly informs which segments are Accessible.

### The Difference

SWOT is **retrospective and internal**. STP is **prospective and external (customer-facing)**.

| Dimension | SWOT | STP |
|---|---|---|
| Focus | Company capabilities & environment | Customer segments & positioning |
| Direction | Inward → outward | Outward → inward |
| Output | Audit of current position | Plan for future market position |
| Actionability | Low (analysis only) | High (produces strategy) |

### Common Mistake

Running SWOT instead of STP when asked "who should we target?" SWOT tells you what you're good at; it doesn't tell you which customer segment will respond to what you're good at.

### Correct Sequence

**SWOT → STP**, not SWOT instead of STP.

Use SWOT to identify:
- Strengths → informs Targeting "Fit" criterion and positioning "Reason to Believe"
- Weaknesses → eliminates segments requiring capabilities you lack
- Opportunities → confirms which segments are growing
- Threats → surfaces competitive intensity in specific segments

### Worked Integration

> Athletic wear brand assessing expansion into Taiwan's yoga market.

**SWOT outputs (abbreviated):**
- Strength: premium manufacturing, established brand equity in running gear
- Weakness: no existing relationships with yoga studios or instructors
- Opportunity: yoga participation up 40% post-COVID in Taiwan
- Threat: lululemon entrenched in premium yoga segment

**SWOT → STP hand-off:**
- The Threat (lululemon) rules out targeting the premium yoga enthusiast segment directly (low Fit, high Competition)
- The Strength (premium manufacturing + running brand) fits the "Athletic Cross-Trainer" segment — people who run AND do yoga
- Positioning leverages the Strength: "For serious athletes who cross-train, [Brand] is the performance wear that moves from track to mat — because it's engineered for high-output movement, not just stretch."

SWOT generated the inputs. STP structured them into a targeting decision and positioning statement.

## STP vs. Customer Journey Map

### What Customer Journey Map Does

Traces the end-to-end experience of a customer from awareness through purchase to advocacy, identifying touchpoints, emotions, and pain points at each stage.

### Overlap

A Journey Map requires a defined customer persona — which STP's Targeting and Positioning step produces. Without a target segment, you're mapping a journey for nobody.

### The Difference

| Dimension | STP | Customer Journey Map |
|---|---|---|
| When in process | Before execution | After target is defined |
| Question | Who do we serve and how do we win? | What does that person experience end-to-end? |
| Output | Positioning statement | Touchpoint audit + pain point list |

### Decision Rule

**STP precedes Journey Mapping.** If someone asks for a customer journey map before you've done STP, run STP first. The journey map is a downstream execution tool for the target segment you've already chosen.

### Anti-Pattern to Avoid

Building a journey map for "all customers" → same error as positioning for everyone. The journey map inherits the precision of your STP. Vague targeting produces an unusable journey map.

## STP vs. 4Ps (Marketing Mix)

### What 4Ps Does

Defines the tactical execution of a marketing strategy: Product, Price, Place, Promotion.

### The Relationship

4Ps is not an alternative to STP — it's the **execution layer that STP drives**.

```
STP → 4Ps
├── Segmentation identifies what each segment values
├── Targeting selects the segment
└── Positioning defines the benefit claim
    ├── Product: what features to build to deliver the benefit
    ├── Price: what price tier fits the segment's willingness to pay
    ├── Place: which channels reach this segment
    └── Promotion: what message, tone, and media lands with this segment
```

### Decision Rule

If the user is asking about product features, pricing, channels, or messaging — they're in 4Ps territory, but they need STP done first. If they haven't done STP, back up.

### Common Error

Jumping into 4Ps ("should we price at NT$299 or NT$399?") without knowing the target segment. Price elasticity differs by segment. The answer depends entirely on which segment you're targeting.

## Framework Sequencing: The Full Stack

When a user is building a market strategy from scratch, the correct sequence is:

```
1. Porter's Five Forces  ←  Is this industry worth entering?
         ↓
2. Ansoff Matrix         ←  What is our growth direction?
         ↓
3. SWOT                  ←  What are our capabilities vs. the environment?
         ↓
4. STP                   ←  Who do we serve and how do we win?
         ↓
5. 4Ps / Marketing Mix   ←  How do we execute tactically?
         ↓
6. Customer Journey Map  ←  What does our target experience end-to-end?
```

Each layer answers a different question. Skipping layers produces downstream errors. The most common skip is **3→5** (jumping from SWOT to 4Ps, bypassing STP entirely), which results in tactics with no coherent customer focus.

STP sits at the center of this stack — it's the translation layer between strategic direction (Porter's, Ansoff, SWOT) and tactical execution (4Ps, Journey Map). If STP is weak, everything downstream is guesswork.
