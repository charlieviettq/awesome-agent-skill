# Risk Communication: Framing Health Information Honestly

## Core Principles

### 1. Absolute Numbers First, Then Relative

**Why:** Relative risk amplifies perception; absolute risk grounds understanding.

**Example:**
```
❌ Lead: "New study shows 50% reduction in stroke risk"
   (Reader thinks: "Wow, major benefit!")

✅ Lead: "Study of 2,000 patients shows drug reduces strokes 
   from 2 per 100 per year to 1 per 100—a 50% relative reduction. 
   Doctors would need to treat 100 people to prevent one stroke"
   (Reader now has accurate sense of clinical magnitude)
```

### 2. Use Natural Frequencies Instead of Percentages

**Why:** "1 in 100" more intuitive than "1%"; "50 per 10,000" than "0.5%"

**Example:**
```
❌ "0.5% risk of serious side effect"
✅ "About 50 in 10,000 patients (1 in 200) experience serious side effect"
✅ "About 1 in 200 patients"
```

### 3. Compare to Baseline / Familiar Risks

**Why:** Risk in vacuum is meaningless; context helps reader calibrate.

**Example:**
```
❌ "Drug increases risk of blood clot by 1.5 times"
✅ "Drug increases risk of blood clot from 3 in 10,000 per year 
    to 4.5 in 10,000—a small absolute increase, though statistically 
    significant. For comparison, pregnancy carries a 5 in 10,000 
    annual risk of blood clot"
```

### 4. Acknowledge Uncertainty Honestly

**Why:** Studies have error bars; pretending certainty is dishonest.

**Example:**
```
❌ "Drug X prevents cancer"
✅ "Study suggests drug X *may* reduce cancer risk, though 
    uncertainty remains (95% CI: 10–30% reduction)"

❌ "More research needed" (vague hedge)
✅ "This finding is based on a single trial of 500 patients; 
    larger, independent trials are underway to confirm"
```

### 5. Avoid Fear-Based Language Without Mitigation

**Why:** Fear-mongering reduces trust; doom without context is irresponsible.

**Example:**
```
❌ "New virus spreading; 10 people infected"
   (No context: is 10 rare? outbreak potential? what's known about severity?)

✅ "Health ministry confirms 10 cases of new virus; 2 hospitalized, 
    no deaths to date. Virus spreads person-to-person; health officials 
    are monitoring contacts. Most patients recover without intensive care"
   (Provides denominator, trajectory, severity, and mitigation context)
```

### 6. Avoid False Reassurance

**Why:** "Everything is fine" is as misleading as catastrophizing.

**Example:**
```
❌ "Vaccine is 99% safe" (What does 99% safe mean? 1 in 100 has side effect?)
✅ "Vaccine has been administered to 1 million people. Side effects rare: 
    serious allergic reaction in 1–2 per million; mild fever in 20%; 
    protection against disease 85–95% depending on variant"
```

## Framing Trade-offs

### Positive vs. Negative Frame

**Same fact, different framing:**
- "90% of patients survived surgery" (positive frame)
- "10% of patients died in surgery" (negative frame)

Both true. Positive frame makes risk seem lower; negative frame emphasizes risk. Choose based on context:

| Scenario | Better Frame | Why |
|----------|-------------|-----|
| Preventive screening (healthy people) | Negative | Emphasizes rare-but-real risk; reduces overscreening |
| Treatment already accepted | Positive | Reassures patient; supports adherence |
| Rare serious side effect | Positive ("X% tolerate") + Negative ("Y% experience serious side effect") | Both needed for informed choice |

**Example:**
```
Setting: Patient considering cholesterol drug with serious muscle-damage risk (1 per 10,000)

❌ Positive only: "99.99% of patients tolerate this drug well"
   → Patient perceives risk as negligible; may miss important side-effect symptoms

✅ Balanced: "This drug is well-tolerated by 9,999 in 10,000 patients. 
   In rare cases (about 1 per 10,000), severe muscle damage occurs. 
   Report any unexplained muscle pain to your doctor immediately"
```

### Visual Aids vs. Text

**When to use visuals:**
- Risk icon arrays ("Out of 10,000 people, X experience this outcome")
- Forest plots (showing range of study estimates)
- Timeline graphs (showing disease trajectory, outcome timing)

**When text is better:**
- Nuanced trade-offs (surgery vs. observation)
- Multiple outcome dimensions
- When visual might oversimplify

## Common Risk Communication Errors

### Error 1: Denominator Omission

```
❌ "A study found 300 cases of illness"
   (Is 300 rare? Common? Outbreak? Unclear.)

✅ "A study of 100,000 people found 300 cases (3 per 1,000 per year), 
   a 50% increase from the prior year"
```

### Error 2: Implying Causation Without Evidence

```
❌ "After vaccine rollout, stroke cases increased 20%"
   (Implies vaccine caused strokes; doesn't prove it)

✅ "Stroke cases increased 20% in the month after vaccine rollout. 
   Epidemiologists are investigating whether this reflects: (1) 
   increased testing, (2) reporting delays, (3) age-group changes 
   in vaccination, or (4) actual causal effect. Preliminary analysis 
   of [source] suggests [preliminary finding]"
```

### Error 3: Anchoring (Using Round Numbers as Baseline)

```
❌ Comparing 0% baseline: "New treatment shows 5% improvement"
   (This sounds small if baseline is healthy; large if baseline is severe)

✅ "Among patients with disease X (10-year mortality 50% without treatment), 
   this new drug improved survival to 55%—a 5 percentage-point absolute 
   reduction in mortality"
```

### Error 4: Conflating Incidence & Prevalence

```
❌ "Every year, X disease affects 100,000 people"
   (Are these new cases or total cases? Unclear)

✅ "In 2024, there were 10,000 new cases of X disease; approximately 
   150,000 people live with X disease in Taiwan"
```

## Risk Categories & Communication Tone

| Risk Category | Threshold | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Minimal** | < 1 per 10,000 | Matter-of-fact | "Serious allergic reaction: < 1 per 10,000" |
| **Low** | 1–10 per 10,000 | Acknowledge but contextual | "Muscle pain: 1 per 100" |
| **Moderate** | 10–50 per 10,000 | Balance risk + benefit | "Nausea: 20%; resolves after first dose in most" |
| **High** | > 50 per 10,000 | Emphasize; requires mitigation | "Pregnancy loss at 10 weeks gestation: 15–20%" |

## Special Populations & Risk Nuance

### Pediatric Patients

```
❌ "Drug has low risk of side effect"
   (Parents have no baseline; "low" is subjective)

✅ "In clinical trials of children aged 5–10, 2 per 100 developed 
   mild headache; 1 per 1,000 experienced serious allergic reaction. 
   For comparison, the untreated illness carries a 1 per 100 risk of 
   hospitalization"
```

### Elderly Patients

```
❌ "Drug slightly increases fall risk"
   (Vague; falls have serious consequences in elderly)

✅ "Fall risk increases from 10% to 15% per year (from prior epidemiology). 
   For an 80-year-old, this absolute increase matters; discuss fall 
   prevention strategies (physical therapy, home safety) with your doctor"
```

### Vulnerable Populations (Low Health Literacy)

- Avoid medical jargon
- Use visual aids (icon arrays are especially effective)
- Verify understanding: "Can you tell me back what you understand about your risk?"
- Provide written summary; discuss how to contact with questions

## Transparency About Uncertainty

### Phrases That Honestly Signal Strength of Evidence

| Phrase | Evidence Strength | When to Use |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| "Research shows..." | Multiple RCTs or meta-analysis | Established findings |
| "A study suggests..." | Single RCT or strong observational | Novel findings needing replication |
| "Early evidence indicates..." | Preprint or Phase I/II trial | Very preliminary |
| "This finding requires confirmation..." | Exploratory, subgroup, or unusual | Cautious language for early stage |
| "We don't yet know..." | Evidence gap | When data is truly absent |

### Example: Evolving Knowledge

```
Initial report (case series): "Doctors report unusual symptom cluster"
→ "Preliminary findings suggest..."

Follow-up (larger cohort): "Study of 500 patients confirms..."
→ "Consistent evidence indicates..."

Meta-analysis (multiple RCTs): "Systematic review of 10 trials shows..."
→ "Established finding: [X] causes [Y]"
```

## Practical Checklist for Risk Framing

Before publishing any health risk claim:

- [ ] **Is absolute risk stated?** (not just relative risk)
- [ ] **Is denominator clear?** ("per 100", "per 10,000", time period)
- [ ] **Is baseline comparison helpful?** (context for how serious risk is)
- [ ] **Are confidence intervals or uncertainty ranges shown?** (CI or range of estimates)
- [ ] **Is evidence level signaled?** ("study shows" vs. "early research suggests")
- [ ] **Is causation claimed only where supported?** (temporal sequence, dose-response, confounding ruled out)
- [ ] **Is fear-mongering avoided?** (proportionate language to actual risk)
- [ ] **Is false reassurance avoided?** (acknowledge real risks, even if small)
- [ ] **Are vulnerable populations considered?** (children, elderly, low literacy)
- [ ] **Is mitigation/next-step action suggested?** ("what to watch for", "talk to your doctor about...")
