# OLI Paradigm: Application to Service Sector FDI

## Why Services Require a Modified OLI Reading

The standard OLI framework was built on manufacturing FDI (Dunning, 1977; drawing on cases like IBM and Ford). Services break several of its background assumptions:

| Manufacturing assumption | Service reality |
|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Product can be exported independently of production | Many services require co-presence of producer and consumer (inseparability) |
| O advantage often resides in a patent or codified technology | O advantage often resides in tacit organizational routines, culture, and reputation |
| L advantage is one option among several | L advantage is often *mandatory* — the service cannot reach the customer any other way |
| I advantage prevents knowledge leakage through licensing | I advantage prevents quality degradation and brand dilution |

These differences change *which* OLI leg does the heavy lifting and how each leg should be scored.

---

## Leg-by-Leg Adaptation

### Ownership Advantages (O) in Services

Service firms rarely own patentable manufacturing processes. Instead, O advantages cluster into three types:

**Oa — Asset-based O in services**

| Asset type | Examples | How to assess |
|------------|----------|---------------|
| Brand reputation | Marriott, McKinsey, Goldman Sachs | Customer willingness-to-pay premium vs. local substitute |
| Proprietary systems | Booking platforms, trading algorithms, logistics OS | Whether system can be licensed without losing control |
| Licensed content / regulatory capital | Insurance reserve model, banking license | Whether the license itself confers cross-border advantage |

**Ot — Transaction-based O in services**

| Capability | Examples | Signal that it is real |
|------------|----------|------------------------|
| Organizational routines | Service delivery playbooks, hiring & training models | Firm can replicate performance in new markets without key-person dependency |
| Tacit process knowledge | Audit methodology, hotel service culture | Competitors cannot imitate even after observing output |
| Relational capital | Key client relationships, regulatory relationships | Portable when senior staff move to the new market |

**Weak O signal in services**: "Our staff are friendly" or "We have good local reputation" — these are not portable O advantages. Portable means: *the advantage travels with the firm, not with the local market.*

---

### Location Advantages (L) in Services

The inseparability of production and consumption means that for many services, L is not a *differential* criterion (as in manufacturing) but a *threshold* criterion: if the customer is there, *some* form of local presence is already forced.

The analytical question shifts from "Is L better than home?" to "Which entry mode delivers the required local presence most efficiently?"

**L scoring framework for services:**

| L driver | Manufacturing weight | Service weight | Notes |
|----------|---------------------|----------------|-------|
| Market size / demand | High | High | Same |
| Trade barrier avoidance | High | Low–Medium | Services rarely blocked by tariffs; blocked by regulatory barriers instead |
| Regulatory access | Low–Medium | **Very High** | Banking licenses, telecom spectrum, professional certifications |
| Physical proximity to client | Low | **Critical for B2B services** | Consulting, legal, audit: client often requires on-site delivery |
| Labor cost arbitrage | High | **Mixed** | Cost-sensitive back-office (call centers, IT services): high. Customer-facing premium services: low |
| Knowledge cluster access | Medium | High for R&D-intensive services | Fintech in Singapore/London; biotech consulting near research hospitals |

**Key implication**: A service firm that identifies a regulatory barrier (e.g., foreign bank must incorporate locally) is not facing a *negative* L factor — the barrier is *simultaneously forcing* FDI, which strengthens the overall OLI case if O and I are present.

---

### Internalization Advantages (I) in Services

Services are disproportionately subject to internalization pressure because:

1. **Quality is observable only post-delivery**: A licensee providing substandard service damages the brand before the licensor can intervene. Manufacturing defects can be caught at the factory gate.

2. **Service quality is co-produced by staff**: Quality resides in people and culture, not in a machine spec. Franchisees can hire the "wrong" people.

3. **Client information is sensitive**: Professional services (law, consulting, finance) handle confidential client data. Licensing means sharing client access with a third party.

4. **Tacit knowledge cannot be fully written down**: A franchise manual can specify room dimensions but cannot fully transfer hotel service culture.

**I-intensity classification for service sub-sectors:**

| Service type | I-intensity | Primary driver | Likely mode |
|--------------|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| Luxury hospitality | Very High | Brand dilution risk + tacit culture | WOS or tight JV |
| Fast food / retail | High | Brand + quality → but codifiable | Franchising viable if royalty control is tight |
| Management consulting | Very High | Tacit knowledge + client confidentiality | WOS preferred |
| Audit / professional services | High–Very High | Regulatory liability + reputation | WOS or regulated network structure |
| IT services / BPO | Medium | Process is codifiable; staff turnover manages quality | Captive center (WOS) or BOT |
| Banking (retail) | High | Regulatory capital + relationship data | Local incorporation (WOS or majority JV) |
| Insurance | Medium–High | Actuarial IP + claims culture | JV common (regulatory capital partner) |
| Education / EdTech | Medium | Curriculum can be codified; delivery varies | Franchise + JV hybrid |

## Entry Mode Decision Table for Services

Applying the standard OLI configuration table, with service-specific annotations:

| O | L | I | Recommended mode | Service caveat |
|---|---|---|-----------------|----------------|
| Strong | Strong | Strong | WOS (greenfield or acquisition) | Preferred for high-brand / high-tacit services |
| Strong | Strong | Moderate | JV (local partner for distribution / regulatory access) | Common in banking, insurance where regulatory capital partner is needed |
| Strong | Strong | Low | Franchising / licensing | Fast food, retail; only viable if quality is fully codifiable |
| Strong | Weak | Any | Export / remote delivery | Viable for purely digital services (SaaS, remote consulting) |
| Weak | Strong | Any | Do not FDI; acquire O first or partner | Risk: the firm will lose the relationship to a local competitor |

**Service-specific override**: If the service has a mandatory co-presence requirement (patient must be in the hospital, client must be in the branch) and O is present, treat L as automatically "Strong" — skip the L scoring step and proceed directly to I assessment.

## Worked Example: Mid-Market Hotel Chain Expanding from Taiwan to Vietnam

**Firm**: Taiwanese business-hotel operator, 40 properties, brand recognition strong in Taiwan and Southeast Asian business traveler segment.

**Target**: Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam — opening one flagship property.

### Step 1: O Assessment

| O component | Evidence | Strength |
|-------------|----------|----------|
| Brand (Oa) | 4.3/5 average TripAdvisor; corporate accounts with 15 MNCs | Moderate (regional, not global) |
| Proprietary booking + revenue management system (Oa) | Internally built system; not sold to third parties | Moderate |
| Service culture + training playbook (Ot) | 60-day onboarding protocol; 92% first-year retention | Strong — demonstrated replicability |
| Regional relationships (Ot) | Existing Taiwanese corporate clients with Vietnam operations | Strong — portable demand |

**O assessment: Moderate–Strong**. Main O is organizational routines and portable client relationships, not a globally recognized brand.

### Step 2: L Assessment

| L driver | Vietnam signal | Strength |
|----------|---------------|----------|
| Market size | Ho Chi Minh City: 8M+ population; ASEAN business hub | Strong |
| Business traveler growth | 12% YoY inbound corporate travel growth (2019–2023) | Strong |
| Trade barriers to export | Service inseparability → export impossible | L is mandatory |
| Labor cost | Hotel staff wages ~30% of Taiwan equivalent | Strong (efficiency-seeking) |
| Regulatory access | Foreign hotel brands can operate WOS under Vietnam's 2014 Investment Law | No blocking barrier |
| Supply chain | Existing F&B suppliers in Vietnam through existing ASEAN network | Moderate positive |

**L assessment: Strong**. Physical co-presence is mandatory; all supporting factors positive.

### Step 3: I Assessment

| I driver | Assessment |
|----------|-----------|
| Brand dilution risk | Moderate — brand is regional, not global; risk real but limited |
| Tacit service culture | High — the 60-day training protocol is not fully codifiable; performance drops without direct management |
| Quality control | High — corporate accounts have SLA expectations; third-party operator cannot be held to same standard |
| Contractual incompleteness | Moderate — franchise manual could cover ~70% of operations; remaining 30% is culture-dependent |

**I assessment: Strong**. Tacit service culture and corporate client SLAs push toward internalization.

### Step 4: OLI Configuration and Entry Mode

**Configuration: O (Moderate-Strong) + L (Strong) + I (Strong)**

→ Recommended mode: **Wholly-Owned Subsidiary** via greenfield (own-brand hotel).

**Rationale**: The primary O advantage is organizational capability that must be deployed by the firm's own management. A franchise or management contract would sever the training pipeline that creates the O advantage.

**Alternative considered**: Management contract with local property owner (asset-light). This would reduce capital exposure but would subordinate management authority to the owner — inconsistent with Strong I requirement. Acceptable *only* if the local owner agrees to a service agreement that gives the Taiwanese operator full staffing and operating authority (functionally equivalent to WOS in terms of control).

## Special Cases in Service FDI

### Digital / Platform Services

For services delivered entirely digitally (SaaS, streaming, online marketplace), the inseparability constraint disappears. L advantage must be reassessed using network effects and data localization requirements rather than physical proximity:

- **Data localization laws** (e.g., Vietnam's Cybersecurity Law 2019, China's PIPL) can create a *forced* L advantage that mirrors physical inseparability — the service cannot legally serve customers from abroad.
- In these cases, L may flip from "Weak" to "Strong/Mandatory" purely due to regulation.

### Professional Services Networks (Legal, Audit, Consulting)

The Big Four accounting firms and Magic Circle law firms operate through **network structures** that are neither WOS nor franchise in the traditional sense. Each national firm is typically a separate legal entity but shares brand, methodology, and quality standards through a centralized coordination body.

From an OLI perspective:
- **O**: Methodology and global brand (centrally held)
- **L**: Each jurisdiction requires local licensure (regulatory barrier forces presence)
- **I**: Moderate — national firms have some autonomy; quality managed through audit standards and partner admission processes, not hierarchical control

This structure reflects an **I advantage that is partially internalized** — a hybrid that does not appear cleanly in the standard 2×2 OLI mode table. When scoring I for professional service networks, note that "internalization" can be achieved through governance mechanisms short of full equity ownership.

### Financial Services

Banking and insurance FDI is heavily conditioned by host-country capital adequacy and licensing requirements. A foreign bank entering a new market typically cannot operate as a branch of the parent without regulatory approval; it must often establish a locally capitalized subsidiary.

The **capital requirement** acts as an I-moderating factor: even when the firm prefers WOS, it may need a local equity partner to meet regulatory capital minimums (common in Southeast Asia and Africa). This produces JV structures driven not by I weakness but by **L-imposed capital constraints**. When assessing I for financial services:

- Score I based on the firm's *operational* internalization preference (usually High)
- Separately note L-imposed capital constraints as a **mode modifier** that may force JV even when WOS is preferred

## Common Scoring Errors Specific to Services

1. **Conflating local brand with portable O**: A service firm well-known in its home market has *local* brand equity, not an O advantage. O requires the advantage to be transferable to a foreign market where the firm is unknown.

2. **Scoring L as "Weak" because export is theoretically possible**: For co-presence services, "export" means the customer travels to you — not a market entry strategy. Always flag inseparability before scoring L.

3. **Underweighting I for franchise-adjacent service models**: A franchise manual covers process but not culture. If the service's quality premium depends on culture (luxury hotel, premium consulting), I is stronger than the existence of a franchise manual implies.

4. **Missing regulatory capital as a JV driver**: Recommending WOS for financial services without checking minimum capital rules can produce an infeasible recommendation.

5. **Treating "local partner knowledge" as sufficient JV justification**: Needing local market knowledge is not a JV rationale — it can be acquired through hiring. JV is justified when the partner contributes *non-replicable* assets (regulatory license, government relationships, proprietary distribution network).

## Summary: Service-Sector OLI Quick Checklist

```
O check:
  [ ] Is the O advantage portable (works in a market where the firm is unknown)?
  [ ] Is the O advantage tacit or asset-based?
  [ ] Can it be replicated abroad without key-person dependency?

L check:
  [ ] Does the service require co-presence? (If yes, L = mandatory)
  [ ] Are there regulatory barriers that force local presence?
  [ ] What is the L motivation: market / regulatory access / labor cost?

I check:
  [ ] Is service quality fully codifiable in a franchise manual?
  [ ] Does brand reputation depend on operational control of staff?
  [ ] Does the service involve confidential client information?
  [ ] Is tacit culture a significant quality driver?

Mode modifier for services:
  [ ] Does the host country impose capital adequacy / local incorporation rules?
  [ ] If yes, does the requirement force a JV even when I is Strong?
```
