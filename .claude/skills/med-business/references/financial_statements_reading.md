# Reading Financial Statements: The Three Tables & Red Flags

Professional financial journalists read three core statements (income, balance sheet, cash flow) to assess company health and detect warning signals. This guide highlights key lines and red flags.

---

## The Three Tables Overview

| Table | Purpose | Key Lines | Watch For |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Income Statement** (損益表) | Revenue, costs, profit over a period | Revenue, COGS, Operating expenses, EBIT, EPS | Revenue recognition timing, one-time items, margin compression |
| **Balance Sheet** (資產負債表) | Assets, liabilities, equity at a point in time | Current assets, inventories, receivables, cash, debt, equity | Receivables growth w/o revenue growth, inventory bloat, sudden asset write-downs |
| **Cash Flow Statement** (現金流量表) | Cash in/out from operations, investing, financing | Operating cash flow, capex, free cash flow | Positive profit but negative operating cash (red flag), capex cuts (survival mode) |

## Income Statement Red Flags

### Revenue Recognition

**What to check:**
- Does revenue growth match operating-cash-flow growth? If not, why?
  - Revenue +20%, OCF flat = receivables are inflating (cash may not come)
  
- Related-party revenue spike?
  - Sales to subsidiaries, related entities not at arm's length
  - Look for loss on related-party sales in subsequent years (wind-down)

**Red flag patterns:**
- Quarter-end spikes (channel stuffing — pushing inventory to distributors at month-end, reverse in next quarter)
- Revenue recognition on consignment or with side agreements ("right of return")

### Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Behavior

**What to check:**
- Gross margin trend: is it stable, shrinking, or inflating?
  - Stable = good operational control
  - Shrinking without explanation = pricing pressure or cost inflation outpacing price increases
  - Inflating suddenly = one-time favorable item (e.g., inventory write-up) or accounting change

**Red flag:**
- COGS ↓ while revenue ↑ → margin expansion may be unsustainable (likely due to leverage, asset-light moves, or mix shift to higher-margin products)

### Operating Expenses (OpEx)

**What to check:**
- R&D as % of revenue: stable = consistent investment; declining = potential future competitiveness erosion
- SG&A (Selling, General, Administrative) as % of revenue
  - If growing faster than revenue, profitability is being compressed
  - If revenue flat but OpEx up 30% = overhead bloat

### One-Time Items

**Always segregate:**
- Asset sales (gain/loss on disposal of subsidiary, plant, equipment)
- Restructuring costs (severance, plant closures)
- Impairments (writing down goodwill, intangible assets)
- Litigation settlements
- Merger-related gains/losses

**Ask:** If we exclude one-timers, what is the sustainable operating income?

### EPS Growth

**Red flag patterns:**
- EPS grows faster than revenue → not from operations; likely share buyback (fewer shares outstanding)
- EPS grows while margin contracts → earnings management, one-time items, or financial engineering (debt leverage)

## Balance Sheet Red Flags

### Current Ratio & Liquidity

- **Current ratio** = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities (normal: 1.0–2.0)
- **Declining trend** = liquidity squeeze; company may struggle to pay near-term obligations

### Accounts Receivable (A/R)

**Red flag:**
- A/R growing much faster than revenue
  - Example: Revenue +10%, A/R +30% → customers taking longer to pay or company is extending terms to drum up sales
  - **Aging analysis:** What % of A/R is >90 days old? Rising % = collectibility risk

**Calculation:** Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) = (A/R ÷ Revenue) × 365
- Comparison: Is it increasing vs. prior year? vs. industry?

### Inventory

**Red flag:**
- Inventory growing faster than revenue
  - Demand is softer; company over-ordered
  - Obsolescence risk (tech products go stale)

**Calculation:** Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) = (Inventory ÷ COGS) × 365
- Declining DIO = good (faster turnover); rising DIO = risk

### Goodwill & Intangible Assets

**Watch for:**
- Large goodwill (often from acquisitions)
- Impairment charges in later years (acquisition did not work out)
- High goodwill relative to total assets = risky accounting (goodwill can evaporate; not as solid as inventory or equipment)

### Debt & Equity Structure

- **Debt-to-Equity ratio** = Total Debt ÷ Shareholders' Equity
  - High ratio = company is leveraged; interest payments compete with dividends
  - During downturns, high debt = solvency risk

- **Interest Coverage** = EBIT ÷ Interest Expense
  - < 2.0× = company struggling to cover interest; default risk rises

### Contingent Liabilities

- Look for lawsuits, guarantees, or commitments mentioned in footnotes
- Large contingent liability (e.g., pending litigation, environmental cleanup) can become actual expense

## Cash Flow Statement Red Flags

### Operating Cash Flow (OCF) vs. Net Income

**Healthy company:**
- OCF ≈ Net Income (cash earnings match reported earnings)

**Red flags:**
- OCF < Net Income consistently → company booking revenue that isn't turning to cash
  - Examples: generous credit terms, related-party sales with delayed payment, inventory buildup
  
- OCF negative while Net Income positive → **alarm bell**
  - Company is not generating cash despite being "profitable"
  - Unsustainable; will need financing or asset sales

### Free Cash Flow (FCF)

**Calculation:** FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures

- FCF > 0 = company has cash left for dividends, debt repayment, or growth investments
- FCF < 0 = company is burning cash; either investing heavily (capex spike) or in distress

**Red flag:**
- Consistent negative FCF for 3+ years without clear plan (e.g., infrastructure buildout)

### Capex Behavior

- **Normal capex:** 3–5% of revenue for mature companies; higher for capital-intensive sectors
- **Cliff capex cuts:** If capex suddenly drops 50%, company may be in survival mode
- **Massive capex increase:** Can be good (growth) or bad (overexpansion); assess against industry norms

## Valuation Multiples: Context & Comparisons

### P/E Ratio Usage

- **Useful for:** Comparing profitable companies in same industry
- **Caution:** Does not work well for:
  - Loss-making companies (negative P/E is meaningless)
  - Different fiscal years / one-time items
  - Different accounting standards (GAAP vs IFRS)

**Safe practice:**
- Compare P/E only to peers (same sector, size, geography)
- Use forward P/E (based on next-year EPS estimate) for growth companies
- Use trailing P/E (based on past 12 months) for mature companies

### P/B Ratio Usage

- **Useful for:** Banks, utilities, asset-heavy industries where book value = real value
- **Caution:** Intangible-heavy companies (software, media, pharma) have inflated P/B (book value understates real value)

### PEG Ratio

- **Formula:** P/E Ratio ÷ Expected annual earnings growth rate (%)
- **PEG < 1.0** traditionally deemed "cheap" relative to growth
- **Caution:** Very sensitive to growth forecast; small forecast changes swing PEG wildly
- **Use carefully:** Better for long-term value-growth analysis than news reporting

## Accounting Policy Red Flags

### Revenue Recognition Policy Changes

- **Watch for:** Switch from cash-basis to accrual, or loosening of revenue recognition criteria
- **Question:** Why the change? Is it required by new standards, or is company managing earnings?

### Depreciation/Amortization Changes

- **Extends useful life:** (e.g., 5 years → 7 years) = lower depreciation charge = higher profit
- **Question:** Is this justified by extended asset life, or is it earnings management?

### Inventory Valuation Method

- **FIFO vs LIFO:** Different methods yield different COGS and profit during inflation
- **Question:** Why did company switch? Did it boost reported earnings?

### Provisions & Reserves

- Accrual for uncertain liabilities (warranty claims, bad debts, restructuring)
- **Watch for:** Sudden large provisions (suggests prior earnings were overstated)
- **Watch for:** Released provisions (boosts current-period earnings; potentially suspicious)

## Quick Financial Health Checklist

Before publishing, ask:

- [ ] **Revenue realistic?** Check OCF vs. reported profit — are they in sync?
- [ ] **Margin stable?** Gross and operating margins within historical range?
- [ ] **Receivables healthy?** A/R growing in line with revenue, or spiking?
- [ ] **Inventory sane?** DIO stable, or inflating? Risk of obsolescence?
- [ ] **Debt manageable?** Debt-to-Equity and interest coverage within norms?
- [ ] **Cash positive?** FCF positive, or burning cash?
- [ ] **One-timers noted?** If profit spiked, did it come from operations or one-off items?
- [ ] **Capex rational?** Is capex level consistent with growth strategy, or a warning sign?
- [ ] **Goodwill stable?** Large goodwill could signal acquisition risk; watch for impairments.
- [ ] **No red accounting flags?** Any unusual policy changes, provisions, or reserve releases?

If you cannot answer these, flag the analysis as incomplete and ask the company for clarification.
