# E-Commerce Benchmark Data by Industry

Benchmark numbers without industry context are misleading. A 1.2% conversion rate is excellent for consumer electronics and terrible for food delivery. Use this reference to compare against peers, not against generic "e-commerce averages."

---

## How to Use These Benchmarks

Benchmarks answer one question: **Is our number a us-problem or an industry-problem?**

If your conversion rate matches the industry median, the fix is not UX — it's pricing, positioning, or traffic quality. If your rate is half the median, UX/checkout friction is the first place to look.

Always compare **same channel, same device type**. Mobile and desktop benchmarks are listed separately where data differs significantly.

### Cart-to-Checkout Rate (ATC → Checkout Initiated)

Industry median: **40–60%** of users who add to cart begin checkout.

Below 35% suggests: cart page friction, forced account creation, or shipping cost shock.

### Checkout Completion Rate (Checkout Initiated → Purchase)

| Checkout Type | Completion Rate |
|---------------|----------------|
| Guest checkout available | 55–75% |
| Account required | 35–55% |
| 1-page checkout | 65–80% |
| Multi-step (3+ steps) | 45–65% |
| Saved payment methods | 70–85% |

**Cart abandonment rate** = 1 − (Purchase ÷ ATC). Industry average is 70–75%. Above 80% warrants intervention.

## Revenue per Session (Master Metric)

Revenue per Session (RPS) = Revenue ÷ Sessions = CVR × AOV

This collapses funnel quality and revenue size into one number. Use it to compare across channels and periods without CVR/AOV being gamed separately.

**Worked example:**

```
Channel A: Email
  Sessions: 5,000
  CVR: 4.2%
  AOV: $88
  RPS = 0.042 × $88 = $3.70

Channel B: Paid Social (prospecting)
  Sessions: 50,000
  CVR: 0.6%
  AOV: $72
  RPS = 0.006 × $72 = $0.43

If CPC for Channel B = $0.80, you're losing money at $0.43 RPS.
If CPC for Channel A = $1.50, you're profitable at $3.70 RPS.
```

**Target RPS by vertical** (desktop, blended channels):

| Vertical | Minimum Viable RPS | Healthy RPS |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Fashion | $1.50 | $3.00+ |
| Electronics | $2.00 | $5.00+ |
| Health & Beauty | $1.80 | $4.00+ |
| Home & Furniture | $2.50 | $6.00+ |
| Food | $1.20 | $3.50+ |

## Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) vs. LTV Ratios

The LTV:CAC ratio determines whether your acquisition economics are sustainable.

| Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| < 1:1 | Destroying value — stop scaling |
| 1:1 – 2:1 | Break-even, no room for error |
| 3:1 | Healthy — industry standard target |
| 4:1+ | Strong unit economics, room to invest |
| > 6:1 | May be underinvesting in acquisition |

**LTV calculation (12-month simplified):**

```
LTV₁₂ = AOV × Purchase_Frequency₁₂ × Gross_Margin

Example:
  AOV = $75
  Purchases per year = 3.2
  Gross margin = 45%
  LTV₁₂ = $75 × 3.2 × 0.45 = $108

If CAC = $30, LTV:CAC = 108:30 = 3.6:1 → healthy
If CAC = $60, LTV:CAC = 108:60 = 1.8:1 → marginal
```

**By vertical, typical 12-month LTV:CAC targets:**

| Vertical | Target Ratio | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Subscription / Consumables | 3:1 – 5:1 | High repeat justifies higher CAC |
| Fashion | 2.5:1 – 4:1 | Seasonal peaks compress ratio |
| Electronics | 2:1 – 3:1 | Low repeat; margin-dependent |
| Furniture | 2:1 – 3:1 | One-time purchase; needs high AOV |

## How to Flag an Anomaly vs. Noise

Use a simple threshold check before escalating a metric drop:

```
Signal threshold = abs(current - baseline) > (2 × weekly_stddev)

If sessions drop 8% but weekly variance is ±12%, this is noise.
If conversion rate drops 1.2 pp and weekly variance is ±0.2 pp, investigate.
```

Practical rule of thumb for weekly reporting:

| Metric | "Worth investigating" threshold |
|--------|--------------------------------|
| Sessions | > 15% WoW or > 10% YoY |
| Conversion rate | > 0.5 pp absolute change |
| AOV | > 10% change without a known promotion |
| Revenue | > 20% WoW without a known campaign |
| Cart abandonment | > 5 pp change |

Smaller deviations should be noted but not acted on without corroborating data from a second metric.
