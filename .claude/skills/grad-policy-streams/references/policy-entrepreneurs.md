# Policy Entrepreneurs

Policy entrepreneurs are the agents who couple the three streams. Kingdon (1984) defines them as individuals willing to invest resources — time, energy, reputation, money — to promote a position in anticipation of future gain. They are not just advocates; they are strategic opportunists who recognize when a window is opening and act to push their preferred solution through it.

---

## Who Qualifies as a Policy Entrepreneur

Policy entrepreneurs can occupy any position relative to government:

| Position | Examples | Typical Resources |
|----------|----------|-------------------|
| Elected officials | Senators, committee chairs | Formal authority, visibility |
| Career bureaucrats | Agency directors, program staff | Technical expertise, persistence |
| Interest group leaders | Union presidents, trade association heads | Organized constituencies, money |
| Researchers / experts | Think tank scholars, academic economists | Ideas, credibility, framing |
| Journalists / advocates | Investigative reporters, NGO directors | Agenda-setting power, public salience |
| Consultants / lobbyists | Policy counsel, former officials | Connections, process knowledge |

**Critical point**: position is neither necessary nor sufficient. A career bureaucrat with no formal authority can couple streams; a cabinet secretary can fail to. What matters is strategic deployment of whatever resources the entrepreneur possesses.

---

## The Five Resources That Enable Coupling

Kingdon and subsequent work (Mintrom 1997, Mintrom & Norman 2009) identify five resource categories:

### 1. Expertise
Detailed knowledge of the policy domain — understanding what solutions are technically feasible, where budget constraints bind, and what has been tried before. Expertise allows the entrepreneur to speak credibly in the policy stream.

### 2. Connections / Social Capital
Access to decision-makers across multiple streams. An entrepreneur who knows the relevant committee staff (politics stream), the OMB examiner (policy stream), and the agency data chief (problem stream) can coordinate all three simultaneously.

### 3. Authoritative Position
Formal standing that gives voice — a position on an advisory committee, a regulatory role, an elected office. This does not require being the final decision-maker; it requires being *in the room*.

### 4. Claim to Speak for Others
The ability to credibly represent a constituency. An entrepreneur who can say "the business community supports this" or "public polling at 67% shows demand" converts diffuse preferences into a political stream asset.

### 5. Persistence and Strategic Patience
Willingness to wait. Most windows are not opened on demand — they occur when a focusing event (problem stream) or election (politics stream) creates an opening. Entrepreneurs who have kept their solution "on the shelf" ready for deployment are positioned to move fast.

## The Coupling Actions

Coupling is not just attaching a solution to a problem. It is a set of specific rhetorical and strategic moves:

### Problem-Solution Matching
Reframe the available solution to fit the newly salient problem. The solution does not change; the *description* of it does.

> **Example (illustrative)**: A carbon tax proposal (pre-existing solution in the policy stream) is reframed from "economic efficiency measure" to "energy security tool" when rising oil prices make energy dependence a salient problem. The policy text is identical; the coupling language shifts.

### Softening
Circulating proto-proposals through policy communities before the window opens — getting early reactions, refining technical details, building familiarity. When the window opens, the solution is already "warmed up" and does not require months of review.

### Building Coalitions Across Streams
Deliberately recruiting actors from each stream to form a cross-stream coalition:
- A scientific agency to lend problem-stream credibility
- A bipartisan legislative champion for political-stream legitimacy
- A respected think tank to provide policy-stream analysis

### Timing the Push
Recognizing the window type and matching the coupling strategy to it:

| Window Type | Trigger | Entrepreneur Strategy |
|-------------|---------|----------------------|
| Problem window | Focusing event (disaster, scandal, crisis) | Move fast; attach solution before competing definitions solidify |
| Political window | Election, administration change | Prepare solution in advance; be ready at transition; brief incoming officials |
| Hybrid window | Both occur simultaneously | Highest priority; most durable policy change possible |

## The Entrepreneur's Decision Calculus

Before committing resources, a rational entrepreneur assesses three factors (adapted from Mintrom & Norman 2009):

```
P(success) × V(outcome) > C(investment)

Where:
  P(success)   = subjective probability that window will open AND solution 
                 will be adopted if pushed
  V(outcome)   = value of the policy change (substantive + personal return)
  C(investment) = resources consumed: time, political capital, career risk
```

This is not a literal calculation but a useful diagnostic. Entrepreneurs who chronically misestimate P(success) tend to either:
- **Over-invest** in closed windows (policy advocates who never read political conditions), or  
- **Under-invest** by waiting for certainty that never comes

### Diagnosing Window Probability

A practical checklist for assessing whether a window is genuinely open:

```
PROBLEM STREAM CONDITIONS (any 2 of 3 increase P)
  [ ] A focusing event has occurred within the last 6 months
  [ ] New indicator data has broken a politically salient threshold
  [ ] Feedback from an existing program has surfaced a recognized failure

POLICY STREAM CONDITIONS (all 3 required)
  [ ] At least one technically feasible solution exists and is documented
  [ ] Solution has been softened (circulated, refined) in relevant communities
  [ ] Solution is perceived as value-compatible by probable coalition majority

POLITICS STREAM CONDITIONS (any 1 of 3)
  [ ] Administration change or major election within 12 months
  [ ] Consistent 60%+ public support in recent polling
  [ ] At least one organized interest group has made this a priority issue

WINDOW ASSESSMENT
  0-2 conditions met: window likely closed — maintain/refine solution, wait
  3-4 conditions met: window possibly cracking — position, do not commit fully
  5-6 conditions met: window open — couple NOW, mobilize all resources
```

## A Worked Example: U.S. Medicare Prescription Drug Benefit (2003)

This example illustrates how an identified entrepreneur operated across all three streams.

**Context**: The MMA (Medicare Modernization Act) added a prescription drug benefit (Part D) for the first time since Medicare's 1965 founding.

### Problem Stream
- Indicator: Drug prices rose significantly in the late 1990s; seniors increasingly reported choosing between drugs and food in polling data
- Focusing event: High-profile media coverage of seniors crossing into Canada to buy cheaper drugs

**Problem definition that stuck**: "Senior drug costs are a government responsibility, not a personal responsibility" — this framing made a market failure argument accessible to a politically moderate audience.

### Policy Stream
- The solution (a prescription benefit) had been in the policy primeval soup since the 1990s
- Clinton-era proposals had failed, but the policy community had spent nearly a decade softening: CBO scoring existed, multiple draft legislative texts had been circulated, means-testing variants had been debated
- Technical feasibility was established; the main unresolved question was the role of private insurers (PDPs) vs. direct government administration

### Politics Stream
- 2000 election brought a Republican administration that had campaigned on a drug benefit as a "compassionate conservative" agenda item
- Republican majorities in both chambers after 2002 midterms
- AARP (largest senior interest group) ultimately endorsed the bill — a major political-stream shift that broke Democratic opposition framing

### The Entrepreneur
Thomas Scully (CMS Administrator) and key Senate Finance Committee staff played entrepreneur roles — specifically:
- Scully maintained direct back-channels between White House and industry (PhRMA, insurers) — coalition-building across streams
- Used his position authority to keep CBO cost estimates strategically contained (a move later investigated, but illustrating the "authoritative position" resource in action)
- Timed the legislative push for 2003, exploiting the political window before 2004 elections narrowed political space

### Outcome
Part D passed with the private PDP structure intact. The alternative — direct government negotiation with drug companies — was excluded. The entrepreneur's preferred solution (market-based) was adopted over the competing proposal (government price negotiation) because it had been better softened and fit the politics stream's composition.

**Lesson**: The winning solution was not the most technically optimal; it was the one that was pre-positioned, coalition-backed, and politically compatible.

## Common Entrepreneur Failures

### Premature Coupling
Pushing a solution before the political stream is ready. The solution gets labeled, opposed, and becomes harder to advance once political conditions improve. *The risk: the act of coupling closes the window by mobilizing opposition.*

### Misreading Problem Framing
Attaching a solution to the wrong problem definition. Even if both streams are active, a mismatch between the problem's public framing and the solution's stated purpose creates incoherence that opponents exploit.

> Failed example pattern: A carbon pricing proposal framed as "cost savings" when the problem stream is primarily driven by a natural disaster framing that demands immediate, visible action — not long-run efficiency.

### Neglecting Softening
Rushing a technically immature solution through an open window. The solution fails implementation scrutiny, generating negative feedback that contaminates the problem stream and makes future attempts harder.

### Over-Reliance on One Resource
Entrepreneurs who rely exclusively on formal authority lose leverage when they leave office. Those who rely only on expertise can be bypassed when political appointees change. Durable entrepreneurship requires at least three of the five resources.

## Distinguishing Policy Entrepreneurs from Issue Advocates

This distinction matters for analysis:

| Dimension | Issue Advocate | Policy Entrepreneur |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Goal | Change public opinion | Couple streams to get policy adopted |
| Time horizon | Long; willing to wait decades | Opportunistic; moves when window opens |
| Relationship to solutions | Committed to one "correct" solution | Flexible on solution design to fit political stream |
| Relationship to problems | Committed to one problem framing | Willing to reframe problem to match solution |
| Success metric | Raised awareness | Policy adoption |
| Typical failure mode | Never enters politics stream | Sticks to principle, misses window |

Confusing the two leads to misidentifying who the entrepreneur actually is in a policy case. The policy entrepreneur may not be the most prominent public advocate.
