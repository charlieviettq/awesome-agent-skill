# Speech Structures

Five proven templates for structuring persuasive speeches and written arguments. Each template is matched to a specific rhetorical context, with the appeals (Ethos/Pathos/Logos) explicitly mapped to each section.

---

## Template 1: Classic Aristotelian (General Persuasion)

Best for: formal speeches, proposals, academic arguments where the audience expects structured reasoning.

```
1. Exordium     — Hook + Ethos establishment
2. Narratio     — Context / background (Logos framing)
3. Propositio   — Thesis statement (what you're arguing)
4. Confirmatio  — Evidence for your position (Logos dominant)
5. Refutatio    — Acknowledge and rebut counterarguments (Ethos: fairness)
6. Peroratio    — Emotional close + call to action (Pathos dominant)
```

**Why the order matters**: Ethos must come before Logos. If the audience doesn't trust you by section 4, your evidence won't land. Refutatio before Peroratio is critical — addressing objections while you still have their analytical attention; trying to rebut after an emotional close feels defensive.

**Worked example — pitch to a city council for a new cycling infrastructure budget:**

| Section | What to say | Appeals |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Exordium | "I've lived in this district for 12 years and run the urban planning non-profit that surveyed 800 residents last spring." | Ethos |
| Narratio | "The cycling accident rate on Renai Road has doubled since 2021. Currently 3 of 5 intersections lack protected lanes." | Logos (framing) |
| Propositio | "I'm asking for NT$12M to implement protected lanes on 4km of Renai Road." | — |
| Confirmatio | "Cities that implemented equivalent infrastructure saw a 40% accident reduction (Barcelona 2019, Amsterdam 2021). Our cost-per-km is 30% below the national average." | Logos |
| Refutatio | "Some worry this will reduce parking. We've designed the plan to eliminate only 18 spaces while adding 200 bike parking spots — net positive for the district's 3,200 daily cyclists." | Ethos (fairness) + Logos |
| Peroratio | "The next family to lose someone at that intersection shouldn't have to wonder if we could have done something. We can. Let's vote yes today." | Pathos |

## Template 2: Monroe's Motivated Sequence (Call to Action)

Best for: sales presentations, fundraising asks, any speech where the explicit goal is to get the audience to DO something.

Developed by Alan Monroe at Purdue in the 1930s; widely used in persuasive speech training.

```
1. Attention    — Disrupt the audience's passive state (Pathos)
2. Need         — Establish a problem that must be solved (Logos + Pathos)
3. Satisfaction — Present your solution (Logos + Ethos)
4. Visualization — Paint the future state with/without your solution (Pathos)
5. Action       — Specific, concrete ask (clear call-to-action)
```

**Critical distinction from Aristotelian**: Monroe adds Visualization explicitly. Most speakers skip straight from solution to ask — this leaves the audience intellectually convinced but not emotionally ready to act. Visualization bridges that gap.

**Worked example — internal pitch to adopt a new project management tool:**

| Step | Script excerpt | Appeals |
|------|---------------|---------|
| Attention | "We lost the Fubon account last quarter. The post-mortem found 3 missed deadlines — all caused by version conflicts in our current system." | Pathos (fear/loss) |
| Need | "We track 47 active projects across 6 teams. Right now, 30% of our weekly sync time is spent reconstructing what happened, not deciding what to do next." | Logos |
| Satisfaction | "Linear integrates with our existing GitHub workflow. Our eng lead ran a 2-week pilot — ticket resolution time dropped 22%." | Logos + Ethos (credibility via pilot) |
| Visualization | "Imagine: next quarter's review, you pull up a dashboard that shows every project status in real time. No last-minute scrambles. No 'who has the latest file?'" | Pathos (aspiration) |
| Action | "I'm asking for approval to migrate the first two teams by May 1. That's a 3-week timeline. Can I get sign-off today?" | — (specific ask) |

## Template 3: Problem-Agitate-Solution (PAS)

Best for: short-form persuasion — emails, memos, one-page proposals, social media threads. Works in 3 paragraphs.

```
Problem     — Name the problem the audience already feels (Pathos recognition)
Agitate     — Deepen the pain: consequences, scale, urgency (Pathos + Logos)
Solution    — Your answer, with evidence (Logos + Ethos)
```

**PAS is the minimum viable persuasion structure.** If a message can't be reduced to these three moves, it isn't clear enough.

**Worked example — email asking management to approve remote work policy:**

> **Problem**: Our current policy requires all staff to be in-office 5 days a week, but half our team commutes 90+ minutes each way.
>
> **Agitate**: We've lost 3 senior engineers in 6 months — exit interviews cited commute as the primary factor. Replacing each costs approximately NT$800,000 in recruiting and onboarding. At current attrition rates, we're on track to spend NT$2.4M this year on preventable turnover.
>
> **Solution**: I'm proposing a 3/2 hybrid model (3 in-office, 2 remote). Companies with similar profiles — including our direct competitor Appier — have used this model since 2022. I've drafted an implementation plan that maintains our collaboration norms. Happy to walk you through it this week.

**What Agitate is doing**: most writers go directly from Problem to Solution, which feels like a pitch. Agitate makes the problem visceral and urgent before you offer relief. The audience must feel the problem is worth solving before they care about your solution.

## Template 4: The Sandwich (Diplomatic Feedback / Difficult Asks)

Best for: performance reviews, critiques, negotiation openings, any situation where the audience is likely to be defensive.

```
Positive     — Genuine acknowledgment of what is working (Ethos: good faith)
Core ask     — The challenge, critique, or request (Logos)
Forward path — Collaborative next step, not a demand (Pathos: shared future)
```

**Warning**: The Sandwich is widely misused as a manipulation technique where the positives are hollow filler. Audiences recognize this instantly and it destroys Ethos. The positive frame must be genuine. If you can't find something real to affirm, lead with shared context instead.

**Worked example — telling a colleague their analysis has a critical flaw:**

> **Positive**: "The competitive landscape section is the strongest I've seen from anyone on the team — really sharp segmentation work."
>
> **Core ask**: "The revenue projection on page 7 uses last year's FX rate. At current rates, the NPV drops by about 15%, which likely changes our recommendation. That needs to be corrected before we present to the board."
>
> **Forward path**: "If you can update the model today, I can re-review by end of day and we can still hit tomorrow's deadline together."

## Template 5: The Pyramid (Executive Communication)

Best for: briefings to senior executives, board presentations, situations where the audience may stop you at any moment and you need your conclusion first.

Developed by Barbara Minto (McKinsey) as the "Minto Pyramid Principle."

```
Conclusion    — State the answer/recommendation immediately (no buildup)
  Key point 1 — First supporting argument
    Evidence A
    Evidence B
  Key point 2 — Second supporting argument
    Evidence C
    Evidence D
  Key point 3 — Third supporting argument
    Evidence E
```

**This inverts the Aristotelian structure deliberately.** Executives operate under time pressure and high information density. They need to know if they agree with your conclusion before they'll invest attention in your evidence. If you bury the recommendation at the end, you've lost them.

**Structure rule**: Maximum 3 key points at any level. If you have 4, merge two or cut one.

**Worked example — briefing the CEO on a vendor negotiation:**

> **Conclusion**: "I recommend we switch our cloud provider from AWS to GCP at renewal in Q3. Expected savings: NT$3.2M annually."
>
> **Key point 1 — Cost**: "Our current AWS bill is NT$8.1M/year. GCP's committed use pricing brings equivalent workloads to NT$4.9M. We've modeled this against our actual usage for the past 12 months."
>
> **Key point 2 — Migration risk is manageable**: "We already run 30% of workloads on GCP via our data pipeline. The remaining migration is estimated at 6 engineer-weeks — one-time cost of NT$600K, ROI in under 3 months."
>
> **Key point 3 — Strategic fit**: "GCP's AI/ML tooling aligns with our roadmap. AWS has no equivalent to Vertex AI at our scale and budget."

**What to do if the executive disagrees with the conclusion**: Don't restructure to build-up. Instead, ask which key point they're skeptical of and go deeper on that branch. The pyramid makes it easy to navigate to the disputed sub-argument.

## Choosing a Template: Decision Table

| Situation | Audience state | Time available | Recommended template |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Formal speech / academic | Attentive, neutral | 10+ minutes | Aristotelian |
| Sales / fundraising / call to action | Passive, needs convincing | 5–15 minutes | Monroe's Motivated Sequence |
| Email / memo / one-pager | Busy, scanning | 30–90 seconds to read | PAS |
| Difficult feedback / critique | Defensive | 3–10 minutes | Sandwich |
| Executive briefing / board update | Time-pressured, skeptical | 2–5 minutes | Pyramid |

## Mapping Templates to the Iron Law

Every template above uses all three appeals — they differ only in **ordering** and **weighting**:

| Template | Lead appeal | Supporting appeals | Audience rationale |
|----------|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Aristotelian | Ethos → Logos | Pathos at close | General: trust before evidence |
| Monroe | Pathos → Logos | Ethos via solution credibility | Action-oriented: emotion drives behavior |
| PAS | Pathos (recognition) | Logos (agitate scale) + Ethos (solution) | Short-form: hook before reasoning |
| Sandwich | Ethos (good faith) | Logos (core ask) + Pathos (shared future) | Defensive audience: lower threat level first |
| Pyramid | Logos (conclusion) | Logos (evidence) + Ethos (methodology) | Executive: respects their time = Ethos |

**No template suspends the Iron Law.** Even the Pyramid — which appears all-Logos — relies on Ethos (the speaker's credibility that makes the conclusion worth hearing) and Pathos (the implied vision of what success looks like). If you find yourself writing a section with only one appeal, that section needs revision.

## Common Structural Mistakes

**Mistake 1: No explicit Refutatio**
Skipping counterarguments makes you look like you haven't considered them. Audiences generate objections mentally while listening; if you don't address them, those objections dominate their attention. Include at least one "Some might argue... however..." moment in any speech over 5 minutes.

**Mistake 2: Call to action buried or vague**
"We hope you'll consider supporting this initiative" is not a call to action. Every persuasive communication ends with a specific, actionable ask: "Vote yes on resolution 14B today" / "Reply to this email with your approval by Friday" / "Sign up at the link in the chat."

**Mistake 3: Visualization skipped in Monroe**
The jump from Satisfaction → Action leaves audiences intellectually convinced but emotionally flat. Visualization takes 60 seconds and dramatically increases follow-through. Never skip it.

**Mistake 4: Using Pyramid for emotional audiences**
Leading with the conclusion requires the audience to trust you enough to hear it without context. If the audience is hostile, grieving, defensive, or in crisis, Aristotelian or Sandwich is safer — they need the relational groundwork before they can process your recommendation.

**Mistake 5: PAS Agitate section using only one type of pain**
Effective Agitate stacks pain types: financial cost + emotional cost + opportunity cost. Using only data feels cold; using only emotion feels alarmist. A 2-3 sentence Agitate typically hits one Logos element (scale/cost) and one Pathos element (human consequence).
