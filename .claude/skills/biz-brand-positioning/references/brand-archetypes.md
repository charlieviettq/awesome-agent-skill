# Brand Archetypes

Archetypes are not decoration — they are a compression mechanism. A well-chosen archetype encodes a consistent worldview, emotional register, and narrative logic that guides every brand expression: copy tone, visual language, product experience, and customer relationship model.

The 12 archetypes originate from Carl Jung's concept of universal psychological patterns. Carol S. Pearson and Margaret Mark operationalized them for branding in *The Hero and the Outlaw* (2001). The core claim: brands that cluster signals around one recognizable archetype are processed more easily and remembered longer than brands that mix signals arbitrarily.

---

## The 12 Archetypes — Structured Reference

Each archetype is defined by its core motivation, shadow (failure mode), and the brand promise it implicitly makes.

| Archetype | Core Motivation | Brand Promise | Shadow |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|--------|
| **Innocent** | Safety, simplicity | "We keep life simple and good" | Naïve, boring, avoids hard truths |
| **Everyman** | Belonging, connection | "We're just like you" | Mediocre, generic, invisible |
| **Hero** | Mastery, achievement | "We help you overcome" | Arrogant, exhausting, elitist |
| **Outlaw** | Liberation, disruption | "Rules are made to be broken" | Nihilistic, gratuitously shocking |
| **Explorer** | Freedom, discovery | "Life is an adventure" | Rootless, unreliable, restless |
| **Creator** | Innovation, expression | "We help you make something meaningful" | Perfectionist, impractical |
| **Ruler** | Control, order | "We help you be in command" | Authoritarian, rigid, out of touch |
| **Magician** | Transformation | "We make the impossible happen" | Manipulative, promises too much |
| **Lover** | Intimacy, pleasure | "We make you feel desired" | Superficial, people-pleasing |
| **Jester** | Play, enjoyment | "Life's too short to be serious" | Disrespectful, frivolous |
| **Sage** | Knowledge, truth | "We help you understand" | Pedantic, paralyzed by analysis |
| **Caregiver** | Service, protection | "We take care of you" | Martyrdom, enabling dependency |

## Archetype Groups (Structural Clusters)

The 12 archetypes cluster into four groups by primary human need. This grouping helps diagnose which cluster fits a category before picking the specific archetype.

```
BELONGING / CONNECTION
  Everyman · Jester · Lover

EGO / ACHIEVEMENT
  Hero · Outlaw · Magician

ORDER / STRUCTURE
  Innocent · Ruler · Caregiver

GROWTH / INDEPENDENCE
  Explorer · Creator · Sage
```

**Selection heuristic**: Start with the cluster that matches what your target segment most needs from the category, then pick the specific archetype that fits your brand's actual delivery mechanism.

---

## Selection Decision Tree

```
1. What is the primary emotional job the brand does for the customer?

   → Help me achieve / overcome something        → EGO cluster
   → Help me belong / connect / feel good        → BELONGING cluster
   → Give me safety / stability / order          → ORDER cluster
   → Help me grow / discover / understand        → GROWTH cluster

2. Within the cluster, what is the brand's relationship with authority/convention?

   EGO cluster:
   → Conforms to high standards, inspires        → Hero
   → Challenges the system, rebels               → Outlaw
   → Works behind the scenes, transforms         → Magician

   BELONGING cluster:
   → Connects through shared human experience    → Everyman
   → Connects through play and laughter          → Jester
   → Connects through intimacy and desire        → Lover

   ORDER cluster:
   → Provides moral/simple goodness              → Innocent
   → Provides protection, nurturing              → Caregiver
   → Provides control and premium authority      → Ruler

   GROWTH cluster:
   → Grows through adventure and movement        → Explorer
   → Grows through making / building             → Creator
   → Grows through knowledge and wisdom          → Sage

3. Is this archetype defensible?
   → Can we deliver the brand promise this archetype implies?
   → Does a competitor already own this archetype in our category?
   If Yes to competitor ownership → pick a different archetype OR a different category frame
```

## Worked Example: Taiwanese Craft Beer (from parent SKILL.md)

The parent skill assigned 島嶼啤酒 the **Explorer** archetype. Here is the full archetype derivation:

**Step 1 — Cluster selection**

The customer's job: "I want a drinking experience that feels different and authentic to somewhere real." This is about discovery and growth, not achievement or belonging primarily → **GROWTH cluster**.

**Step 2 — Specific archetype**

Within GROWTH:
- Creator would mean the brand helps customers make something → doesn't fit beer
- Sage would mean knowledge / education → too cerebral for craft beer
- Explorer means adventure through discovery of new ingredients and places → fits Taiwan-sourced botanicals narrative

→ **Explorer** confirmed.

**Step 3 — Archetype coherence check**

| Brand signal | Explorer-consistent? |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Local ingredients (longan honey, oolong tea) | ✓ — discovery of specific places |
| Small batch brewing | ✓ — artisanal, not mass produced |
| "Taipei brewery" specificity | ✓ — grounds the exploration in a real place |
| If they ran a generic "premium quality" tagline | ✗ — that's Ruler/Competence, conflicts |

**Step 4 — Competitor archetype audit**

| Competitor | Archetype | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| 台灣啤酒 | Everyman | Mass market, belonging, affordable |
| Kirin / Asahi | Ruler | Premium, order, Japanese precision |
| Jim & Dad's | Explorer | Risk: same archetype — differentiate via Taiwan-local angle |

Jim & Dad's is the closest conflict. Resolution: 島嶼啤酒 should emphasize the **Taiwan-sourced** specificity more than general "craft exploration" — that's the white space.

## Archetype × Tone of Voice Matrix

Concrete writing register for each archetype. Use this to audit actual copy.

| Archetype | Sentence structure | Vocabulary register | What to avoid |
|-----------|--------------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Innocent | Short, warm, declarative | Simple words, positive framing | Irony, complexity, negativity |
| Everyman | Conversational, inclusive "we/you" | Colloquial, unpretentious | Jargon, exclusivity signals |
| Hero | Active voice, strong verbs | Achievement, challenge, overcome | Passive, self-pity, uncertainty |
| Outlaw | Provocative, short punches | Edge, subvert, reject | Corporate euphemism, hedging |
| Explorer | Open-ended, sensory | Discover, journey, horizon | Closure, definitive, "one true way" |
| Creator | Precise, detailed | Craft, build, design, make | Vagueness, shortcuts |
| Ruler | Authoritative, formal | Lead, command, excellence, legacy | Informality, casualness |
| Magician | Evocative, transformational | Transform, possibility, envision | Literal specs, boring details |
| Lover | Intimate, second-person | Desire, beauty, feel, you | Distance, clinical, transactional |
| Jester | Playful, unexpected | Wit, absurdity, wordplay | Earnest sincerity, self-importance |
| Sage | Measured, evidence-based | Understand, research, insight, because | Hype, unsupported claims |
| Caregiver | Warm, empathetic, inclusive | Care, support, protect, together | Selfishness, exclusion signals |

## Common Archetype Mistakes

**Mixing incompatible archetypes**

Hero + Caregiver = achiever who also nurtures → psychologically incoherent. Customers cannot hold both. Exception: sub-brands can hold different archetypes (Nike main brand = Hero; Nike Foundation CSR arm can be Caregiver).

**Aspirational archetype ≠ operational archetype**

A startup that wants to be Magician (transformation) but delivers Everyman (we're just like you, budget-friendly) has a brand identity crisis. The archetype must match what the product *actually delivers*, not what leadership *wishes* they delivered.

**Category mismatch**

Some archetypes rarely work in certain categories:
- Outlaw for financial services → destroys trust (exception: challenger banks explicitly disrupting the category, e.g., early Revolut)
- Innocent for cybersecurity → implies naïveté about threat landscape
- Ruler for youth fashion → alienates the target segment

**Competitor mirroring**

If the dominant brand in your category owns Hero, and you position as Hero too, you're not differentiating — you're a cheaper Hero. Map competitor archetypes explicitly before selecting your own.

## Archetype Consistency Audit Checklist

Run this when reviewing any brand asset (website, ad, packaging, social post):

```
□ Does the copy register match the archetype's tone of voice? (see matrix above)
□ Do the visual cues (imagery style, color palette, typography weight) signal the same archetype?
□ Does the customer experience at key touchpoints reinforce the archetype?
□ Would a competitor's name fit this asset without changing it? (If yes → insufficient differentiation)
□ Does the asset contradict the archetype? (A Jester brand running a solemn grief-narrative ad?)
```

If more than one box fails: the asset needs revision before publication. Brand personality incoherence accumulates — one-off inconsistencies are fine; systematic inconsistency erodes the archetype signal over years.

## Aaker Brand Personality ↔ Archetype Mapping

The parent SKILL.md uses both Aaker dimensions and archetypes. They are not the same system. This table maps between them to prevent confusion.

| Aaker Dimension | Typically maps to archetypes |
|----------------|------------------------------|
| Sincerity (honest, wholesome) | Innocent, Caregiver, Everyman |
| Excitement (daring, imaginative) | Explorer, Outlaw, Magician |
| Competence (reliable, intelligent) | Sage, Hero, Ruler |
| Sophistication (elegant, upper-class) | Ruler, Lover |
| Ruggedness (tough, outdoorsy) | Explorer, Hero, Outlaw |

**How to use both systems together**: Use Aaker dimensions to select *traits* (3-5 adjectives), then confirm the dominant archetype is consistent with those traits. If your traits are "reliable, intelligent, successful" (all Competence), your archetype should be Sage, Hero, or Ruler — not Jester.

The 島嶼啤酒 example: Excitement (imaginative, spirited) + Sincerity (wholesome) → maps cleanly to Explorer. An Outlaw would be Excitement only, without the Sincerity layer. The Sincerity component is what distinguishes Explorer (authentic discovery) from Outlaw (transgressive rebellion).
