# Reputation Recovery After a PR Crisis

Reputation recovery is not a communications campaign — it is a sequence of credible actions that earn back trust over time. The asymmetry is brutal: trust accumulates slowly and collapses fast. Recovery timelines run 3–18 months depending on crisis severity and response quality.

---

## Recovery Readiness Gate

Before beginning active recovery, confirm the crisis is actually over. Starting a "we're back" narrative while the fire still burns destroys credibility permanently.

**Gate Checklist** — all must be TRUE before Phase 2 begins:

| Gate Item | Check |
|-----------|-------|
| Root cause publicly acknowledged | ☐ |
| Corrective actions implemented (not just promised) | ☐ |
| No active media investigations ongoing | ☐ |
| Affected parties compensated / made whole | ☐ |
| Internal process fixed and documented | ☐ |
| No new incidents in the past 14 days | ☐ |

If any item is ☐, stay in crisis management mode. Premature recovery signals spin, not substance.

## Recovery Timeline Model

### Phase 1: Stabilization (Days 1–30)

Goal: Stop the bleeding. Not recovery yet — just halt deterioration.

Actions:
- Continue publishing factual updates as corrective actions complete
- Respond to every media inquiry within 4 hours (consistent message only)
- Brief key stakeholders privately before any public announcement: investors, enterprise clients, regulators
- Suppress no criticism; do not engage in argument with critics

Metric: **Sentiment Velocity** — rate of new negative mentions per day should be declining, not the absolute count. A plateau is acceptable; an increase means the crisis is not over.

### Phase 2: Proof (Days 31–90)

Goal: Demonstrate change through actions, not words.

The "Proof Framework" — each corrective action needs a corresponding public signal:

```
Action → Evidence → Channel

Fired responsible executive → "Leadership accountability" statement → Press release
Refunded affected customers → Customer DMs + aggregate numbers → Twitter / PTT
New safety process implemented → Third-party audit certificate → Website + press
Policy changed → Published new policy document with diff → Blog post
```

Do NOT say "we have made changes." Say "here is the change, here is the evidence."

### Phase 3: Rehabilitation (Days 91–180)

Goal: Re-establish domain credibility.

Tactics ranked by credibility signal strength:

| Tactic | Credibility Signal | Reach |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| Third-party audit / certification published | Very High | Low |
| Earned media coverage of corrective actions | High | Medium |
| Key stakeholder endorsements (partners, customers) | High | Medium |
| Industry award or recognition | Medium | Low |
| Owned content (blogs, social posts) | Low | High |

Common mistake: over-investing in owned content (easy to produce, low credibility) while neglecting third-party signals (hard to obtain, high credibility).

### Phase 4: Normalization (Days 181–365+)

Goal: Shift the primary narrative from "the company that had [crisis]" to "the company that [value proposition]."

This happens organically when Phase 3 produces enough positive signal. You cannot force-normalize — attempting to do so via aggressive positive PR while critics remain unaddressed produces a "reputation whitewashing" counter-narrative.

## Sentiment Tracking Protocol

Track four dimensions weekly, not just overall sentiment:

```
1. Volume: total mentions (news + social + forums)
2. Sentiment Ratio: positive / (positive + negative) — target: return to pre-crisis baseline
3. Reach-Weighted Negativity: negative mentions × estimated reach — tracks whether critics are amplified
4. Narrative Theme: what % of mentions reference the crisis vs. other topics
```

**Narrative Theme** is the best leading indicator of normalization. When crisis-related mentions drop below 15% of total mention volume, Phase 4 is working.

Practical tool for Taiwan market: monitor PTT (`/search?q=brand+name`), Dcard, and ETtoday comment sections daily during Phase 1–2, weekly during Phase 3–4.

## Key Stakeholder Re-Engagement Sequence

Re-engage stakeholders in this order. Getting it wrong alienates the group you most need:

```
1. Directly affected parties (victims, customers harmed)
   → Private, before any public statement
   → Offer: concrete remedy, not just apology

2. Employees
   → Internal briefing before external announcement
   → They are your reputation ambassadors; they need the full story

3. Investors / Board
   → Simultaneous with or before public communications
   → Focus: financial impact quantified, recovery plan with milestones

4. Key enterprise clients / partners
   → Personal outreach from senior leadership (not mass email)
   → Specific to their relationship: "here is how this affects you"

5. Industry / regulators
   → Proactive briefing if regulatory exposure exists
   → Demonstrates good faith; reduces enforcement risk

6. General public / media
   → Last — only after internal and high-value stakeholders are briefed
```

Sending a press release before affected customers receive a personal apology is a Phase 2 failure pattern that generates a second wave of negative coverage.

## Taiwan-Specific Recovery Playbook

### PTT / Dcard Dynamics

PTT hot posts age out of the front page in 3–7 days but remain searchable permanently. The recovery goal is not erasure — it is dilution: ensure that searching the brand name returns proportionally more positive/neutral content over time.

Do not attempt to suppress PTT threads. Downvote manipulation is detectable and generates a meta-scandal. The only effective PTT strategy is:
1. Have a company representative post an official response in the original thread (disclose affiliation)
2. Ensure positive actions generate new threads organically (customers sharing refund experiences, etc.)

### Media Cycle (PTT → TV News Pipeline)

Taiwan's news cycle is fast and recycling-heavy. The same story re-surfaces when:
- A slow news day occurs (Friday afternoons, holiday weekends)
- A similar incident at another company recalls the original
- An anniversary date (3-month, 6-month follow-up stories)

Prepare a "year-later" narrative in advance: what has changed, in numbers, by Month 6. Proactively pitch this to journalists who covered the original story. They often prefer to write the "redemption arc" if handed the data.

### Public Apology Format

A formal public apology in Taiwan context must include:

```
1. Named acknowledgment of the specific harm caused
   (NOT: "if anyone was affected"; YES: "X customers experienced Y")

2. Unambiguous acceptance of responsibility
   (NOT: "mistakes were made"; YES: "we failed to [specific thing]")

3. Specific corrective actions with named owners and deadlines
   (NOT: "we are reviewing our processes"; YES: "VP of Operations [name]
   will complete [action] by [date]")

4. Mechanism for ongoing accountability
   (NOT: "we are committed to improvement"; YES: "we will publish a
   60-day update on [date] at [URL]")
```

Executive video apologies perform better in Taiwan than written statements for Level 3 crises. A 90-second, direct-to-camera video from the CEO, without production gloss, signals sincerity. Over-produced apology videos read as spin.

## Recovery Anti-Patterns

**1. The Premature Pivot**
Launching positive brand campaigns before corrective actions are complete. Critics will contrast the campaign spend with unresolved victim compensation. This is a second-order crisis.

**2. Apology Inflation**
Issuing a second, "stronger" apology when the first is criticized. Each successive apology devalues the previous and signals that the original was insincere. Get the apology right once.

**3. Whack-a-Mole Engagement**
Responding to every critical social media comment during Phase 1 consumes resources and amplifies critics by boosting post engagement. Select: respond to high-reach accounts, ignore low-reach. Never argue.

**4. Reputation Laundering via CSR**
Announcing a large charitable donation immediately after a crisis is widely read as buying goodwill. If you already had CSR programs, continue them. If you launch new ones, wait until Phase 3 and do not connect them explicitly to the crisis.

**5. Treating Recovery as a Sprint**
Assigning a "recovery campaign" with a 6-week timeline and then declaring victory. Reputation recovery is a regime, not a project. It ends when audience behavior returns to baseline, not when the team's calendar says so.

## Measuring Recovery Completion

Recovery is complete when three conditions hold simultaneously for 60+ consecutive days:

```
Condition 1: Sentiment Ratio ≥ pre-crisis baseline (±5%)
Condition 2: Crisis Narrative Theme < 10% of total mentions
Condition 3: No ongoing media investigations or regulatory proceedings
```

If Condition 1 recovers but Condition 2 does not, the brand is liked again but still defined by the crisis. This is a partial recovery — the next incident will immediately resurface the original crisis in media coverage.

Post-crisis, run an annual brand audit that explicitly includes the crisis in the historical context. This prevents the organization from declaring recovery prematurely and helps calibrate future crisis preparedness.
