# Six Paths Framework

The Six Paths framework is a structured search method for finding Blue Ocean opportunities when the Four Actions brainstorm stalls. It works by systematically looking across boundaries that the industry takes for granted — boundaries that, once crossed, reveal new demand and new value curves.

**When to use**: After drawing the current Strategy Canvas and finding that the team cannot identify meaningful Eliminate or Create candidates. The Six Paths force-generate inputs for the Four Actions Framework.

---

## The Six Paths at a Glance

| Path | The boundary crossed | Core question |
|------|----------------------|---------------|
| 1. Alternative Industries | Industry definition | What do buyers sacrifice by NOT using alternatives outside our industry? |
| 2. Strategic Groups | Segment definition | What do buyers sacrifice by trading up or down within our industry? |
| 3. Buyer Groups | Who is the "customer" | Are we optimizing for the wrong buyer in the purchase chain? |
| 4. Complementary Offerings | Product scope | What happens before, during, and after use of our product — and is that painful? |
| 5. Functional-Emotional Appeal | Value type | Is our industry over-investing in functional OR emotional value at the expense of the other? |
| 6. Time | When to look | What trend is already in motion and irreversible, and what does it demand from buyers? |

---

## Path 1 — Alternative Industries

### Mechanism

Buyers often choose between industries, not just between competitors within one industry. When they choose an alternative, they gain something and lose something. The loses they accept are latent demand your product could serve.

### Procedure

1. List 3–5 alternative industries buyers use to fulfill the **same functional job** (not the same product category).
2. For each alternative, identify what buyers **gain** and what they **sacrifice**.
3. Identify which sacrifices are largest — these are candidates for your **Create** or **Raise** actions.
4. Identify which gains from your industry the buyer barely cares about — candidates for **Eliminate** or **Reduce**.

### Worked Example — Business Travel Software

**Current industry**: B2B travel booking software. Competitors all race on price negotiation, hotel inventory breadth, and travel policy compliance features.

| Alternative industry | What buyers gain | What buyers sacrifice |
|---------------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Corporate credit cards with travel perks | Simplicity, single bill | No policy compliance, no reporting |
| Personal use of consumer booking apps (Booking.com, Google Flights) | Speed, familiar UI, real-time pricing | No corporate rates, reconciliation nightmare |
| Travel management agencies (TMAs) | Full-service, duty of care | High cost, slow, phone-dependent |

**Insight from Path 1:**
- Buyers who default to personal apps sacrifice reconciliation and compliance — that pain is large.
- Buyers who use TMAs sacrifice speed — they accept a phone call to do what should take 2 minutes.
- Buyers on corporate cards sacrifice reporting — finance teams hate the month-end scramble.

**Four Actions inputs generated:**
- **Create**: Instant receipt capture and auto-reconciliation (nobody in the software industry makes this frictionless)
- **Reduce**: Hotel inventory breadth (buyers using personal apps already have Booking.com; don't replicate it)
- **Eliminate**: Deep policy customization modules (TMC clients need it; self-service SMB clients don't — two separate markets)

## Path 2 — Strategic Groups

### Mechanism

Within an industry, strategic groups are clusters of companies competing on similar strategy (e.g., budget airlines vs. full-service airlines). Buyers often move between groups when the price-value trade-off shifts. The gap between groups — what buyers accept and what they wish they had — is where Blue Ocean lives.

### Procedure

1. Draw the industry's strategic groups on a 2×2: **price** (X) vs. **perceived prestige / feature richness** (Y).
2. Identify the 2–3 dominant groups.
3. For each group, ask: "What does a buyer in Group A want from Group B that Group B won't give them affordably? And vice versa?"
4. The answer points to factors that no group currently offers together — candidates for your new value curve.

### Worked Example — Online Education

Strategic groups in online education:

```
High price / High prestige: MBA programs ($50–150k, 2 years, credential)
Mid price / Mid structure:  Bootcamps ($10–20k, 3–6 months, portfolio)
Low price / Low structure:  MOOCs ($0–50/month, self-paced, no credential)
```

| What Group A buyers want from other groups |
|--------------------------------------------|
| MBA buyers want MOOC **speed** and **flexibility** — they don't want 2 years |
| MOOC buyers want bootcamp **accountability** — self-paced often means abandoned |
| Bootcamp buyers want MBA **credential signal** — hiring managers still gatekeep |

**Insight from Path 2:**
No group offers: structured cohort accountability + industry-recognized credential signal + sub-12-month timeline + sub-$5k cost.

**Four Actions inputs generated:**
- **Create**: Employer-backed credential (hire-or-refund model signals outcome quality without requiring accreditation)
- **Raise**: Cohort accountability (weekly live sessions, peer projects — higher than MOOCs)
- **Reduce**: Curriculum breadth (bootcamp-narrow focus beats MBA generalism for career switchers)
- **Eliminate**: On-campus infrastructure cost (passes savings to learner)

## Path 3 — Buyer Groups

### Mechanism

Most industries optimize their product for one buyer — but the purchase chain typically includes a **purchaser** (who pays), a **user** (who operates), and an **influencer** (who recommends). Shifting focus to a different buyer in the chain often reveals an entirely different set of factors to compete on.

### The Buyer Chain

```
Influencer → Purchaser → User
```

Common mismatches:

| Industry | Who the industry optimizes for | Who is actually underserved |
|----------|-------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Enterprise software | IT purchaser (security, integration) | End user (daily usability) |
| Children's toys | Child (fun, novelty) | Parent purchaser (safety, educational value, price) |
| Hospital equipment | Hospital procurement (cost, maintenance) | Nurse user (ergonomics, speed) |
| B2B SaaS tools | CFO/CTO approver (ROI, compliance) | Ops team user (time saved, simplicity) |

### Procedure

1. Map your current product's feature set against the three buyer groups. Rate each feature: does it primarily serve the influencer, purchaser, or user?
2. Identify which buyer is most underserved.
3. Redesign the value curve around that buyer's top 3 priorities.

### Decision Test

Ask: "If we redesigned entirely for the **user** instead of the **purchaser**, what would we eliminate and what would we create?" Then ask the same question swapping in each buyer group. The answer that produces the most divergent value curve is your Blue Ocean candidate.

## Path 4 — Complementary Offerings

### Mechanism

Products don't exist in isolation. Buyers experience a total solution context: what they do **before** acquiring the product, **during** use, and **after** (support, disposal, renewal). The industry typically optimizes the core product and ignores the context — which is where friction accumulates.

### The Utility Map (simplified)

Map buyer experience across three stages × six utility levers:

| Utility lever | Before purchase | During use | After use |
|---------------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| Customer productivity | | | |
| Simplicity | | | |
| Convenience | | | |
| Risk reduction | | | |
| Fun / Image | | | |
| Environmental friendliness | | | |

Mark each cell: **high pain** (🔴), **medium pain** (🟡), **low pain** (🟢). Clusters of 🔴 outside the core product are Blue Ocean candidates.

### Worked Example — Power Tools (Consumer)

The power tool industry competes hard on **during use**: torque, battery life, ergonomics, speed. But:

| Stage | Dominant pain |
|-------|--------------|
| **Before** | "Which bit do I need? I always buy the wrong one." Storage chaos. Incompatibility between brands. |
| **During** | Already well-served by the industry |
| **After** | "My old tool broke 6 months after warranty. Repair is impossible — I have to buy a new one." |

**Four Actions inputs generated:**
- **Create**: Modular bit system sold as a starter kit with the tool (eliminate the "which bit" decision)
- **Create**: 3-year repair guarantee with local repair network (after-use pain; no competitor offers this)
- **Raise**: Cross-model battery compatibility (before-use convenience; brand-locked batteries frustrate buyers who own multiple tools)
- **Eliminate**: High-end torque specifications in consumer line (enthusiast specs that DIY buyers don't understand or need)

## Path 5 — Functional vs. Emotional Appeal

### Mechanism

Industries tend to cluster around one type of value over time:

- **Functionally-oriented industries** compete on price and performance — they have stripped away emotion and experience (e.g., commodity chemicals, generic software tools).
- **Emotionally-oriented industries** compete on feeling and status — they have added emotional layers on top of functional commodities (e.g., luxury watches, premium coffee).

Blue Ocean moves in both directions:

- **Strip emotion from an emotional industry** → lower cost, more rational buyers, broader market
- **Add emotion to a functional industry** → create attachment, justify premium, expand non-buyers

### Decision Table

| Current industry orientation | Blue Ocean direction | Prototype example |
|-----------------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Heavily emotional | Add functional rigor | Swatch: stripped luxury out of watches → fashion accessory with reliable Swiss movement at low price |
| Heavily functional | Add emotional resonance | The Body Shop: added ethical sourcing narrative to commodity cosmetics |
| Mixed | Check which emotion/function axis buyers actually care about vs. which the industry over-invests in | — |

### Test

Rate your industry's current competitive factors:
- **F** = primarily functional (specification, performance, price)
- **E** = primarily emotional (brand, experience, status, community)

If >70% of factors are F: ask what emotional layer would convert non-buyers.
If >70% of factors are E: ask which emotional layers buyers secretly don't care about and would trade for a lower price.

## Path 6 — Time

### Mechanism

External trends change what buyers value. The Blue Ocean move is to extrapolate an already-irreversible trend to its logical conclusion — and redesign the value curve for where the market **will be**, not where it is today.

### Three Criteria for a Valid Trend

A trend qualifies for Path 6 analysis only if it meets all three:

1. **Decisive** — will clearly reshape industry (not speculative)
2. **Irreversible** — cannot be reversed by market or regulatory forces in your planning horizon
3. **Clear trajectory** — you can describe what the world looks like when the trend is 80% penetrated

Trends that fail any criterion are too speculative to build a value curve on.

### Procedure

1. Identify 2–3 trends meeting all three criteria.
2. For each trend: describe the "trend fully realized" state in one paragraph.
3. Ask: "In that state, which of our current competitive factors become irrelevant? Which buyer problems get worse? Which new problems emerge?"
4. Map answers to Four Actions.

### Worked Example — Physical Retail

**Trend**: Digital-first purchasing behavior (online research before any in-store purchase). Decisive, irreversible, clear trajectory.

**"Trend fully realized" state**: Buyers arrive in-store having already researched product specifications online. They know the price, the reviews, and the alternatives. They come to the store to touch, confirm, or pick up — not to be educated.

| Current factor | Fate in "trend realized" world |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Product knowledge staff | **Eliminate** — buyer already knows more than most sales staff |
| Wide product selection on floor | **Reduce** — buyer selects online; store is for confirmation + pickup |
| Long store hours | **Reduce** — buyers pick up on a schedule, not browse randomly |
| Same-day availability | **Raise** — buy online, pick up in 2 hours beats 2-day shipping |
| In-store experience (tactile, social) | **Raise** — the one thing the website cannot replicate |
| Return process | **Create** — make returns instant and no-questions (reverses the biggest online shopping anxiety) |

## Applying Six Paths: Decision Flow

```
START: Team cannot identify Blue Ocean factors from Four Actions alone
         │
         ▼
Run Path 3 first (Buyer Groups) — fastest to execute, often most surprising
         │
    Found strong signal?
    ├─ YES → Generate Four Actions inputs, test value curve divergence
    └─ NO ↓
         ▼
Run Path 4 (Complementary Offerings) — maps the full buyer experience
         │
    Found 🔴 pain outside core product?
    ├─ YES → Generate Four Actions inputs
    └─ NO ↓
         ▼
Run Path 1 (Alternative Industries) + Path 2 (Strategic Groups) in parallel
— both target demand that exists but goes to other options
         │
    Either found signal?
    ├─ YES → Generate Four Actions inputs
    └─ NO ↓
         ▼
Run Path 5 (Functional/Emotional) — reframe the value type, not the features
         │
Run Path 6 (Time) last — requires trend certainty; don't anchor on speculation
```

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing Path 1 with competitive analysis.** Path 1 looks at industries buyers use as *substitutes for the same job*, not at competitors. Netflix's Path 1 alternatives include sleep, gaming, and social media — not other streaming services.

**Treating Path 6 as futurism.** If the trend doesn't meet all three criteria (decisive, irreversible, clear trajectory), Path 6 produces speculative value curves that fail the Three Characteristics test (focus, divergence, compelling tagline).

**Running all six paths and merging them.** Each path produces its own Four Actions hypothesis. Merge only after testing each hypothesis against the Three Characteristics. Mixing signals from all six paths usually produces a cluttered value curve that converges back toward the industry average.

**Using Path 3 to justify serving everyone.** Shifting to a different buyer group means *stopping* optimization for the current buyer group. If you try to satisfy the purchaser and the user simultaneously, you typically end up with a Kitchen Sink strategy — not Blue Ocean.
