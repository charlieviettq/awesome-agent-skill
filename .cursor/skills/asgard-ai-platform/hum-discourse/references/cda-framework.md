# Fairclough's Critical Discourse Analysis Framework

Norman Fairclough's CDA treats every communicative event as simultaneously a **text**, a **discourse practice**, and a **social practice**. The three dimensions are not sequential steps — they are three simultaneous lenses applied to the same object.

---

## The Three-Dimensional Model

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              SOCIAL PRACTICE                │  ← Explanation: Why does this
│   (ideology, hegemony, institutional order) │    discourse exist? What does it
│  ┌───────────────────────────────────────┐  │    reproduce or challenge?
│  │         DISCOURSE PRACTICE           │  │
│  │  (production, distribution,          │  │  ← Interpretation: How is this
│  │   consumption of the text)           │  │    text produced and consumed?
│  │  ┌─────────────────────────────┐     │  │
│  │  │           TEXT              │     │  │  ← Description: What does the
│  │  │  (linguistic features)      │     │  │    text actually say/do?
│  │  └─────────────────────────────┘     │  │
│  └───────────────────────────────────────┘  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Fairclough's key claim: **discourse is dialectical**. Social structures shape discourse, but discourse also constitutes and transforms social structures. Neither side determines the other fully.

## Dimension 1 — Text (Description)

Linguistic analysis of the artifact itself. Fairclough draws from Systemic Functional Linguistics (Halliday): every utterance performs three simultaneous functions.

| Metafunction | Question it answers | What to look for |
|---|---|---|
| **Ideational** | What is represented as real? | Transitivity, nominalization, lexical choices |
| **Interpersonal** | What relationship is enacted between participants? | Modality, pronouns, speech acts |
| **Textual** | How is the message organized? | Theme/rheme, cohesion, information flow |

### 1a. Transitivity Analysis

Transitivity maps **who does what to whom** at the grammatical level. The key question: is an action represented as having an agent?

**Types of processes** (after Halliday):

| Process type | Example | Effect when used |
|---|---|---|
| Material | "Police dispersed protesters" | Action, causation, agency visible |
| Mental | "Management feels confident" | Interior state, less accountable |
| Relational | "The merger is inevitable" | State of being — naturalizes, hides agency |
| Verbal | "Experts say…" | Attributed claim, distancing |

**Nominalization** converts a process into a noun, erasing agency entirely:
- "The company decided to cut 500 jobs" → "The decision to restructure"
- Agent: visible → erased
- Process: dynamic → frozen noun
- Accountability: present → absent

When you see heavy nominalization, ask: *who is the erased subject?*

### 1b. Modality

Modality expresses the speaker's commitment to a proposition. Scale from high to low epistemic commitment:

```
High: "This will destroy communities."
      "This must be addressed."
Mid:  "This could have serious consequences."
      "This may affect local workers."
Low:  "Some have suggested this might be a concern."
```

**Why it matters for power analysis**: Powerful actors tend to use high modality for claims that favor them ("growth will follow") and low modality for claims that constrain them ("some impacts may be possible"). Note the asymmetry, not just the absolute value.

### 1c. Lexical Choices and Semantic Fields

Map the vocabulary to its semantic field, then identify what alternative fields were available.

**Procedure**:
1. Identify the text's key noun phrases and verbs
2. For each, list 2–3 alternatives at the same register level
3. Note what the chosen word **presupposes**, **excludes**, or **connotates**

| Chosen word | Presupposes | Excluded alternative | Excluded presupposition |
|---|---|---|---|
| "right-sizing" | current size is wrong | "laying off 500 people" | human beings, not organizational geometry |
| "investment" (in military spending) | future return | "expenditure" | cost without guaranteed return |
| "pro-life" | the other position is anti-life | "anti-abortion" | a policy position, not a moral verdict |

## Dimension 2 — Discourse Practice (Interpretation)

This dimension examines how the text is **produced** and **consumed**, and how it relates to other texts (**intertextuality**).

### 2a. Intertextuality

Every text is woven from fragments of other texts. Fairclough distinguishes:

**Manifest intertextuality** — explicit reference to other texts:
- Direct quotation: `"As the CEO stated last quarter…"`
- Reported speech: `"Critics argue that…"`
- Allusion: invoking a prior document without naming it

**Constitutive intertextuality (interdiscursivity)** — drawing on conventions from other discourse types:
- A hospital mission statement that borrows the vocabulary of business strategy
- A political speech that uses religious cadences
- A terms-of-service document that borrows scientific neutrality

**Analytical question**: Which discourse orders is this text drawing from, and what does that borrowing accomplish? When a tech company's layoff notice uses military vocabulary ("strategic re-alignment," "forward position"), what does that genre-mixing legitimize?

### 2b. Production and Consumption Context

| Question | What to investigate |
|---|---|
| Who produced the text? | Individual, team, institution — and under what constraints? |
| What technology of production? | Press release vs tweet vs leaked memo have different affordances |
| Who is the intended audience? | Primary, secondary, overhearing audience |
| How will it be distributed? | Channel shapes what can be said |
| How will it be consumed? | Skimmed vs read carefully vs heard aloud |

**Example**: A corporate sustainability report is produced by a communications team under legal review, distributed to investors and regulators, consumed in 30-second scans. These production conditions explain the nominalization-heavy, low-modality language — not ideological conspiracy, but institutional constraint.

## Dimension 3 — Social Practice (Explanation)

The outermost layer: how does this discourse relate to **social structures**, **institutions**, and **power**? Fairclough draws here from Gramsci (hegemony) and Althusser (ideology).

### 3a. Hegemony and Consent

Hegemony (Gramsci): dominant groups maintain power not through force alone but through **winning consent** — making their worldview appear natural and universal.

Discourse is a site of hegemonic struggle. Questions to ask:

- What does this text **naturalize**? (Present as inevitable, obvious, or simply "how things are")
- What does it **marginalize**? (Treat as extreme, unrealistic, or unspeakable)
- What **subject positions** does it make available? (Who can speak legitimately? Who is positioned as "emotional," "radical," "uninformed"?)

### 3b. Orders of Discourse

An **order of discourse** is the totality of discursive practices in a social domain, and their relationships. Examples: the medical order of discourse, the legal order of discourse, neoliberal economic discourse.

When a text draws on an order of discourse, it imports that domain's authority structures and implicit assumptions.

**Diagnostic question**: What order of discourse does this text position itself within, and what does that positioning accomplish?

| Text type | Order of discourse invoked | What it imports |
|---|---|---|
| "Evidence-based policy" | Scientific discourse | Objectivity, expert authority, quantification as truth |
| "We're all in this together" | Family/community discourse | Solidarity norms that suppress conflict |
| "Market signals indicate…" | Economic discourse | Inevitability, depoliticization of choices |

## The Three-Stage Analysis in Practice

Apply the three dimensions iteratively, not linearly:

```
Step 1: DESCRIBE (Text)
  - Collect: transitivity patterns, modality, key lexical choices, nominalization
  - Collect: intertextual references
  - Do not interpret yet. Just catalog what is there.

Step 2: INTERPRET (Discourse Practice)
  - What genre is this? What production conditions shaped it?
  - What other texts/discourses does it draw on?
  - How would different audiences decode it differently?

Step 3: EXPLAIN (Social Practice)
  - What social relations does this discourse reproduce?
  - What is naturalized? What is silenced?
  - Who benefits from this particular configuration of meaning?

Step 4: REFLEXIVE CHECK
  - What discourse am I, as analyst, operating within?
  - What does my own analysis presuppose?
```

## Worked Example: Tech Layoff Announcement

**Source text** (composite, representative):
> "After careful reflection, we have made the difficult decision to right-size our workforce by approximately 8%. This was not a decision we took lightly. We remain deeply committed to our team members affected by this change and will provide comprehensive transition support. These adjustments position us to invest in the areas of highest strategic value and ensure our long-term competitiveness."

### Dimension 1 — Text

**Transitivity scan**:

| Clause | Process type | Agent | Patient |
|---|---|---|---|
| "we have made the difficult decision" | Mental/verbal | "we" (management) | the decision (not the workers) |
| "to right-size our workforce" | Material | [nominalized — no visible agent doing the right-sizing] | "our workforce" (abstracted) |
| "This was not a decision we took lightly" | Relational | — | repositions management as sufferer |
| "will provide comprehensive transition support" | Material | "we" | "team members affected" |
| "These adjustments position us" | Material | "adjustments" (not management) | "us" — company benefits |

**Nominalization count**: "right-size" (verb nominalized to a sizing operation), "adjustments" (hides the action of firing), "transition support" (hides the action of ending employment).

**Modality pattern**:
- High certainty for forward-looking claims: "position us," "ensure our long-term competitiveness"
- Absent: any modality markers around the *need* for the cuts — no "may," no acknowledgment it was a choice among alternatives

**Lexical choices**:

| Used | Presupposes | Available alternative | Excluded content |
|---|---|---|---|
| "right-size" | size was wrong (passive, not a management error) | "lay off 8% of staff" | human individuals |
| "team members affected by this change" | change = external agent, workers = passive receivers | "employees we are letting go" | management as actor |
| "careful reflection" | deliberation and wisdom preceded the decision | — | any acknowledgment of error, over-hiring, or external pressure |
| "strategic value" | some work has intrinsic value, some does not (but never specified) | — | transparency about criteria |

### Dimension 2 — Discourse Practice

**Genre**: Corporate press release / all-staff memo hybrid. Designed for two simultaneous audiences: employees (who must be managed emotionally) and investors/media (who must be reassured of strategic clarity). The dual audience explains the tonal contradiction: "deeply committed to our team members" (employee-facing) + "highest strategic value" (investor-facing).

**Interdiscursivity**: The text borrows from:
- Therapeutic discourse ("difficult," "not lightly," "deeply committed") — imports care and legitimacy
- Military-strategic discourse ("right-size," "strategic value," "position," "competitiveness") — imports inevitability and rationality
- The combination naturalizes the action: it is both caring AND strategically necessary, leaving no rhetorical space for objection.

**Production context**: Almost certainly drafted by communications/legal, reviewed by HR and general counsel. The hedged language, absent modality, and nominalization are signatures of legal review — designed to minimize grounds for wrongful termination claims.

### Dimension 3 — Social Practice

**What is naturalized**:
- That workforce size should track "strategic value" (a market logic, not presented as a choice)
- That management's role is to make "difficult decisions" (positions them as burden-bearers, not agents of harm)
- That the appropriate response to being fired is receiving "transition support" (a service relationship, not a power relation)

**What is silenced**:
- Why was the company over-staffed? (Prior decisions, VC pressure to grow at all costs)
- Who decided which 8% are "not strategic"? (Criteria, process, bias risks — entirely absent)
- The asymmetry in outcomes: executives who made the hiring decisions face no comparable "right-sizing"

**Hegemonic function**: The discourse invites employees to see layoffs as a natural, regrettable feature of the business environment — like weather — rather than as a political choice made by identifiable people with identifiable interests. This is precisely the "naturalization" move that CDA is designed to expose.

## Common Analyst Errors in CDA

**Over-determination**: Concluding that every word was strategically chosen to deceive. CDA should account for the mundane: genre conventions, legal constraints, and professional jargon are often more explanatory than conspiracy.

**Under-contextualizing**: Analyzing the text in isolation from its production conditions. The same sentence in a union contract vs a CEO memo has different meanings.

**Confirmation bias**: Beginning with a conclusion ("this text is oppressive") and marshaling evidence. CDA requires genuinely open textual description (Step 1) before interpretation.

**Ignoring the analyst's position**: Your choice to analyze *this* text, using *these* categories, from *this* position, is itself a discourse act. Reflexivity is not optional in CDA — it is methodologically required.
