# MSF in Comparative Politics: Adaptation Beyond the U.S. Federal Model

Kingdon's original framework (1984) was built on U.S. federal agenda-setting: fragmented authority, separated powers, a bicameral Congress, and strong interest group pluralism. Applying MSF outside this context requires explicit adjustments to how each stream operates and what constitutes a "policy window."

---

## System-Type Adjustment Table

| Political System | Problem Stream | Policy Stream | Politics Stream | Window Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **U.S.-style separation of powers** | Fragmented indicators; congressional hearings as focal points | Multiple competing proposals across branches | Elections, party turnover, public mood | Problem or political window, both routes viable |
| **Westminster parliamentary** | Executive dominates problem definition | Cabinet-controlled policy development; opposition proposals rarely enter primeval soup | Government change = immediate window; backbench revolts rare | Primarily political window (election, PM change) |
| **Consensus/coalition** | Multi-party committees define problems jointly | Coalition negotiations filter proposals before public agenda | Coalition formation and collapse as windows | Coalition formation window; requires multi-party coupling |
| **Semi-presidential** | Dual executive creates competing problem frames | Cohabitation splits policy stream between president and PM | Election timing and cohabitation dynamics | Cohabitation resolution as window |
| **Authoritarian/single-party** | Top leadership controls problem recognition; technocrats provide indicators | Policy proposals circulate within party-state apparatus | Factional shifts, leadership succession, external pressure | Leadership transition or external shock window |
| **Federal (non-U.S.)** | Sub-national governments can independently define problems | Policy solutions may originate in states/provinces ("laboratories") | Intergovernmental elections create asymmetric timing | Federal-state alignment window |

## Stream-by-Stream Adaptations

### Problem Stream

In Westminster systems, **ministers actively suppress** competing problem definitions. Indicators that do not align with government priorities are rarely amplified through official channels. The "focusing event" pathway remains viable — crises are harder to suppress — but feedback from existing programs is filtered through party-aligned bureaucracies.

**Practical implication**: In non-U.S. systems, look for problem stream activation through:
1. **Opposition spotlighting** — minority parties force problems onto the agenda via parliamentary questions, budget debates, or media coordination
2. **Cross-national diffusion** — a policy failure in a comparable country creates a focusing event by analogy ("This could happen here")
3. **Subnational experimentation failure** — a regional program fails visibly, elevating the problem to national attention

### Policy Stream

The "primeval soup" metaphor assumes a diffuse community of specialists — academics, think tanks, advocacy groups — who float proposals independently. This is primarily a pluralist, U.S.-centric assumption.

**In corporatist systems** (Austria, Germany, Scandinavia): the policy stream is semi-institutionalized. Peak associations (employer federations, trade unions) participate in formal consultation processes. Proposals that have not passed through this filter rarely survive. The primeval soup is more like a **managed menu**.

**In developmental states** (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore): the policy stream is concentrated in elite economic bureaucracies (Korea Development Institute, CEPD/NDRC equivalents). External think tanks and civil society play a limited proposal-generation role. Feasibility assessment is internally controlled.

**In authoritarian systems**: the policy stream exists within the party-state. Policy entrepreneurs are typically senior technocrats or think tanks affiliated with the ruling party. Proposals circulate in internal documents before any public agenda presence.

### Politics Stream

Kingdon identified three components: national mood, organized political forces, and government composition. In comparative context:

**National mood** is most relevant where public opinion influences policy (democracies with responsive electoral systems). In authoritarian systems, substitute: **elite consensus**, **factional balance**, or **regime legitimacy pressure**.

**Organized political forces**: In pluralist systems, this means competing interest groups with autonomous resources. In corporatist systems, fewer but more powerful actors with institutionalized access. In authoritarian systems, internal party factions replace interest groups as the primary organized force.

**Government composition**: In presidential systems, divided government can block stream coupling (the executive entrepreneur cannot close the loop without legislative partner). In parliamentary systems, majority governments remove this barrier; minority governments reintroduce it.

## Worked Example: Taiwan's 2019 Marriage Equality Legislation

This case illustrates MSF operating in a semi-presidential system with strong civil society but executive dominance of agenda-setting.

### Problem Stream
- **Indicator**: Constitutional Court Interpretation No. 748 (2017) declared existing marriage law unconstitutional; two-year legislative deadline created urgency
- **Focusing event**: The court ruling itself, amplified by the 2016 Tsai Ing-wen election (first female president, DPP majority)
- **Problem definition contest**: LGBT rights advocates framed it as a constitutional rights issue; conservative religious groups reframed it as family structure protection

### Policy Stream
- **Proposals in the primeval soup**:
  1. Full marriage equality (amending Civil Code)
  2. Special civil partnership law (separate but equal)
  3. Administrative registration only
- **Technical feasibility**: All three technically workable; legal drafts existed for each
- **Value compatibility filter**: The 2018 referenda (three anti-equality questions passed) revealed that full Civil Code amendment had low value compatibility with majority public sentiment
- **Surviving proposal**: Special Act path — technically workable, values-compatible with both court mandate and referendum outcome, fiscally neutral

### Politics Stream
- **National mood**: Split — urban/younger population favored equality; rural/older population opposed (referendum results confirmed this)
- **Organized forces**: LGBT rights coalition (strong street presence, international visibility) vs. conservative religious alliance (strong local mobilization, church networks)
- **Government composition**: DPP legislative majority, but internal dissent among rural DPP legislators who faced constituency pressure

### Coupling and Window

**Window type**: Problem window triggered by court deadline (legislative action required by May 24, 2019)

**Policy entrepreneur**: Executive Yuan (Cabinet) as institutional entrepreneur; Premier Su Tseng-chang's team drafted the Special Act bill after the 2018 referendum results foreclosed the Civil Code path

**Coupling mechanism**: The Special Act bill satisfied the court mandate (problem stream) through a technically different instrument (policy stream shift) while staying within the post-referendum political constraint (politics stream). The deadline prevented the window from closing without action.

**What was left out**: Full Civil Code marriage equality — not adopted until 2023 in a separate legislative process.

## Decision Framework: Which Stream Will Be the Bottleneck?

Use this to identify where to focus analytical effort when applying MSF to a new case.

```
1. Is there a hard deadline or crisis?
   YES → Problem stream is already activated. Focus on policy + politics streams.
   NO  → Ask: Is the condition recognized as a "problem" at all?
         NO → Problem stream is the bottleneck. Identify who must define it as a problem.

2. Are there technically feasible, value-compatible policy proposals already circulating?
   NO → Policy stream is underdeveloped. Who generates proposals in this system?
       [Pluralist: think tanks, advocacy groups]
       [Corporatist: peak associations, ministry working groups]
       [Developmental state: elite technocracy]
       [Authoritarian: party-affiliated research bodies]
   YES → Policy stream is ready. Focus elsewhere.

3. Is political will present?
   Check government composition: majority/minority? unified/divided?
   Check organized forces: is a blocking coalition present?
   Check national mood (democracies) or elite consensus (authoritarian):
       BLOCKING COALITION PRESENT → Politics stream is the bottleneck.
       GOVERNMENT FRAGMENTED → Window requires coalition formation first.
       ELITE CONSENSUS ABSENT → Window requires leadership change or external shock.
```

## MSF in Authoritarian Contexts: Specific Modifications

Zahariadis (2008) and subsequent comparative scholars have argued that MSF applies to authoritarian systems with three structural modifications:

1. **The politics stream becomes the "leadership stream"**: Public opinion and elections are replaced by elite bargaining, factional competition, and succession dynamics. The "national mood" variable can be re-read as **regime legitimacy pressure** — when the regime perceives a legitimacy threat (protest, international criticism, economic crisis), the politics stream opens.

2. **Policy entrepreneurs are inside the apparatus**: External advocacy groups may trigger problem stream activation but rarely couple streams. The coupling role belongs to internal champions — technocrats, faction leaders, or reformist officials with access to top leadership.

3. **Windows are less predictable from outside**: In democratic systems, elections provide observable window triggers. In authoritarian systems, the opening and closing of windows is often invisible until after the fact. Retrospective identification is even more pronounced.

**China case illustration**: The 2015 elderly care policy expansion followed this pattern:
- Problem stream: Aging demographic data (NBS indicators) + media coverage of abandoned elderly
- Policy stream: OECD-model long-term care proposals circulating within MOHRSS since 2010
- Politics stream: Xi administration's "people-centered development" framing (2013 onward) + NPC elder-care proposals gaining traction
- Entrepreneur: MOHRSS technocrats + NPC welfare committee
- Window: 13th Five-Year Plan drafting process (2015–2016) — a predictable internal window in the Chinese system

## Common Misapplications in Comparative Work

**Treating all windows as politically triggered**: In non-U.S. systems, researchers often miss problem windows (crises, court rulings, statistics releases) because they focus on elections. Both window types operate in all systems, though their relative frequency varies.

**Conflating policy stream with legislative agenda**: In parliamentary systems especially, researchers mistake the government's legislative program for the entire policy stream. The primeval soup includes proposals that never reach the legislative agenda — these matter for understanding what was *not* adopted and why.

**Single-country policy entrepreneur identification**: In comparative work, transnational policy entrepreneurs operate across systems — international organizations (WHO, OECD, World Bank), bilateral aid agencies, and diaspora networks can inject proposals into a national policy stream from outside. This pathway is invisible if analysis stays within national borders.

**Ignoring the EU as a structural variable**: For European member states, the EU represents a permanent structural coupling mechanism. Problem definitions, policy templates, and political pressure can all originate at the EU level, pre-coupling streams before domestic entrepreneurs act. This is not a "window" in the classic sense — it is a standing coupling channel.
