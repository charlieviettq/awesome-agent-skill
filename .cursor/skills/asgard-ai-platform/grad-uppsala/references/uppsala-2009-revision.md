# Uppsala 2009 Revision: From Psychic Distance to Network Position

**Source**: Johanson, J., & Vahlne, J.-E. (2009). The Uppsala internationalization process model revisited: From liability of foreignness to liability of outsidership. *Journal of International Business Studies*, 40(9), 1411–1431.

---

## What Changed and Why

The 1977 model treated internationalization as a firm-vs-environment problem: psychic distance created uncertainty, experiential learning reduced it, and firms moved up the establishment chain accordingly.

The 2009 revision reframes the problem: **markets do not exist as abstract environments — they exist as networks of relationships.** The key barrier to internationalization is not unfamiliarity with a country, but exclusion from the business networks that operate within it.

| Dimension | 1977 Model | 2009 Revision |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Core barrier | Liability of foreignness | **Liability of outsidership** |
| Unit of analysis | Firm vs. country | Firm vs. network |
| Knowledge type | Market knowledge (codifiable + experiential) | Relationship knowledge (opportunity recognition in networks) |
| Entry trigger | Accumulated market knowledge | Network position / relationship building |
| Psychic distance | Central: sequencing mechanism | Retained but subordinated to network logic |
| Stage model | Establishment chain as default path | Stages still exist but can be bypassed via network access |

## The Revised State-Change Model

### State Variables (what the firm holds at any given moment)

**1. Knowledge Opportunities**
Not abstract market knowledge, but **specific opportunities** identified through existing network relationships. This includes knowledge of:
- Partners' capabilities and needs
- Gaps in the network the firm could fill
- Signals from relationships that a new market is accessible

**2. Network Position**
The firm's embeddedness in a set of relationships with customers, suppliers, distributors, regulators, and complementors — both at home and in foreign markets.

- **Insider position**: the firm has established trust and commitment within a network
- **Outsider position**: the firm is excluded from the relevant network

> An outsider firm in a target market faces liability of outsidership regardless of how well it understands the country's culture or language.

### Change Variables (what the firm does)

**1. Learning, Creating, and Trust-Building**
Instead of "experiential learning" as passive accumulation, the 2009 model emphasizes active **relationship investment**:
- Learning happens in dyadic and multilateral interactions, not solo market research
- Trust and commitment are jointly produced by both parties
- Knowledge creation (not just acquisition) emerges from collaborative relationships

**2. Relationship and Network Commitment Decisions**
Commitment decisions now apply to **relationships**, not just to market modes:
- Deepening a relationship with an existing partner (more resources, exclusivity, joint development)
- Extending the network (entry into new relationships that provide access to a new market)
- Exiting a relationship (which may sever access to a market entirely)

### The Feedback Loop (2009 version)

```
[State]                          [Change]
Knowledge Opportunities   <---   Learning, Creating, Trust-Building
Network Position          <---   Relationship/Network Commitment Decisions
       |                                        ^
       |_________________ enables ______________|
```

The practical implication: **a firm with strong network position in Market A can access Market B faster than a rival with superior country knowledge but no network position**, because the partner in Market A can bridge into Market B's network.

## Liability of Outsidership: Operationalizing the Concept

The 2009 revision introduces "liability of outsidership" as a precise concept, not a metaphor.

### Definition

A firm suffers liability of outsidership when it lacks:
1. **Trust** — counterparties in the target network have no basis to believe the firm will honor commitments
2. **Knowledge** — the firm cannot identify which network actors matter or how the network is structured
3. **Position** — the firm has no incumbent relationships that carry reputation into the new context

### Assessment Checklist (apply to each target market)

For each target market, assess the firm's current network status:

| Criterion | Questions | Score (0–2) |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Existing relationships | Does the firm have ANY direct relationships with actors in this market? | 0=none, 1=indirect, 2=direct |
| Relationship depth | Are existing relationships transactional or trust-based? | 0=none, 1=transactional, 2=trust-based |
| Network reach | Can current partners bridge the firm into the target network? | 0=no, 1=possible, 2=confirmed |
| Reputation transfer | Will the firm's reputation in existing markets carry meaning in the target? | 0=no, 1=partial, 2=yes |
| Opportunity visibility | Has the firm identified specific actors or deals through network signals? | 0=no, 1=vague signals, 2=specific opportunities |

**Score interpretation:**
- 8–10: Low outsidership — rapid entry viable
- 5–7: Moderate outsidership — phased entry via relationship investment
- 0–4: High outsidership — enter through acquisition or by joining an existing insider's network first

## How the Establishment Chain Changes Under the 2009 Model

The four-stage chain (no export → agent → sales subsidiary → production subsidiary) is **not abolished** but **reinterpreted**:

| 1977 Interpretation | 2009 Reinterpretation |
|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Agent stage exists to gather market knowledge | Agent stage exists to build network position through a local insider |
| Sales subsidiary follows when knowledge is sufficient | Sales subsidiary follows when the firm has enough relationships to operate without the agent's network access |
| Stages are sequential | Stages can be bypassed if network access is obtained via acquisition or existing partner bridging |
| Psychic distance determines sequence | **Network position gap** determines sequence |

### Stage Bypass Conditions

A firm can skip the agent stage if it acquires a target that is already embedded in the local network. The acquisition buys **network position**, not just assets.

A firm can skip the sales subsidiary stage if a strong strategic partnership grants production rights and local distribution simultaneously.

The IRON LAW from the parent skill still applies with one caveat: **skipping stages is only feasible if network access substitutes for the missing experiential knowledge**. Without that substitution, failure risk remains high.

## Worked Example: Nordic Machinery Firm Entering Southeast Asia

**Background**: A Swedish industrial equipment manufacturer (120 employees, strong position in Nordic and German markets, limited Asia experience).

### Step 1: Assess Current Network Position in Target Markets

| Market | Direct Relationships | Bridging Partners | Outsidership Score |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Singapore | 1 distributor (transactional) | German partner has Singapore agent | 5/10 |
| Vietnam | None | None | 1/10 |
| Thailand | None | Swedish Trade Council contact | 2/10 |

**Decision**: Singapore is the entry point — not because psychic distance is lower (it is roughly similar to all three), but because the existing distributor relationship and German partner's network provide a bridge.

### Step 2: Map the Relationship Investment Required

Singapore entry sequence under the 2009 model:

1. **Deepen the existing distributor relationship** (6–12 months)
   - Move from transactional to trust-based: joint market development agreement, shared sales targets, transparency on pipeline
   - Goal: become an "insider" in the distributor's local network — access to their customers, complementors, and government contacts

2. **Leverage German partner's Singapore agent** (parallel, months 3–9)
   - Formal introduction: the German partner signals vouches for the firm
   - Reduces trust-building time from ~18 months to ~6 months (reputation transfer)

3. **Identify Vietnam bridge through Singapore network** (months 12–18)
   - The Singapore distributor likely has Vietnam contacts (ASEAN network overlap)
   - Enter Vietnam via Singapore distributor's referral, not through independent market research

4. **Thailand enters only after Vietnam relationships provide ASEAN-level position** (month 24+)

### Step 3: Commitment Decision Gate

Before committing to a Singapore sales subsidiary (Stage 3 in the establishment chain), verify:

- [ ] Distributor relationship is trust-based (not merely transactional)
- [ ] The firm has direct relationships with ≥3 end customers independent of the distributor
- [ ] At least one local hire is embedded in the customer network
- [ ] Revenue from Singapore covers the subsidiary fixed cost at 70% utilization

If these conditions are not met, stay at the agent/distributor stage. Premature subsidiary establishment without network position creates costs without the access benefits.

## The Opportunity Concept

The 2009 model adds "opportunities" explicitly as a state variable. This is a significant departure: in the 1977 model, firms respond to environmental stimuli; in the 2009 model, **opportunities are created in relationships, not discovered in markets**.

### Practical implication for market scanning

Do not ask: "What is the market size in Country X?"

Ask instead: "Which of our existing partners has relationships in Country X that could surface specific opportunities for us?"

| Question Type | 1977 Frame | 2009 Frame |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Where to enter? | Psychic distance ranking | Which networks can we access? |
| When to enter? | When market knowledge is sufficient | When a relationship provides opportunity visibility |
| How to enter? | Mode follows knowledge level | Mode follows network position available |
| Speed? | Determined by learning pace | Determined by relationship investment pace + partner willingness |

## Network Position as a Strategic Asset

The 2009 model implies that **network position is more durable than market knowledge**. Market knowledge depreciates (institutions change, competitors shift); network relationships, once established with trust, persist.

This has implications for divestment decisions:

> If a firm exits a market by closing a subsidiary but retains the key local relationships (through hiring the local manager, maintaining advisory ties, keeping the distributor contract active), it preserves the option to re-enter at low cost. If it severs all relationships on exit, re-entry carries full outsidership penalty.

### Network Position Audit (apply annually for each market)

For active markets:
- How many direct customer relationships does the firm hold independently (not mediated by distributors)?
- Which relationships are trust-based vs. transactional?
- Which actors in the local network are not yet connected to the firm?

For exited or un-entered markets:
- Does the firm retain any residual relationships?
- Can current network partners bridge into this market?

## Tensions and Known Limitations

**1. Network lock-in**
A strong network position in Market A may constrain which partners are acceptable in Market B (partner exclusivity, competitive conflicts). Firms can become trapped by their own network success.

**2. Network obsolescence**
Relationships provide access to the network as it is structured today. If the network reconfigures (consolidation, new entrants disrupting distribution), the firm's position may become worthless. The 2009 model underspecifies how to handle network disruption.

**3. Measurement difficulty**
"Network position" is harder to operationalize than psychic distance. Psychic distance can be estimated from secondary data (language, Hofstede dimensions, trade flows). Network position requires primary relationship mapping, which is expensive and firm-specific.

**4. Born globals remain partially unexplained**
The 2009 revision is better than 1977 at handling born globals (network relationships built by founders prior to firm founding explain rapid internationalization), but still does not fully address digital platforms that internationalize without physical network ties.

**5. Power asymmetry in networks**
The model treats network relationships as collaborative. In practice, a dominant buyer or a state-owned distributor may impose terms that reduce the internationalizing firm's network access rather than expanding it. The model has no explicit mechanism for handling power imbalance.

## Key Constructs Summary

| Construct | Definition | How to Observe |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Liability of outsidership | Cost of being excluded from relevant business networks | Inability to access customers, distributors, or regulators without intermediary; high cost of initial sales |
| Network position | Firm's set of established, trust-based relationships in a given market | Count of direct customer/partner relationships; presence of firm personnel embedded in local industry associations |
| Opportunity | A specific, identified possibility for value creation surfaced through a relationship | Named partner, named deal, named market gap — not abstract "market potential" |
| Trust | Counterparty's expectation that commitments will be honored | Repeat transactions, referrals provided by counterparty, exclusivity granted |
| Commitment (2009) | Resources dedicated to a specific relationship, not just a market mode | Joint development agreements, shared headcount, co-investment |
