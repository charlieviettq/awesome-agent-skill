# Disruption Cases: Steel Minimills & Disk Drives

These two cases are Christensen's primary empirical evidence for Disruptive Innovation theory. Both are longitudinal datasets — not anecdotes — covering 10–30 year industry dynamics. Use them as calibration anchors when assessing new disruption claims.

---

## Case 1: Steel Minimills vs. Integrated Mills (1960s–1990s)

### Industry Structure at Disruption Onset (~1960)

**Integrated mills** (U.S. Steel, Bethlehem Steel):
- Produced steel from raw ore via blast furnaces
- Cost structure: high fixed costs, unionized labor, wide product range
- Served premium segments: sheet steel for automobiles, appliances, construction beams
- Gross margin on rebar (lowest grade): ~7%
- Gross margin on sheet steel (highest grade): ~12–15%

**Minimills** (Nucor, Chaparral Steel):
- Melted scrap metal in electric arc furnaces (EAF)
- Cost structure: ~20% lower operating costs than integrated mills
- Initial product quality: acceptable only for the lowest-grade application — **concrete reinforcing bar (rebar)**
- Rebar customers: construction companies. Requirement: "doesn't snap under tension." No cosmetic or precision requirements.

### Performance Trajectory Data

| Year | Minimill cost/ton (rebar) | Integrated mill cost/ton (rebar) | Minimill product range |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|------------------------|
| 1970 | $150 | $190 | Rebar only |
| 1980 | $135 | $180 | Rebar + structural sections |
| 1988 | $120 | $170 | Rebar + sections + angle iron |
| 1992 | $115 | $165 | All of above + bar & rod |
| 1997 | $110 | $160 | Enters sheet steel |

*Figures approximate; sourced from Christensen (1997) Chapter 3 reconstructions.*

### Incumbent Response: Rational Retreat

When minimills entered rebar in the 1960s:
- Integrated mills calculated rebar margin = 7%
- Minimills could serve that segment profitably at lower cost
- **Rational incumbent move**: exit rebar, redeploy capacity to higher-margin products

This is the **asymmetric motivation trap**. The integrated mills were not stupid — they were correctly optimizing for margin. Every time minimills moved upmarket (into structural sections, then bar and rod), integrated mills repeated the calculation and retreated again.

By 1992, minimills had displaced integrated mills from every product category except sheet steel. When Nucor announced entry into sheet steel in 1987, integrated mills had already lost the organizational capability to compete on cost in lower grades.

### Disruption Classification

| Criterion | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| Performance overshoot? | **Yes** — rebar buyers needed "doesn't snap," integrated mills' metallurgical precision was irrelevant |
| Foothold type | **Low-end** — rebar is the cheapest, lowest-prestige steel product |
| Upmarket migration path? | **Yes** — EAF technology improved; thin-slab casting (1989) enabled sheet steel entry |
| Asymmetric motivation? | **Yes** — each retreat improved integrated mills' average margin short-term |

### What "Good Enough" Looked Like

Rebar specification (ASTM A615): yield strength ≥ 40,000 psi, deformations for concrete bonding. That is the entire performance threshold. Minimill rebar met this from day one. The integrated mills' additional metallurgical capabilities (consistency, alloying control) were **zero economic value** to rebar buyers.

This is the key diagnostic: identify the **minimum viable performance threshold** for the target segment, then ask whether the entrant clears it. If yes, quality disadvantage is irrelevant.

## Case 2: Hard Disk Drives (1976–1995)

### Why This Case Is Uniquely Valuable

The disk drive industry ran through **four complete disruption cycles** in under 20 years, with different incumbents disrupted each time. This compresses what normally takes decades into a controlled natural experiment.

### The Four Cycles

#### Cycle 1: 14-inch → 8-inch (1978–1982)

**Incumbents**: IBM, Memorex, Storage Technology (14-inch drives for mainframes)
**Entrant footholds**: Shugart Associates, MiniScribe, Micropolis (8-inch drives)

- 14-inch drives: 200–500 MB capacity, required for mainframes
- 8-inch drives at launch: 10–40 MB — far below mainframe needs
- **Foothold market**: minicomputers (Digital Equipment Corporation, Data General)
- Minicomputer engineers needed: small physical footprint, lower power draw
- They *accepted* lower capacity because their workloads didn't need 200 MB

Incumbent response: mainframe customers demanded more capacity, not less. Investing in 8-inch was cannibalizing their core business.

By 1981, 8-inch drives reached 60+ MB and began winning minicomputer contracts from 14-inch suppliers. 14-inch incumbents lost the minicomputer segment entirely.

#### Cycle 2: 8-inch → 5.25-inch (1980–1986)

**Incumbents**: Shugart, MiniScribe (former disruptors, now incumbents)
**Entrant footholds**: Seagate Technology, Tandon (5.25-inch drives)

- 8-inch drives at this point: 60–300 MB
- 5.25-inch drives at launch: 5–10 MB
- **Foothold market**: desktop personal computers (Apple II, IBM PC)
- PC customers needed: fits in a desktop case, cheap enough for consumer price point
- Capacity needs of early PCs: 5–10 MB was adequate for word processing + spreadsheets

Former disruptors (8-inch drive makers) had now built organizations optimized for minicomputer customers demanding high capacity. The 5.25-inch opportunity looked unattractive.

Seagate shipped 5.25-inch drives in 1980. By 1983 it was the largest disk drive company in the world by unit volume.

#### Cycle 3: 5.25-inch → 3.5-inch (1985–1990)

**Incumbents**: Seagate, Micropolis, Maxtor (5.25-inch makers)
**Entrant footholds**: Rodime, Conner Peripherals (3.5-inch)

- **Foothold**: laptop computers and portable workstations
- Key non-capacity attributes valued by laptop buyers: weight, shock resistance, battery draw
- 3.5-inch drives used less power and survived being jostled — 5.25-inch drives failed in field use on laptops
- Capacity gap at launch: 3.5-inch had ~20 MB vs 5.25-inch's 60–100 MB

Seagate's sales force talked to existing desktop PC customers, who said they wanted more storage, not smaller drives. The product development roadmap reflected what customers asked for. **This is the innovator's dilemma in action**: customer feedback accurately described existing customers' needs and led Seagate to underinvest in 3.5-inch.

#### Cycle 4: 3.5-inch → 2.5-inch (1989–1994)

**Foothold**: notebook computers. Same pattern: non-capacity attributes (weight, power) opened the door before capacity parity was reached.

### Cross-Cycle Pattern: Performance Trajectory Data

| Drive size | Capacity at launch | Capacity 5 years later | Time to reach predecessor's launch capacity |
|------------|-------------------|------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| 8-inch | 10 MB | 100 MB | ~3 years |
| 5.25-inch | 5 MB | 70 MB | ~3 years |
| 3.5-inch | 20 MB | 200 MB | ~3 years |
| 2.5-inch | 20 MB | 340 MB | ~2 years |

The improvement rate of each smaller form factor consistently exceeded what the target segment required. The disruption window is the period **before** the entrant reaches performance parity — that is when incumbents should respond but don't.

### Survival Analysis: Incumbents Across Cycles

Of the 17 firms that produced 14-inch drives:
- **0** became significant 8-inch producers
- Of the 14-inch → 8-inch survivors, **none** became significant 5.25-inch producers

The data pattern: firms that survived disruption did so by entering the new form factor **as new ventures**, not by transitioning their existing businesses. Seagate itself was a startup entrant in 5.25-inch; it then failed to lead the 3.5-inch transition as an incumbent.

## Cross-Case Diagnostic: Separating Disruption from Sustaining Innovation

Both cases clarify a common misapplication of the theory:

### What is NOT Disruption

| Scenario | Classification | Reason |
|----------|---------------|--------|
| Western Digital releases a faster 3.5-inch drive | Sustaining | Competes on incumbent's performance dimension |
| Tesla Model S enters luxury EV market | Sustaining | Started at the high end; outperformed ICE on key metrics |
| Nucor enters sheet steel after 30 years | Sustaining (at this point) | Competing directly on incumbent's home turf after capability maturation |
| Budget airlines in 1970 (Southwest) | Low-end disruptive | Started with routes majors ignored; customers accepted no meals/assigned seats |

### The "Non-Consumer" Test

New-market disruption targets people currently **not buying at all**. Diagnostic question: *Who is the entrant's first customer, and why weren't they buying from the incumbent?*

- Personal computers → non-consumers of computing (individuals, small businesses priced out of minicomputers)
- Minimills → same rebar buyers, but the integrated mills had started to exit
- 3.5-inch drives → laptop users who literally could not use existing drives (size/power incompatibility)

If the first customers were already buying from the incumbent, it is a low-end foothold, not a new-market foothold. The distinction matters for strategy: new-market disruption expands the total addressable market; low-end disruption redistributes existing demand.

## Applying Cases as Calibration

When assessing a new disruption claim, map it against these templates:

**Steel minimill template** (low-end foothold):
1. Identify the lowest-prestige, lowest-margin product in the incumbent's portfolio
2. Confirm the entrant serves *only* that segment initially
3. Confirm the entrant has a cost advantage (not just a price advantage — margin matters)
4. Trace the upmarket trajectory: what does the entrant need to improve to take the next segment?

**Disk drive template** (form factor / new-context foothold):
1. Identify a customer context where non-capacity attributes (size, power, ruggedization, price) dominate
2. Confirm incumbent's sales force is not calling on these customers
3. Measure capacity gap at launch and improvement rate — disruption window = time for entrant to close the gap
4. Check if incumbent's own customers are asking for the new form factor — if not, incumbents will rationally ignore it

## Common Misreads of These Cases

**"The incumbents were incompetent."**
Wrong. Integrated steel mills and 14-inch drive makers were managed well by conventional metrics. They followed the advice of their best customers, allocated capital to highest-margin opportunities, and exited low-margin segments. The failure mechanism is rational optimization, not incompetence.

**"Better technology caused the disruption."**
Electric arc furnaces and smaller disk platters were not superior technologies at launch — they were inferior on the metrics incumbents' customers cared about. The disruption mechanism was the *business model fit* with an overlooked segment, not raw technological capability.

**"Disruption was inevitable."**
IBM successfully launched the PC division as an autonomous unit. Merrill Lynch launched Cash Management Account to preempt disruptors in retail brokerage. The disk drive and steel cases are instances where incumbents *did not* respond adequately — they are not proof that response is impossible.
