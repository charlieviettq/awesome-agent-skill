# Distribution Channels

## Channel Structure Fundamentals

A distribution channel is the path a product takes from producer to end customer. Every channel decision trades off **control**, **cost**, and **coverage**.

```
Producer → [Channel Layers] → End Customer

Level 0 (Direct):   Producer ────────────────────→ Customer
Level 1:            Producer → Retailer ──────────→ Customer
Level 2:            Producer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Customer
Level 3:            Producer → Distributor → Wholesaler → Retailer → Customer
```

Each layer adds cost (typically 20–60% markup per layer) but also adds reach and local expertise. The deeper the channel, the less control the producer retains over pricing and presentation.

---

## Channel Coverage: The Three Modes

The SKILL.md lists three coverage modes. Here is how to choose between them.

| Mode | Definition | Use when... | Risk |
|------|-----------|-------------|------|
| **Intensive** | Available in every possible outlet | Commodities, convenience goods, impulse buys | Price war; brand dilution if placed alongside inferior substitutes |
| **Selective** | Chosen outlets that meet criteria | Mid-range goods with comparison shopping; want some control | Under-coverage in weak regions |
| **Exclusive** | One partner per territory | Premium/luxury goods; franchise systems; high-involvement purchases | Dependence on single partner; antitrust issues in some markets |

### Coverage Decision Checklist

Answer yes/no to each:

1. Is the product purchased by impulse or convenience? → **Intensive**
2. Is the target segment willing to seek out a specific retailer? → at least **Selective**
3. Does the product require trained staff to sell or install? → **Selective** or **Exclusive**
4. Does the price point or brand story require controlled presentation? → **Selective** or **Exclusive**
5. Is protecting price integrity critical? → **Exclusive**

If you answer yes to #1 but also yes to #4, you have a channel-brand conflict. Resolve at the Product/Price level first before choosing coverage.

## Direct vs. Indirect Channel Decision

### The Channel Economics Formula

**Direct channel (own website, own store, own sales team):**

```
Gross Margin (Direct) = Revenue − COGS − Fulfillment Cost − Marketing CAC
```

**Indirect channel (retailer, marketplace, distributor):**

```
Gross Margin (Indirect) = (Revenue × (1 − Channel Take Rate)) − COGS − Trade Spend
```

Where:
- **Channel Take Rate**: the percentage the intermediary keeps (Shopee/Lazada: 3–8%; physical retail: 40–60%; Amazon US: 8–15%)
- **Trade Spend**: slotting fees, promotional discounts, co-op advertising required by the channel

### Worked Example: Taiwanese Tea Brand

Product: NT$65 cold-brew oolong bottle. COGS = NT$18.

**Option A — Direct (own website + line shopping):**
- Fulfillment cost per unit: NT$12 (packaging + logistics)
- CAC amortized per unit: NT$8
- Gross margin per unit: 65 − 18 − 12 − 8 = **NT$27 (41.5%)**

**Option B — 7-ELEVEN selective placement:**
- Retail price: NT$65
- Retailer take: 40% → producer receives NT$39
- Trade spend (promo, listing): NT$3/unit amortized
- Gross margin per unit: 39 − 18 − 3 = **NT$18 (27.7%)**

**Option C — Shopee marketplace:**
- Commission: 5%; payment fee: 2% → take rate = 7%
- Fulfillment: NT$10
- CAC/ads: NT$6
- Gross margin per unit: (65 × 0.93) − 18 − 10 − 6 = 60.45 − 34 = **NT$26.45 (40.7%)**

**Decision:** Direct and Shopee have similar unit economics. 7-ELEVEN wins on volume and brand visibility but shrinks margins. The correct answer depends on volume assumptions, not unit margin alone.

### Volume-Adjusted Comparison

```
Annual Profit = Units Sold × Gross Margin per Unit
```

| Channel | Est. Monthly Units | Gross Margin/Unit | Monthly Profit |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Own website | 200 | NT$27 | NT$5,400 |
| 7-ELEVEN (100 stores) | 3,000 | NT$18 | NT$54,000 |
| Shopee | 800 | NT$26.45 | NT$21,160 |

At early stage, 7-ELEVEN dominates on absolute profit despite lower margins. At scale, own website becomes strategically valuable (customer data, pricing control).

## Channel Selection Framework (5-Factor Score)

Rate each candidate channel 1–5 on each factor, weight by importance to your situation.

| Factor | What to assess | Weight (adjust to your case) |
|--------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Reach** | How many target-segment customers does this channel already have? | 25% |
| **Fit** | Does the channel's positioning match your Product/Price positioning? | 25% |
| **Control** | Can you control pricing, presentation, and returns? | 20% |
| **Cost** | Total channel cost (take rate + trade spend + logistics) vs. margin floor | 20% |
| **Reversibility** | How hard is it to exit if this channel underperforms? | 10% |

**Scoring example — premium tea, three channels:**

| Factor | 7-ELEVEN | PChome | Own D2C |
|--------|----------|--------|---------|
| Reach (1–5) | 5 | 3 | 1 |
| Fit (1–5) | 3 | 2 | 5 |
| Control (1–5) | 2 | 3 | 5 |
| Cost (1–5, 5=low cost) | 2 | 3 | 4 |
| Reversibility (1–5) | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| **Weighted Score** | **2.85** | **2.90** | **3.95** |

D2C wins on fit and control but loses on reach. The practical strategy: use 7-ELEVEN for volume and brand awareness early, invest in D2C for long-term margin and data ownership.

## Channel Conflict

Channel conflict occurs when multiple channels compete for the same customer in ways that undercut each other.

### Types

**Horizontal conflict**: Two retailers in the same channel tier fight over customers (e.g., two 7-ELEVEN franchisees near each other — usually not your problem to solve).

**Vertical conflict**: Producer and channel partner disagree on pricing, territory, or promotional terms.

**Inter-channel conflict** (the most common problem): You sell direct online at NT$65 and also through 7-ELEVEN at NT$65. Shopee sellers start discounting to NT$50. The channel price integrity collapses.

### Conflict Prevention Rules

1. **Price floor policy**: Set a Minimum Advertised Price (MAP). State it contractually with every channel partner. NT$65 is the floor; you don't enforce it at 7-ELEVEN's register, but you refuse to supply any partner who advertises below it.

2. **SKU differentiation**: Sell different SKUs in different channels. 7-ELEVEN gets a 350ml bottle; D2C offers a 500ml bundle. Prevents direct price comparison.

3. **Territory isolation**: Exclusive distributors get geographic territory; reduces horizontal conflict.

4. **Margin protection**: Never let any channel partner's list cost drop below COGS + minimum acceptable margin. Once a channel starts dumping, brand equity erodes faster than you can recoup.

### The Premium Channel Exit Problem

Entering a discount channel (e.g., Costco, wholesale clubs, clearance promotions) is **very hard to reverse**:

- Customers who discovered you at NT$30 will not repurchase at NT$65
- Premium channels (boutiques, City Café shelf) will delist you if they see you at Costco
- SKU differentiation helps but cannot fully isolate perceived quality

The SKILL.md Iron Law applies here: "Place decisions are hard to reverse." Operationally, this means: **require board-level or founder approval before entering any channel whose average selling price is more than 20% below your primary channel price.**

## Multi-Channel Sequencing Strategy

Most brands cannot launch every channel simultaneously. Common sequencing patterns:

### Pattern A: Prove at D2C, then expand (works for premium/niche)

```
Month 0–6:   D2C only (own website, Instagram shop)
             Goal: prove demand, collect customer data, refine messaging
Month 6–12:  Add 1–2 selective marketplace channels (Momo > Shopee for premium)
Month 12+:   Approach physical selective retail only after proven sell-through
```

**Why this order:** D2C validates the proposition cheaply. Marketplaces add volume without pricing risk if you enforce MAP. Physical retail requires consistent volume to justify trade spend.

### Pattern B: Retail-led, then defend with D2C (works for mass/convenience goods)

```
Month 0–3:   Secure distributor → enter CVS or pharmacy chains
             Goal: reach, trial, brand awareness via distribution
Month 3–9:   Launch D2C to capture repeat buyers at higher margin
Month 9+:    Use D2C data to negotiate better placement with physical retail
```

**Why this order:** For convenience goods, trial requires physical availability. D2C comes second as a margin-recapture play, not a primary launch vehicle.

### Pattern C: Marketplace-first (works for new brands with no distributor relationships)

```
Month 0–3:   Shopee or Momo — lowest barrier to entry, fastest feedback
Month 3–6:   If Shopee unit economics work, add D2C for margin; if not, fix product first
Month 6+:    Selective physical retail only if product-market fit is confirmed
```

## Channel Readiness Checklist

Before committing to a new channel, confirm:

- [ ] Unit economics at that channel's take rate still exceed your margin floor
- [ ] You have sufficient inventory depth to meet the channel's minimum order / replenishment requirements
- [ ] Your packaging and labeling meet the channel's requirements (barcode, language, size)
- [ ] You have agreed MAP policy and the channel partner will enforce or accept it
- [ ] You have a clear plan to handle returns from that channel
- [ ] Entering this channel does not conflict with existing exclusivity agreements
- [ ] The channel's typical customer matches your STP target segment (verify with data, not assumption)

## Key Numbers to Know

| Metric | What it means | When to use |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Channel Take Rate** | % of retail price kept by intermediary | Gross margin modeling |
| **Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO)** | Avg days from production to sale via this channel | Cash flow planning; physical retail often 60–90 days |
| **Sell-Through Rate** | % of units placed that actually sell to end customers | Predicts restocking; below 70% triggers delisting risk |
| **Trade Spend %** | Slotting, co-op, promotions as % of channel revenue | True cost of physical retail channel |
| **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) by channel** | Total marketing spend ÷ new customers from that channel | Compare D2C vs marketplace vs paid media |

## Common Mistakes

**Entering too many channels at launch**: Stock splits across 5 channels means thin coverage everywhere. One or two channels done well outperform five channels done poorly.

**Confusing marketplace rank with brand equity**: High Shopee ranking is rented, not owned. If Shopee changes its algorithm or a competitor undercuts price, the rank disappears. D2C customer lists are owned assets.

**Assuming physical retail is "premium" by default**: A product placed on the wrong shelf (end-cap clearance vs. chilled premium section) can damage rather than enhance positioning. Placement within the store matters as much as which store.

**Ignoring channel data feedback loops**: Physical retail gives almost no customer data. Marketplaces give transaction data only. D2C gives behavioral data. If you only sell through physical retail, you are flying blind on customer behavior.

**Treating Place as a one-time decision**: Channel strategy should be reviewed quarterly in the first two years. Sell-through data, competitor moves, and channel economics all shift faster than most founders expect.
