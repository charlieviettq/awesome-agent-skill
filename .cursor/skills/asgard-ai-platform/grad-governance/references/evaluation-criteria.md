# Governance Evaluation Criteria and Indicators

Governance performance cannot be measured by a single metric. This reference operationalizes the six core criteria from the parent skill's Output Format into concrete indicators, scoring rubrics, and a composite scoring procedure. The goal is to move from "legitimacy: medium" to a defensible, evidence-backed rating.

---

## The Six Criteria and Their Logic

| Criterion | Core Question | Primary Governance Failure It Detects |
|-----------|--------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Legitimacy** | Do affected parties accept the arrangement's right to govern? | Democratic deficit, capture, exclusion |
| **Accountability** | Can actors be held responsible for outcomes? | Accountability gaps in networks/PPPs |
| **Effectiveness** | Are intended outcomes achieved? | Design failure, implementation failure |
| **Efficiency** | Are resources used without waste? | Overlap, duplication, transaction costs |
| **Equity** | Are costs and benefits distributed fairly? | Structural exclusion, regressive outcomes |
| **Transparency** | Is information accessible to those who need it? | Information asymmetry, elite capture |

> **Note**: These criteria trade off against each other. Hierarchical governance often scores higher on accountability and transparency but lower on effectiveness in complex problems. Network governance reverses this pattern. Your analysis should name the trade-off explicitly — not resolve it arbitrarily.

## Operationalizing Each Criterion

### 1. Legitimacy

Legitimacy has two analytically distinct dimensions:

**Input legitimacy** (procedural): Were the right actors included? Were procedures fair?

**Output legitimacy** (substantive): Did the arrangement produce acceptable outcomes?

Beetham (1991) adds a third:

**Belief legitimacy**: Do participants and affected publics believe the arrangement has the right to govern?

#### Legitimacy Indicator Set

| Indicator | Measurement Method | Scoring |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| Actor inclusiveness | Count of affected groups with formal representation ÷ total identified affected groups | 0–1 ratio |
| Procedural fairness | Presence of published decision rules, appeal mechanisms, dissent recording | 0–3 checklist |
| Public acceptance | Survey or media sentiment; protest frequency | Qualitative scale |
| Legal authorization | Explicit legal mandate (statute, regulation, treaty) vs. informal arrangement | Binary + context |
| Democratic linkage | Number of steps between elected representative and governance body | Integer (fewer = higher legitimacy) |

**Democratic linkage example:**
- City council directly oversees a housing authority → 1 step → high input legitimacy
- National ministry delegates to regional body, which contracts to an NGO, which subcontracts delivery → 3+ steps → legitimacy risk

#### Legitimacy Rating Rubric

| Rating | Conditions |
|--------|-----------|
| **High** | Legal mandate present; >80% of affected groups represented; public acceptance documented; appeal mechanism exists |
| **Medium** | Legal mandate present OR majority represented, but not both; limited public consultation; no formal appeal |
| **Low** | No legal mandate; key stakeholders excluded; opposition documented; no accountability to affected public |

### 2. Accountability

The accountability chain has three components (Bovens 2007):

1. **Forum**: who holds actors to account (parliament, courts, citizens, auditors)?
2. **Actor**: who is being held to account?
3. **Conduct**: over what (financial, procedural, substantive outcomes)?

Multi-level and network governance creates **accountability gaps** when the chain is broken or when no single forum has jurisdiction over a cross-sector arrangement.

#### Accountability Diagnosis Checklist

```
For each major actor in the governance arrangement, ask:

□ Is there a designated forum with authority to demand an account?
□ Are performance obligations written into formal agreements (contracts, MoUs)?
□ Is there regular public reporting with standardized indicators?
□ Are sanctions possible and proportionate (not merely reputational)?
□ Is accountability continuous (ongoing) or episodic (elections, audits only)?
□ Who is accountable when multiple actors share responsibility for failure?
```

If the last question has no clear answer, document an **accountability gap** — this is the single most common governance failure in network arrangements.

#### Accountability Gap Severity Scale

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Gap-0** | Clear single actor accountable; forum has jurisdiction and capacity |
| **Gap-1** | Accountability distributed but forums coordinate; some ambiguity on shared failures |
| **Gap-2** | Multiple forums with overlapping or conflicting jurisdiction; blame-shifting occurs |
| **Gap-3** | No forum has effective jurisdiction over the arrangement as a whole; outcomes are ungoverned |

### 3. Effectiveness

Effectiveness requires defining outcomes *before* evaluating whether they were achieved. The most common error is measuring outputs (activities completed) instead of outcomes (conditions changed).

#### Output vs. Outcome vs. Impact Distinction

| Level | Example (Urban Housing PPP) | Measurement |
|-------|----------------------------|-------------|
| **Input** | €50M public subsidy, 3 private developers | Budget records |
| **Output** | 800 units constructed | Physical count |
| **Outcome** | Vacancy rate in low-income housing falls 15% | Administrative data |
| **Impact** | Homelessness rate falls; attributable to this arrangement | Counterfactual required |

Most governance evaluations can reach the outcome level. Impact claims require a counterfactual (what would have happened without this arrangement?), which is rarely available — be explicit about this limitation.

#### Effectiveness Rating Procedure

**Step 1**: Identify the arrangement's stated objectives (from founding documents, contracts, legislation).

**Step 2**: For each objective, identify the measurable outcome indicator.

**Step 3**: Obtain baseline (pre-arrangement) and current values.

**Step 4**: Calculate achievement ratio:

```
Achievement Ratio = (Observed Change) / (Target Change)
```

**Example:**
- Target: reduce street homelessness by 30% over 5 years
- Baseline: 4,200 persons
- Current: 3,500 persons
- Observed change: −700 (−16.7%)
- Achievement Ratio: 16.7% / 30% = **0.56** (partially effective)

**Step 5**: Apply contextual adjustment — were objectives realistic given resources?

| Achievement Ratio | Raw Rating | Contextual Adjustment |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| ≥ 0.90 | High | Downgrade if targets were set deliberately low |
| 0.60–0.89 | Medium | Apply as-is in most cases |
| 0.30–0.59 | Low-Medium | Upgrade if context changed substantially |
| < 0.30 | Low | No adjustment warranted |

### 4. Efficiency

Governance efficiency examines transaction costs (the costs of operating the arrangement itself, separate from the costs of delivering outputs) and overlap/duplication.

#### Transaction Cost Categories in Governance

Williamson's framework applied to governance:

| Cost Type | Description | Governance Context |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Search costs** | Finding partners, information about capacity | PPP procurement, network formation |
| **Negotiation costs** | Time and resources to reach agreements | Inter-agency coordination, contract design |
| **Monitoring costs** | Ensuring compliance and performance | Contract management, audit functions |
| **Enforcement costs** | Resolving disputes, imposing sanctions | Dispute resolution bodies, court use |
| **Adaptation costs** | Renegotiating when conditions change | Long-term contracts in changing environments |

High transaction costs are not automatically a reason to change governance mode — they may be justified by the benefits of network coordination. But they become an efficiency failure when:
- Transaction costs exceed a significant fraction of the value of outcomes delivered (>20–30% is a common rule of thumb for PPPs)
- Duplication of functions exists across actors without compensating benefit
- Time-to-decision is systematically longer than alternatives

#### Efficiency Diagnostic Table

| Signal | Evidence | Interpretation |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Multiple agencies doing identical reporting | Audit, document review | Duplication; merge or standardize |
| Contract renegotiation frequency | Number of amendments / contract age | High adaptation costs; may indicate scope uncertainty |
| Meeting-to-decision ratio | Minutes reviewed | High coordination overhead; streamline decision rights |
| Cost per unit output vs. comparator | Budget ÷ outputs | Benchmark against comparable arrangements |

### 5. Equity

Equity analysis asks: who bears costs, who receives benefits, and is the distribution justifiable?

#### Three Equity Lenses

**Distributional equity**: Are outcomes distributed fairly across social groups (income, geography, race/ethnicity, gender)?

**Procedural equity**: Were groups equally able to participate in decisions?

**Recognitional equity** (Fraser 2000): Are groups' identities and interests acknowledged, not just accommodated?

#### Equity Analysis Procedure

**Step 1**: Identify the relevant social categories (context-dependent — income in housing, geography in infrastructure, ethnicity in health services, etc.).

**Step 2**: Disaggregate outputs and outcomes by category.

**Step 3**: Apply the appropriate equity standard:

| Standard | Formula / Test | When to Apply |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| **Equal treatment** | Each group receives the same per-capita allocation | Where needs are roughly equal |
| **Proportionate to need** | Higher-need groups receive proportionally more | Social services, health, housing |
| **Non-regressive** | Low-income groups pay ≤ proportional share of costs | Fee-setting, taxation |
| **Pareto improvement** | No group is made worse off | Efficiency-focused reforms |

**Step 4**: Calculate a simple distributional ratio where data allow:

```
Equity Ratio = (Benefit Share of disadvantaged group) / (Population Share of disadvantaged group)
```

- Ratio > 1.0: arrangement favors the disadvantaged group (pro-equity)
- Ratio = 1.0: proportional distribution
- Ratio < 1.0: arrangement benefits advantaged groups disproportionately (equity concern)

**Example:**
- Low-income households = 30% of population
- Low-income households receiving housing subsidies = 18% of total subsidy value
- Equity Ratio = 18% / 30% = **0.60** → equity concern

#### Equity Red Flags in Governance Arrangements

- Civil society actors in a "network" all represent organized, middle-class interests
- PPP contract terms shift financial risk to public sector while private sector captures upside
- Geographic concentration of benefits in politically powerful areas
- Informal norms exclude non-English-speaking or non-networked actors from key decisions

### 6. Transparency

Transparency is not simply "information is published." It requires that relevant information reaches relevant actors in usable form and in time to influence decisions.

#### Transparency Conditions (all three required for genuine transparency)

1. **Disclosure**: information is made available
2. **Accessibility**: it can be found and understood by affected parties (not just experts)
3. **Timeliness**: it is available before decisions are made, not after

#### Transparency Indicator Checklist

```
Disclosure:
□ Are meeting agendas and minutes published?
□ Are contracts or their substantive terms public?
□ Are performance data reported with consistent indicators over time?
□ Are financial flows (subsidies, fees, transfers) disclosed?

Accessibility:
□ Is information available in formats accessible to non-specialists?
□ Are languages of affected communities accommodated?
□ Is information proactively distributed or only available on request?

Timeliness:
□ Are decisions published before implementation, allowing response?
□ Are consultations held when changes can still influence decisions?
□ Is reporting cycle frequent enough to detect problems early?
```

**Scoring**: Count checkmarks. 9–12: High. 5–8: Medium. <5: Low.

Note: Transparency in the presence of complexity can be misleading — disclosing 500-page PPP contracts satisfies formal transparency while defeating its purpose. This is a common technique for **nominal compliance**.

## Composite Scoring Procedure

When a summary rating is needed (e.g., comparing two governance arrangements), use the following weighted composite:

### Default Weights

| Criterion | Default Weight | Rationale |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Effectiveness | 0.25 | Core purpose of any governance arrangement |
| Accountability | 0.20 | Most commonly absent in complex arrangements |
| Legitimacy | 0.20 | Foundation for durability |
| Equity | 0.15 | Often neglected, analytically distinct |
| Transparency | 0.10 | Enables the others |
| Efficiency | 0.10 | Important but insufficient alone |
| **Total** | **1.00** | |

> **Weight adjustment**: These are defaults, not dogma. If the governance question is specifically about democratic legitimacy (e.g., a constitutional reform), weight legitimacy higher. If the question is a PPP value-for-money review, weight efficiency and effectiveness higher. Document any adjustment and its justification.

### Rating to Numeric Conversion

| Rating | Score |
|--------|-------|
| High | 3 |
| Medium | 2 |
| Low | 1 |

### Composite Score Formula

```
Composite = Σ (Weight_i × Score_i)
```

**Worked Example — Urban Water PPP:**

| Criterion | Weight | Rating | Score | Weighted |
|-----------|--------|--------|-------|---------|
| Effectiveness | 0.25 | Medium | 2 | 0.50 |
| Accountability | 0.20 | Low | 1 | 0.20 |
| Legitimacy | 0.20 | Medium | 2 | 0.40 |
| Equity | 0.15 | Low | 1 | 0.15 |
| Transparency | 0.10 | Medium | 2 | 0.20 |
| Efficiency | 0.10 | High | 3 | 0.30 |
| **Composite** | | | | **1.75 / 3.00** |

Interpretation scale:

| Composite Range | Overall Assessment |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 2.50–3.00 | Well-governed; minor improvements only |
| 2.00–2.49 | Adequately governed; specific failures warrant attention |
| 1.50–1.99 | Governance gaps; reform recommended |
| 1.00–1.49 | Serious governance failures; restructuring warranted |

The worked example (1.75) falls in the "reform recommended" band — the accountability and equity failures are the primary drivers.

## Common Scoring Errors

**Error 1 — Mistaking outputs for effectiveness**: A PPP "built 500 units" is an output. Whether housing outcomes improved is the effectiveness question.

**Error 2 — Symmetry assumption**: Not all criteria have equal weight in every context. A governance arrangement scoring Low on accountability but High on everything else is not a "medium" arrangement — accountability failures can be catastrophic in high-stakes settings.

**Error 3 — Static snapshot**: Governance performance changes over time, especially in long-term PPPs. A score from year 3 of a 25-year concession may not reflect year 15. Note the evaluation date and flag dynamic conditions.

**Error 4 — Conflating transparency with accountability**: An arrangement can be highly transparent (all data published) while accountability remains low (no forum has authority to act on the data).

**Error 5 — Ignoring the shadow of hierarchy**: A network governance arrangement may appear to lack hierarchy, yet government retains the power to dissolve or override the network. This shapes actor behavior and must be reflected in legitimacy and accountability ratings.
