# Example: First-Generation PhD Students and the Lived Experience of Impostor Syndrome in STEM

## Scenario

Dr. Lin Mei-hua, a qualitative researcher at National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University's Institute of Education, is designing a study for a grant proposal due 2026-06-01. Her research question: *"What is the lived experience of impostor syndrome among first-generation PhD students in STEM programs in Taiwan?"*

She has reviewed quantitative scales (Clance Impostor Phenomenon Scale) and finds them inadequate — they measure frequency of feelings but not what the experience *is like* from the inside. She wants to understand the essential structure of the experience itself, not just its prevalence. She asks: "How do I apply IPA to this? What does bracketing look like in practice here? And what should my output look like?"

Dr. Lin has a personal background: she was herself a first-generation university student. She is worried this will bias her analysis.

---

## Analysis

### Step 1: Phenomenon Definition and Bracketing

**Phenomenon precisely defined:** The lived experience of perceiving oneself as intellectually fraudulent despite evidence of competence, as experienced by first-generation PhD students during their doctoral training in STEM.

**Epoche — Dr. Lin's bracketing statement (written before data collection):**

> I believe impostor syndrome among first-gen students stems from class-based cultural mismatch — that the academy is structurally hostile to working-class epistemologies. I expect participants to describe shame and concealment. I have personally felt "found out" after receiving a scholarship. I expect the academic supervisor relationship to be central.
>
> I am suspending these assumptions. I will not guide participants toward class narratives, shame, or supervisor dynamics. I will let them tell me what the experience is.

**IPA note:** This bracketing is partial — Dr. Lin's insider status will surface during analysis. She must note each moment she "hears" her own story in a participant's account and mark it in her reflexive journal.

**Approach chosen:** IPA (not Moustakas descriptive) because the research interest is in *meaning-making* — how each individual makes sense of feeling like a fraud — not in abstracting a universal essence across all humans. Idiographic depth is valued.

---

### Step 2: Sampling and Data Collection

**Participants:**
- 5 participants (IPA standard: 3–6 for a journal article; 5 allows cross-case comparison)
- Criteria: currently enrolled STEM PhD Year 2–4 at a Taiwanese research university; self-identify as first-generation (neither parent holds a university degree); have experienced feeling like an impostor at least once in the past 6 months
- Recruited via departmental listservs; pseudonyms assigned

| Pseudonym | Field | Year | Age |
|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Ah-Cheng | Electrical Engineering | Y3 | 27 |
| Mei-Ling | Biochemistry | Y2 | 25 |
| Wei-Hao | Computer Science | Y4 | 29 |
| Hsiao-Yen | Materials Science | Y2 | 26 |
| Bo-Rui | Civil Engineering | Y3 | 28 |

**Interview protocol (open, not structured):**
- Opening: *"Can you describe a recent moment when you felt like you didn't belong here, or like you might be found out?"*
- Follow: *"What was that like for you?"* / *"Can you say more about that feeling?"*
- No mention of "impostor syndrome" until end — avoids theory contamination
- Duration: 60–90 min; recorded and transcribed verbatim

---

### Step 3: IPA Analysis — Illustrated with Ah-Cheng's Case

**Initial reading notations on Ah-Cheng's transcript:**

Ah-Cheng describes a lab meeting where he could not answer a question from his advisor:
> *"Everyone else just... they knew the paper. I hadn't read it carefully enough. I thought — my advisor's face — I saw him think 'why did I accept this one.'"*

Analyst notes in margin:
- *Surveillance of advisor's expression — reading micro-expressions for evidence of fraud exposure*
- *"This one" — objectification, not belonging to the category "real PhD student"*
- *Retrospective attribution: advisors' face becomes proof, not just ambiguous stimulus*

**Emergent themes for Ah-Cheng:**
1. The watching face — experience of being read by authority figures
2. Retrospective evidence-gathering — past neutral events reinterpreted as signs of inadequacy
3. Provisional membership — sense of conditional belonging contingent on next performance
4. Strategic concealment — deliberate management of what to reveal in seminars

**Cross-case pattern analysis (all 5 participants):**

| Theme | Ah-Cheng | Mei-Ling | Wei-Hao | Hsiao-Yen | Bo-Rui |
|-------|----------|----------|---------|-----------|--------|
| Watching authority faces for exposure cues | ✓ strong | ✓ strong | ✓ moderate | ✓ strong | ✓ moderate |
| Retroactive reinterpretation of neutral events as failure signs | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | — | ✓ |
| Provisional/conditional belonging | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| Concealment labor (hiding confusion, performing confidence) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ |
| "Translation fatigue" — code-switching between home and lab world | — | ✓ | — | ✓ | ✓ |

**Divergent case:** Hsiao-Yen did *not* report retroactive reinterpretation — she described the experience as sharply present-tense panic, not a narrative reconstructed afterward. This divergence is preserved and analyzed, not smoothed over.

## Result

```markdown
## Phenomenological Analysis: Impostor Syndrome in First-Generation STEM PhD Students

### Phenomenon
- Definition: The lived experience of perceiving oneself as fraudulent in doctoral STEM training
- Participants: 5 (Ah-Cheng, Mei-Ling, Wei-Hao, Hsiao-Yen, Bo-Rui), 2nd–4th year PhD, first-generation, Taiwan research universities
- Approach: Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis (IPA); Smith, Flowers & Larkin 2022

### Bracketing Statement
- Researcher preconceptions suspended: class-based structural explanation; expected shame and concealment as primary features; personal experience of scholarship "being found out"; centrality of supervisor relationship
- Reflexive journal maintained throughout; insider resonance flagged in analyst memos

### Essential Themes

| Theme | Textural Description (What) | Structural Description (How) |
|-------|---------------------------|----------------------------|
| Watching faces | Continuously scanning authority figures' expressions for signs of exposure | Hypervigilance transforms neutral social interaction into an evidence-gathering exercise |
| Retroactive reinterpretation | Past neutral or positive events reread as early warnings | Time collapses — current inadequacy makes prior acceptance feel like a mistake that will be corrected |
| Provisional belonging | Sense of PhD membership as temporary and performance-contingent | Identity is held as a loan, not an achievement — each success is a deferral, not proof |
| Concealment labor | Deliberate management of questions asked, confusion shown, and opinions voiced | The experience generates ongoing cognitive and social work to sustain the appearance of legitimacy |
| Translation fatigue | Code-switching between home habitus and academic culture produces exhaustion (3/5 participants) | First-generation students carry two selves; the switching cost is experienced as evidence of not fully belonging to either world |

### Essence Statement

> The lived experience of impostor syndrome for these students is not primarily the fear of being caught — it is the exhausting labor of *maintaining a credible self in borrowed territory*. The experience is constituted by a continuous surveillance loop: the student reads authority faces for exposure cues, reinterprets their own history to find the error that will explain their eventual removal, and performs competence not to achieve but to *delay discovery*. Belonging is experienced not as earned but as *lent*. The phenomenon's invariant structure is this temporal instability: there is no moment at which accomplishment converts to identity. Each success extends the loan without retiring it.

### Individual Voices (IPA)

| Participant | Unique Contribution | Illustrative Quote |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Ah-Cheng | Clearest account of face-watching; advisor's expression as real-time fraud detector | *"I watch his face the whole time I'm answering. If he nods slowly, I know I'm wrong."* |
| Mei-Ling | Translation fatigue most elaborated; experience of shame when home dialect slips in lab | *"At home I think in Taiwanese. In lab I think in English. I'm never fully one person."* |
| Wei-Hao | Only participant nearing graduation; reveals the essence does not resolve — it transforms | *"I thought it would go away when I passed quals. It didn't. Now I'm afraid of my own papers."* |
| Hsiao-Yen | Divergent case: present-tense panic, no retroactive narrative; challenges the temporal structure | *"It's not a story. It's just — right now, in this moment, I don't know enough and they will see."* |
| Bo-Rui | Most articulate on concealment labor; quantified the effort | *"I spend maybe one hour before every group meeting deciding what I can safely not know."* |

### Implications
1. The essence — borrowed belonging with no conversion mechanism — challenges interventions that focus on *evidence provision* (showing students their grades, publication counts). Evidence does not retire the loan; it extends it.
2. Hsiao-Yen's divergent case suggests the experience may have two phenomenologically distinct forms: a narrative-temporal structure (most participants) and an acute-present-tense panic structure — these may require different support designs.
3. Translation fatigue as a theme (3/5) was not in the bracketed preconceptions and emerged inductively — it points toward first-generation phenomenology as distinct from general impostor phenomenon research.
```

---

**Methodological note for Dr. Lin's grant proposal:**

The bracketing exercise revealed that her anticipated finding (class-based structural hostility) was *partially* confirmed (translation fatigue, provisional belonging) but the *mechanism* was wrong — participants did not primarily describe structural exclusion; they described a *temporal* experience of identity-as-loan. Had she not bracketed, she would likely have coded toward her class narrative and missed the surveillance and concealment-labor themes. This is the demonstration that epoche, even partial, does methodological work.
