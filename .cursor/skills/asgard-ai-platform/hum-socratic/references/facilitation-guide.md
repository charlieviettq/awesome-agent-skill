# Facilitation Guide: Running a Socratic Session

## Session Architecture

A Socratic session has three phases. The proportions matter:

```
Opening (10-15%)  →  Core Inquiry (70-80%)  →  Synthesis (10-15%)
```

Facilitators who skip the opening jump into questions the thinker isn't
ready for. Facilitators who over-invest in synthesis end up lecturing —
violating the Iron Law.

---

## Phase 1: Opening — Anchor the Position

**Goal:** get the thinker's actual claim on the table, stated in their own words.

Do not begin with a challenge. Begin with invitation.

### Opening Move Patterns

| Situation | Opening move |
|-----------|-------------|
| Thinker has stated a clear belief | "Can you say a bit more about what you mean by that?" |
| Thinker is exploring, no clear position | "Where does your thinking land right now, even tentatively?" |
| Thinker is defensive from the start | "I'm not looking to argue — I want to understand how you got there." |
| Thinker gives a one-word answer | "Say more." (then wait in silence for at least 5 seconds) |

**Avoid:** "That's interesting!" or "Great question!" — evaluative filler
signals you're judging rather than listening. It primes the thinker to
seek your approval rather than pursue their own reasoning.

### Anchoring the Claim

Before moving to Phase 2, you need a clear anchor — a specific, falsifiable
statement. Help the thinker produce one:

Vague claim: *"The education system is broken."*
Anchor procedure:
1. "Broken in what way, specifically?"
2. "For which students, or all students?"
3. "Compared to what alternative?"

Target anchor: *"Public high schools in Taiwan fail to develop critical
thinking skills because the curriculum is entirely exam-driven."*

A good anchor has: a **subject**, a **predicate**, and an implied **standard**.

## Phase 2: Core Inquiry — The Question Ladder

The core inquiry moves through question types in a sequence. The canonical
order from SKILL.md (Clarification → Assumption → Evidence → Perspective →
Implication) is a default, not a rule. Use the **Question Selection Matrix**
to decide which type to reach for next:

### Question Selection Matrix

| If the thinker... | Reach for... | Avoid... |
|-------------------|--------------|----------|
| Uses undefined terms | Clarification | jumping to assumptions |
| Makes a broad generalization | Probing assumptions | asking for evidence yet |
| States their evidence upfront | Probing evidence quality | re-asking for clarification |
| Is confident but one-sided | Exploring perspectives | challenging their evidence first |
| Has examined all sides | Probing implications | more perspective questions |
| Is going in circles | Questioning the question | any object-level question |

### Ladder Logic

Each question should connect visibly to the thinker's last answer. The
facilitator's job is to track what the thinker has committed to and build
the next question on that commitment.

Example ladder (tracking commitments):

> **Thinker:** "Remote work hurts team culture."
>
> **Q1 [Clarification]:** "What do you mean by 'team culture'?"
> → **Committed:** culture = shared norms + trust + spontaneous collaboration
>
> **Q2 [Assumption]:** "You're saying trust requires physical proximity.
> Is that always the case?"
> → **Committed:** (hedges) "Maybe not always, but it's harder remotely."
>
> **Q3 [Evidence]:** "What have you seen that makes remote trust-building
> harder — in your own experience or elsewhere?"
> → **Committed:** cites one team that drifted post-pandemic
>
> **Q4 [Perspective]:** "There are distributed-first companies —
> GitLab, Automattic — with strong cultures. What do you make of that?"
> → **Committed:** "OK, it's possible, but requires more deliberate effort."
>
> **Q5 [Implication]:** "If it requires more deliberate effort, what
> would 'doing it well' look like?"
> → Arrives at nuanced view: remote work doesn't destroy culture, but
> changes the investment required.

**Key:** Q2 uses the thinker's own definition from Q1. Q3 uses their hedge
from Q2. Q4 uses their evidence gap. Q5 uses their revised claim. The
thinker cannot feel ambushed because each question follows from what they
said.

## Silence as a Tool

The most underused facilitation technique is **the pause**.

After asking a question, wait. Research on wait time (Rowe, 1986) shows:
- Most teachers wait less than 1 second before rephrasing or answering
- Extending wait time to 3-5 seconds significantly increases response depth
- Extending to 7-10 seconds is appropriate for complex philosophical questions

### Wait Time Guidelines

| Question complexity | Minimum wait |
|--------------------|-------------|
| Clarification (low) | 3 seconds |
| Assumption/Evidence | 5 seconds |
| Implication/Meta | 7-10 seconds |

If the thinker says "I don't know" within the wait window, do not immediately
rephrase. Say: "Take your time" and wait again. "I don't know" is often the
beginning of thinking, not its end.

**Only rephrase if:** the thinker looks genuinely confused about what the
question is asking (not just struggling to answer it).

## Handling Common Deflections

Thinkers under examination use predictable moves to escape the question.
Recognize them and redirect without becoming adversarial.

### Deflection Patterns and Responses

**The Scope Escape**
> "Well, it depends on the situation."

Response: "Absolutely — so in the situation we're discussing, which is
[restate the specific context], what do you think?"

---

**The Authority Shield**
> "Studies show that..." (without citing specifics)

Response: "What study are you thinking of? And what did it actually find?"
(Gentle but specific. Do not accept appeals to unnamed authority.)

---

**The Pivot**
> "But the real issue is X, not Y."

Response: "That's worth examining too. Before we move there — are you
saying Y is a distraction, or are you conceding Y for now?"
(Forces them to either defend or explicitly abandon their prior position.)

---

**The Emotional Exit**
> "I just feel like it's wrong, okay?"

Response: "I hear that — strong intuitions are worth taking seriously.
Can you try to articulate what's driving that feeling? Sometimes intuitions
are tracking something important that we haven't put into words yet."
(Validates without capitulating. Keeps the inquiry going.)

---

**The Reversal**
> "What do *you* think?"

Response: "I'd love to discuss my view after we've fully explored yours.
Right now I'm more interested in how *you* got to your position."
(Return the question. The facilitator's opinion is irrelevant to the method.)

## Scaffolding for Stuck Thinkers

When a thinker genuinely cannot proceed — not deflecting, but stuck —
scaffolding is appropriate. Scaffolding is NOT providing the answer. It is
reducing the cognitive load of the question.

### Scaffolding Techniques

**Analogical Bridging**
Map the stuck question to a simpler, familiar domain.

> Original question: "What does it mean for an economic policy to be 'fair'?"
> Scaffold: "Imagine two siblings splitting a pizza. What would 'fair' mean
> in that case? Now zoom out — does the same logic apply to a whole economy?"

---

**Hypothesis Offer**
Offer two possible answers and ask which fits better — removing the blank-page problem.

> "Some people would say X; others would say Y. Which resonates more with
> your view, and why?"

Note: both options should be defensible. Do not make one obviously wrong —
that's leading the witness.

---

**Partial Completion**
Start the sentence and have the thinker finish it.

> "So if your claim is right, that means that for someone in [specific
> group], we'd expect to see... ?"

---

**Lower the Abstraction**
Ask for a concrete example before returning to the abstract claim.

> "Before we resolve this at the general level — can you think of one
> specific case where this played out? Real or hypothetical."

---

## Phase 3: Synthesis

Synthesis is where the session closes. The facilitator does NOT summarize
what the thinker should have learned. The facilitator invites the thinker
to summarize.

### Synthesis Move Sequence

1. **Signal the closing:** "We've covered a lot of ground here. I want to
   give you a moment to pull it together."

2. **Invite their summary:** "How would you now describe your position on
   [original claim]? Has it changed, deepened, or stayed the same?"

3. **Reflect the journey:** "Earlier you said [original claim]. Now you're
   saying [evolved claim]. What was the hinge point — the question or
   observation that shifted something?"

4. **Surface remaining uncertainty:** "What are you still unsure about?
   What would you need to know to be more confident?"

5. **Optional: name the question type:** (in teaching contexts) "Notice
   the question about [X] — that was probing an assumption. That type of
   question is often the most productive. Why do you think that is?"

**Iron Law reminder:** Step 3 and 4 are invitations, not fill-ins. If the
thinker's summary is incomplete or partially wrong, your next move is
another question ("Is that the full picture?"), not a correction.

## Group vs. One-on-One Facilitation

Most facilitation techniques apply in both settings, but group dynamics
introduce additional considerations.

### Group-Specific Challenges

**The Dominant Voice**
One participant answers every question, silencing others.

Response: Direct subsequent questions by name. "Mei-ling, we've heard
Chang-wei's view — what's your read?" Alternatively, use written
reflection before open discussion (think-pair-share).

---

**Groupthink Cascade**
The group converges on a view and nobody wants to dissent.

Response: Assign devil's advocate explicitly. "For the next five minutes,
I want someone to argue the opposite. Wei-chen, you're on the opposing
team. Go." This makes dissent a role, not a personal attack.

---

**Cross-Talk Confusion**
Multiple threads open simultaneously.

Response: Use a "parking lot" (visible list of open threads) and explicitly
close one thread before opening another. "We have two questions open. Let's
resolve the first one, then return to what Jia-ling raised."

---

**Public Exposure Risk**
In some cultural contexts, being questioned publicly feels like being
tested or shamed.

Response: Use anonymous written submissions for initial positions (index
cards, polling tools). Discuss the positions rather than the people.
"Someone wrote X — let's examine that." This depersonalizes the inquiry.

## Self-Assessment Checklist for Facilitators

After a session, use this checklist to audit your own facilitation:

- [ ] Did I state the answer at any point? *(Iron Law violation if yes)*
- [ ] Did each question build on what the thinker just said?
- [ ] Did I wait at least 5 seconds after complex questions?
- [ ] Did I use all six question types, or did I over-rely on one?
- [ ] Did the thinker do most of the talking? (Target: thinker >70% of words)
- [ ] When the thinker was stuck, did I scaffold rather than explain?
- [ ] Did the synthesis come from the thinker, not from me?
- [ ] Was the thinker's final position more nuanced than their starting position?

If you answer "no" to more than two of these, identify which phase broke
down and focus there in the next session.

## Common Facilitator Errors (Anti-Patterns)

| Error | What it looks like | Fix |
|-------|--------------------|-----|
| **Funneling** | Questions converge on a predetermined answer | Genuinely follow the thinker's reasoning; be willing to end somewhere surprising |
| **Loaded questions** | "Don't you think that's problematic?" | Neutral framing: "How do you evaluate that?" |
| **Rapid-fire** | Next question before the thinker finishes processing | One question at a time; enforce wait time |
| **Assumption confirmation** | Only asking questions that challenge, never that affirm | Occasionally ask "What makes you confident about that part?" |
| **Ignoring affect** | Continuing when the thinker is visibly frustrated or shut down | Name the dynamic: "This seems to be hitting something important for you. Want to slow down?" |
| **Premature synthesis** | Summarizing the thinker's position for them | "How would you put that?" not "So what you're saying is..." |

## Worked Session Template

For practitioners who want a reusable structure, a 45-minute Socratic
session on a single claim fits the following template:

```
[0:00-0:05]  Opening
  - Invite the thinker to state their position
  - Clarify the anchor claim (subject + predicate + standard)

[0:05-0:15]  Clarification pass
  - 2-3 clarification questions
  - Goal: shared, precise understanding of the claim

[0:15-0:30]  Core inquiry
  - Assumption probe (2 questions)
  - Evidence probe (2 questions)
  - Perspective shift (1-2 questions)

[0:30-0:40]  Implication trace
  - If their position is right, what follows?
  - If their position is wrong, what changes?

[0:40-0:45]  Synthesis
  - Thinker restates their position (evolved or defended)
  - Thinker names one thing that shifted
  - Facilitator notes any remaining open threads for next session
```

Adjust proportions for shorter sessions by compressing the implication
phase. Never cut synthesis entirely — it is where learning consolidates.
