# Meeting Facilitation Techniques

## The Facilitator's Core Responsibility

The facilitator's job is **not** to participate in the discussion — it is to make the discussion produce outcomes. A facilitator who argues their own position has abdicated the role.

Facilitator's only three levers:
1. **Time**: keep the meeting on schedule
2. **Focus**: keep the conversation on-topic
3. **Closure**: force decisions and capture them

---

## Pre-Meeting: Agenda Design

### The Time-Budget Formula

```
Total meeting time = Σ(topic time) + (number of topics × 2 min buffer) + 5 min wrap-up
```

Worked example — 60-minute product review:
```
Topic 1: Q1 metrics review      — 15 min
Topic 2: Roadmap prioritization — 20 min
Topic 3: Launch go/no-go        — 10 min
Buffer (3 topics × 2 min)       —  6 min
Wrap-up (decisions + actions)   —  5 min
Slack                           —  4 min
────────────────────────────────────────
Total                             60 min
```

**Rule**: If the agenda can't fit in the time budget, either cut topics or book a longer meeting — never run over.

### Agenda Item Types

| Type | Time allocation | Decision needed? |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| **FYI** — pure information share | 3–5 min | No |
| **Discussion** — explore options | 10–20 min | Possibly |
| **Decision** — pick one option | 5–15 min | Yes, required |
| **Workshop** — collaborative work | 30–60 min | Yes, required |

Label each agenda item with its type. Example:
```
1. [FYI] Q1 metrics — Alice (5 min)
2. [DISCUSSION] Pricing model options — Bob (15 min)
3. [DECISION] Q2 launch date — all (10 min)
```

FYI items should almost never be in a meeting. Send them async. Reserve meeting time for Discussion and Decision.

## Opening the Meeting (First 3 Minutes)

State all four of these explicitly, every meeting:

1. **Goal**: "By the end of this meeting, we will have decided X and assigned Y."
2. **Role**: "I'm facilitating. Sarah is taking notes. Decisions and action items are what we're capturing."
3. **Time**: "We're ending at 3:00 PM sharp."
4. **Parking lot**: "Off-topic items go on the parking lot — we won't lose them, but we won't chase them now."

If you don't say these out loud, the group will fill in their own assumptions.

## Real-Time Facilitation Moves

### Redirecting Off-Topic Discussion

```
"That's worth discussing — I'm adding it to the parking lot. 
 Let's get back to [current topic]. We have 8 minutes left on it."
```

Never say "that's off topic." Say "I'm capturing that for later." The person feels heard; the group stays on track.

### Breaking a Decision Logjam

When discussion loops without converging, use one of these:

**Technique 1 — Forced choice**
```
"We've identified two options: A and B. I'm going to call for a show of hands. 
 Whoever owns this decision, can you make a call based on what you've heard?"
```

**Technique 2 — Defer with a deadline**
```
"We don't have enough information to decide today. Who will get us that information, 
 and when? Let's make that an action item and decide at our next check-in."
```

**Technique 3 — One-week rule**
```
"If we can't decide in the next 3 minutes, we default to Option A (the status quo) 
 unless someone escalates by [specific date]."
```

Never let a meeting end with "we need to discuss this more" as the only output.

### Handling Dominant Voices

When one person is talking too much:
```
"[Name], thank you — let me hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet. [Other name], 
 what's your read?"
```

When two people are debating and everyone else has tuned out:
```
"I'm going to pause the dialogue here. [Name A], what would you need to see to 
 consider [Name B]'s position? Let's start there."
```

### Checking for Silent Disagreement

Before closing any significant decision, do a quick temperature check:
```
"Before we lock this in — anyone has a strong objection? 
 Not just a preference, but something that would block you from executing on this?"
```

This surfaces blockers without opening a new debate.

## The Last 5 Minutes Protocol

Reserve the final 5 minutes for this exact sequence. Do not skip it.

**Step 1 — Read back decisions (2 min)**
```
"Let me read back what we decided:
 1. We're launching on May 15, not June.
 2. Budget for the campaign is capped at $50K.
 3. Sarah owns the vendor selection.
 Does anyone have a correction?"
```

**Step 2 — Read back action items (2 min)**
```
"Action items:
 1. Sarah — vendor shortlist — by April 12
 2. David — revised budget breakdown — by April 14
 3. Alice — draft launch checklist — by April 10
 Missing anything?"
```

**Step 3 — Confirm next meeting (30 sec)**
```
"Next sync is April 16 at 2 PM. We'll start by reviewing these action items."
```

If you run out of time before this, stop the content discussion early. The last 5 minutes matter more than the last 5 minutes of discussion.

## Decision-Making Formats

Not every decision needs the same process. Match the format to the stakes.

| Stakes | Participants Affected | Best Format |
|--------|----------------------|-------------|
| Low — reversible in days | One team | **Autocratic**: owner decides, announces |
| Medium — reversible in weeks | Cross-functional | **Consultative**: owner decides after input |
| High — reversible in months | Multiple stakeholders | **Consensus**: group agrees, owner ratifies |
| Irreversible | Organization-wide | **Executive**: escalate above meeting |

**Consultative** is the most common and most underused format. The meeting owner collects input, then makes the call — they don't need unanimous agreement.

```
"I've heard the concerns about timeline risk. My decision is we proceed with May 15. 
 If anyone disagrees strongly enough to escalate, please do so by Friday."
```

### RAPID Decision Framework (for ambiguous ownership)

When it's unclear who decides:

| Letter | Role | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| **R** | Recommend | Prepares the proposal |
| **A** | Agree | Must sign off (veto power) |
| **P** | Perform | Executes the decision |
| **I** | Input | Consulted but not binding |
| **D** | Decide | Makes the final call |

Assign one person to **D**. Only one. If you can't assign D, the meeting shouldn't be making this decision.

## Handling Specific Difficult Situations

### Meeting Starts Without Key Decision-Maker

Options in order of preference:
1. **Delay the decision item**: run other agenda items first; reschedule the decision
2. **Delegate**: ask the decision-maker to designate a proxy before the meeting
3. **Advisory mode**: have the discussion, document the recommendation, send it for async approval

Do not make high-stakes decisions without the person who owns them.

### Conflict Between Two Stakeholders

If two people are in direct conflict:
1. Acknowledge both positions explicitly: "I'm hearing [A]'s concern is X, and [B]'s concern is Y."
2. Test for the underlying interest: "What would need to be true for you to be okay with the other option?"
3. If unresolved in 3 minutes: "This needs more data / a side conversation. I'm parking it. Who will drive resolution by [date]?"

Do not try to mediate the substance of a conflict in a group meeting. Separate it out.

### Recurring Meeting With No Preparation

Symptom: people arrive without reading the pre-read, agenda is news to them.

Structural fix:
```
Opening ritual: "Before we start — quick show of hands, who read the pre-read?"

If < 50%: "Let's take 5 minutes to read it now, then we'll discuss."
If 0%:    Reschedule. Send the pre-read again with a hard deadline.
```

If this happens twice, the pre-read format is broken — shorten it or change the format.

## Meeting Type Quick Reference

Different recurring meeting types need different facilitation emphasis.

### Weekly Standup (15 min)

Focus: blockers, not status reports.

```
Per person (2 min max):
- What did I complete that affects others?
- What am I blocked on?
- Do I need a decision from anyone in this room?

Facilitator's job: cut status reports ("I'm working on X") — that's email.
                   Surface blockers and assign resolution on the spot.
```

### Monthly Business Review (60–90 min)

Focus: trend analysis and course correction.

```
Structure:
1. Metrics review — what moved, what didn't (20 min)
2. Root cause on underperforming metrics (20 min)
3. Decision: continue / adjust / stop on each initiative (20 min)
4. Action items from decisions (10 min)
5. Wrap-up (5 min)

Facilitator's job: prevent the meeting from becoming a reporting ceremony.
                   Push "what are we doing differently?" on every red metric.
```

### Quarterly Planning (Half-day)

Focus: alignment on priorities, not execution detail.

```
Danger zone: teams present their roadmaps as faits accomplis.
Facilitator's job: surface cross-team dependencies and conflicts.

Technique — dependency matrix:
- After each team presents, ask: "Who else is affected by this?"
- Build a live matrix: rows = teams, columns = dependencies
- Flag cells where Team A's plan assumes Team B's capacity without B's commitment
```

## What Good Facilitation Looks Like

After the meeting, the note-taker should be able to answer:
- What was decided? (not just discussed)
- Who owns each action item?
- When is each action item due?
- What was explicitly deferred, and when does it get revisited?

If any answer is "unclear," the facilitation failed — not the note-taking.
