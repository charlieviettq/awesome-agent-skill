# Example: B2B SaaS Pitch to Traditional Manufacturer

## Scenario

Jason Wu is CMO of **CloudSync Taiwan**（雲鏈科技）, a B2B SaaS platform for
supply chain visibility. He has been pitching a three-year contract worth NT$18M
to **Longhua Precision**（龍華精密）, a 40-year-old Taiwanese contract manufacturer
with 800 employees and no prior SaaS adoption.

After two failed pitch meetings, Jason asks:

> "I've delivered this deck six times. The data is solid — 23% inventory
> reduction, 99.8% uptime, three Tier-1 reference clients. Why do they keep
> saying 'we'll think about it'? How do I make this pitch actually land?"

He shares his current opening paragraph:

> *"CloudSync is the leading supply chain visibility platform for mid-market
> manufacturers in Taiwan. Our platform integrates with SAP and Oracle to
> provide real-time inventory tracking. We serve 47 clients with an average
> 99.8% uptime SLA. Today I'd like to walk you through our ROI model."*

---

## Analysis

### Step 1 — Rhetorical Situation

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Speaker** | Jason Wu, CMO, CloudSync Taiwan — outsider to Longhua's culture |
| **Audience** | Longhua's founding chairman (68), operations VP (52), IT manager (44) — risk-averse, relationship-driven, skeptical of vendor claims |
| **Purpose** | Close NT$18M three-year contract; overcome "we'll think about it" stall |
| **Context** | Previous ERP implementation in 2019 ran 8 months late and 40% over budget; vendor trust is low |

---

### Step 2 — Appeal Analysis of Current Opening

| Phrase | Appeal | Problem |
|--------|--------|---------|
| "leading supply chain visibility platform" | Ethos | Unverified self-claim — erodes trust with skeptical audience |
| "integrates with SAP and Oracle" | Logos | Relevant but cold; no emotional hook |
| "47 clients, 99.8% uptime" | Logos | Numbers without context; chairman can't calibrate whether 99.8% is good |
| "walk you through our ROI model" | Logos | Announces a lecture, not a conversation |

**Diagnosis**: The opening is **all Logos with borrowed Ethos** (industry jargon as proxy for credibility). It violates the Iron Law — zero Pathos. For a risk-averse traditional manufacturer burned by a past IT project, Pathos must come first to lower the defensive posture; Ethos must be earned through *their* reference points, not generic claims.

**Appeal balance (current)**:
- Ethos: Weak — self-declared; no third-party validation; no shared experience
- Pathos: Absent — no story, no acknowledgment of their pain
- Logos: Over-weighted — ~80% of pitch is data the chairman cannot contextualize

---

### Step 3 — Rhetorical Devices Audit

The current pitch uses no memorable devices. It reads like a spec sheet.

Missing opportunities:
- **Rhetorical question** — invite the audience into the problem before presenting the solution
- **Analogy** — abstract SaaS concepts need a concrete anchor for a 40-year manufacturer
- **Specific detail as Ethos signal** — naming "Taichung warehouse" instead of "your facilities" signals real research

---

### Step 4 — Revised Opening

**Calibration**: Traditional executive audience in Taiwan → Lead with Pathos (shared pain), establish Ethos through peer references in *their* industry, deliver Logos as justification only after emotional buy-in.

> *"Chairman Chen, in 2019 you implemented your current ERP system. I've spoken
> with your operations team — the go-live ran eight months late and came in 40%
> over budget. You were promised transformation and delivered a headache.*
>
> *That story is not unusual. 67% of manufacturing IT projects in Taiwan run over
> schedule (MIC, 2023). So when another vendor walks in with a big promise, I
> understand why the answer is 'we'll think about it.'*
>
> *I'm not here to give you another promise. I'm here because Formosa Plastics
> used our platform for 14 months before they agreed to let us use their name —
> they needed to be sure it worked first. Their procurement lead, David Lin, will
> take your call this afternoon if you'd like to ask him directly.*
>
> *What Formosa found: NT$23M in cash tied up in excess inventory they couldn't
> see. Within six months they freed NT$8.4M of it. Chairman Chen — how much
> inventory is sitting in your Taichung warehouse right now that you're not
> certain you need?"*

---

### Step 5 — Revised Opening Annotated

| Section | Appeal | Technique | Why It Works |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| "In 2019 your ERP went 8 months late..." | Pathos | Specific pain acknowledgment | Shows homework; triggers memory of real frustration |
| "67% of manufacturing IT projects run over (MIC, 2023)" | Logos | Cited industry statistic | Legitimizes their skepticism rather than fighting it |
| "I understand why the answer is 'we'll think about it'" | Pathos + Ethos | Honest disarmament | Disarms defensiveness; differentiates from every other vendor |
| "Formosa Plastics used our platform 14 months before..." | Ethos | Peer reference — same industry, same scale | Taiwan manufacturers trust peer endorsement over vendor claims |
| "David Lin will take your call this afternoon" | Ethos | Verifiable, specific offer | Transforms claim into checkable fact — highest Ethos signal available |
| "NT$23M hiding... NT$8.4M freed in six months" | Logos | Named-company outcome with exact figures | Data grounded in a named peer carries 10× the weight of generic benchmarks |
| "How much inventory in your Taichung warehouse?" | Pathos | Rhetorical question + specific location | "Taichung" shows you know their operation; question makes abstract ROI personal |

## Result

# Rhetorical Analysis: CloudSync Taiwan — Longhua Precision Pitch

## Rhetorical Situation
- **Speaker**: Jason Wu, CMO — technical credibility, low relational capital with Longhua
- **Audience**: Risk-averse traditional manufacturer leadership; burned by prior IT project; relationship-driven decision culture
- **Purpose**: Close NT$18M contract; overcome persistent "we'll think about it" stall
- **Context**: Post-bad-ERP environment; vendor trust deficit; no prior SaaS adoption

## Appeal Analysis

| Section | Appeal | Technique | Effectiveness |
|---------|--------|-----------|--------------|
| "Leading platform… 47 clients" (original) | Ethos | Self-declaration | L — unverified in audience's frame |
| "ROI model walk-through" (original) | Logos | Data presentation | L — lecturing a skeptical, emotionally closed audience |
| "2019 ERP went 8 months late…" (revised) | Pathos | Named shared pain | H — earns trust by naming their wound before selling anything |
| "67% run over schedule — MIC 2023" (revised) | Logos | Cited industry stat | M — contextualizes their experience as structural, not personal failure |
| "I understand why you say 'we'll think about it'" (revised) | Pathos + Ethos | Candid acknowledgment | H — rare vendor vulnerability; immediately differentiates |
| Formosa Plastics + David Lin call offer (revised) | Ethos | Verifiable peer endorsement | H — highest credibility signal for this audience type |
| NT$8.4M freed in six months (revised) | Logos | Named-company outcome data | H — concrete, checkable, industry-relevant |
| "How much in your Taichung warehouse?" (revised) | Pathos | Rhetorical question + specificity | H — personalizes abstract ROI into their physical reality |

## Overall Balance

- **Ethos**: Weak → **Strong** — shifted from self-declared to peer-verified, verifiable, specific
- **Pathos**: Absent → **Strong** — opens with acknowledged pain, closes with personalised question
- **Logos**: Over-weighted → **Calibrated** — data appears after emotional buy-in, grounded in named examples

## Verdict

The original pitch failed because it assumed data persuades before trust is established. For a traditional Taiwanese manufacturer carrying recent IT trauma, the persuasion sequence must be:

**Acknowledge pain → Earn trust through honesty and peer proof → Deliver data in a named-company frame → Close with a question that makes the problem personal.**

No new facts were invented in the revision — only the rhetorical sequence changed. The same 23% inventory reduction and 99.8% uptime numbers appear later in the deck where the audience is now ready to receive them.

**One remaining risk**: Ethos is fragile. If the Formosa Plastics reference call doesn't happen, or goes badly, the entire credibility structure collapses. Never use a reference you haven't fully briefed and confirmed in advance.
